# Audience and Levels

## Purpose of This Document
This document defines who Math Journey is for and how difficulty should be tuned across the product.

It exists to prevent two common failures:
- writing as if the learner is much younger than they are
- writing as if all learners arrive with the same confidence, pace, and goals

## Core Audience Groups

### Audience 1: Curious Kids
Typical range:
- roughly ages 10 to 13

Typical traits:
- high curiosity
- uneven fluency
- responds strongly to story, image, and concrete examples
- may be intimidated by dense symbolic language

What they need:
- clear pacing
- visual anchors
- concrete examples before abstraction
- respect without childishness

### Audience 2: Curious Teenagers
Typical range:
- roughly ages 14 to 18

Typical traits:
- can handle rigor
- often has school math baggage
- may want both meaning and performance
- benefits from seeing why ideas matter beyond school

What they need:
- intellectual seriousness
- efficient explanation
- visible structure
- challenging but fair practice

### Audience 3: Curious Adults Restarting
Typical range:
- adults returning after a long gap

Typical traits:
- motivated by meaning and self-direction
- may carry anxiety or shame from prior math experiences
- often appreciates plain language and conceptual framing

What they need:
- dignity
- clarity without condescension
- reminders that confusion is normal
- visible progress and coherent sequencing

## Shared Audience Assumptions
Across all groups, the product should assume:
- the learner is capable of serious thought
- the learner may have weak confidence
- the learner benefits from historical and real-world meaning
- the learner needs rigorous examples and practice, not only inspiration

## Starting Knowledge Assumption
The default learner may arrive with only:
- addition
- subtraction
- multiplication
- division

The early chapters should not assume prior comfort with:
- formal algebraic notation
- fractions as a system
- negative numbers
- proof
- scientific vocabulary

## Difficulty Framework
Math Journey should separate **chapter importance** from **learner pathway difficulty**.

### 1. Chapter Level
Each chapter should have a structural level:
- `foundational`
- `core`
- `advanced`
- `capstone`

These levels describe where the chapter sits in the curriculum, not who is allowed to read it.

### 2. Learner Pathway
Inside many chapters, the product may support three difficulty lenses:
- `Explorer`
- `Scholar`
- `Physicist`

### Explorer
Best for:
- younger learners
- learners rebuilding confidence
- learners meeting an idea for the first time

Characteristics:
- gentler pacing
- more visual and concrete explanation
- fewer abstraction jumps
- shorter practice sets

### Scholar
Best for:
- the standard product path

Characteristics:
- full formal lesson
- complete worked examples
- standard practice expectations
- regular unlock requirements

### Physicist
Best for:
- learners who want more challenge or more formal depth

Characteristics:
- richer derivations
- harder extensions
- stronger applications to science
- more demanding reasoning tasks

## Tone by Audience
The voice should shift slightly by audience need, but not by becoming three separate personalities.

Always preserve:
- precision
- warmth
- seriousness
- momentum

Adjust mainly through:
- sentence density
- amount of scaffolded explanation
- pace of notation introduction
- problem difficulty

## Reading-Level Guidance
The prose should usually aim for:
- short to medium paragraphs
- concrete wording before specialized wording
- one major idea at a time
- minimal jargon unless the term is being explicitly taught

Historical names, mathematical terms, and scientific ideas may be advanced, but they should be introduced clearly.

## First-Version Level Strategy
For the first version:
- write primarily to the `Scholar` path
- make early chapters friendly enough for `Explorer` learners
- include one or two optional extension prompts for `Physicist` learners

This keeps the system scalable without splitting the content too early.

## Signs the Level Is Wrong
The writing is too easy if:
- it sounds childish
- it avoids precise language for too long
- it hides the formal structure of the idea

The writing is too hard if:
- symbols arrive before the concept is clear
- the learner must infer too many steps
- examples jump past the stated starting knowledge

## Final Rule
Math Journey should feel welcoming to beginners and worthy of ambitious learners at the same time.
