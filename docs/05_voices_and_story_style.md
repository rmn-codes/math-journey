# Voices and Story Style

## Purpose of This Document
This document defines how Math Journey should sound.

It covers two linked but different modes:
- the instructional voice
- the story voice

The goal is to make the platform feel intelligent, cinematic, human, and rigorous without becoming theatrical or childish.

## Core Voice Qualities
The writing should be:
- clear
- precise
- vivid
- historically grounded
- warm without being sugary
- ambitious without becoming pompous

## Instructional Voice
When teaching, the voice should feel like a strong guide:
- calm
- direct
- respectful
- patient
- confident

The instructional voice should:
- explain exactly what matters
- name the idea before overloading the learner
- admit difficulty without dramatizing it
- keep the learner moving

## Story Voice
When opening a chapter or framing a historical moment, the voice can become more atmospheric and image-rich.

The story voice should:
- place the learner inside a real human need or scene
- use sensory detail sparingly and purposefully
- move quickly toward the mathematical idea

The story voice should not:
- become fantasy prose
- invent inner thoughts as if they are known facts
- turn history into legend

## Tone Rules

### Respect the learner
Do not talk down to the reader.

Avoid:
- babyish phrasing
- fake enthusiasm
- empty reassurance

Prefer:
- honest guidance
- earned encouragement
- elegant clarity

### Respect the math
Do not flatten important ideas into vague slogans.

Avoid:
- “it just works”
- “don’t worry about why yet” as a long-term escape
- decorative historical facts with no connection to the concept

Prefer:
- plain but exact definitions
- clear transitions from intuition to formalism
- explanations of what is true and why it matters

### Respect the history
Avoid treating historical figures as isolated geniuses floating outside culture.

Prefer:
- scenes grounded in needs, institutions, trade, astronomy, measurement, engineering, or transmission
- cross-cultural continuity
- careful phrasing when evidence is partial

## Standard Story Structure
Most chapter openings should follow this rhythm:

1. a concrete problem, scene, or moment
2. the tension or need inside that moment
3. the turn toward mathematical structure

This keeps the story structural instead of ornamental.

## Length Guidance
- story hooks should usually be short
- early-chapter historical passages should usually be one to three paragraphs
- longer historical digressions should be side content, not the spine of the lesson

## Sentence-Level Style
Prefer:
- active voice
- concrete nouns
- strong verbs
- short to medium sentences with occasional longer cadence lines

Use rhetorical questions sparingly. One well-placed question can create curiosity; many of them make the lesson feel performative.

## Metaphor Rules
Metaphors are useful when they clarify structure.

They should:
- illuminate the concept
- remain mathematically safe
- be retired once the formal idea is established

They should not:
- replace the formal idea
- create false intuitions that later have to be unlearned

## Handling Difficulty
When a topic is hard, the voice should say so plainly.

Good pattern:
- acknowledge the challenge
- clarify the next step
- show that mastery comes in stages

Bad pattern:
- pretending the difficulty is trivial
- over-comforting instead of teaching

## Quotes and Dialogue
- use quotations only when they are sourced and genuinely useful
- do not invent dialogue
- do not fabricate dramatic last words, realizations, or personal emotions

If a quote cannot be verified, paraphrase the idea instead.

## Chapter Voice Balance
A good chapter usually feels like:
- 10 percent story atmosphere
- 70 percent precise teaching
- 20 percent reflective or motivating connective tissue

This is an intuition, not a formula, but it helps maintain balance.

## Example Voice Targets

### Good
"A merchant does not need philosophy to notice that five jars of oil must be tracked somehow. But once trade grows, memory is no longer enough. Quantity has to become visible."

### Not Good
"Imagine a super ancient merchant freaking out because math had not been invented yet."

## Final Rule
The writing should make the learner feel they are entering a serious, beautiful human project and that they are capable of following it.
