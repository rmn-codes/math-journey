# Historical Sourcing Rules

## Purpose of This Document
This document defines how historical claims should be handled in Math Journey.

History is central to the product, so historical care is not optional. The platform should be vivid without becoming sloppy.

## Core Rule
Do not present uncertain or invented historical claims as clean fact.

## Source Hierarchy

### 1. Primary sources
Use when available and relevant:
- historical texts
- translations of original works
- inscriptions
- archival materials

### 2. Strong secondary sources
Use for interpretation and synthesis:
- books by historians of mathematics or science
- academic papers
- museum or university resources with named scholarship

### 3. Tertiary or summary sources
Use with caution:
- encyclopedia entries
- general surveys
- classroom summaries

They may help orientation, but they should not be the only basis for a strong historical claim.

## Required Historical Standards

### No invented quotes
If a quotation is included, it must be sourced.

### No fabricated scenes presented as fact
Narrative openings may reconstruct a plausible setting, but they must not invent fake certainty about personal thoughts, dialogue, or events.

### No hero myths
Avoid the pattern of portraying one isolated genius as if an entire field appeared from nowhere.

### Cross-cultural honesty
When an idea has roots across multiple traditions, say so.

## Acceptable Story Framing
It is acceptable to write:
- a historically grounded scene based on documented practices
- a careful paraphrase of what a community or profession needed
- a condensed account of transmission across cultures

It is not acceptable to write:
- false dramatic monologues
- invented emotional turning points attributed to real people
- clean certainty where historians disagree

## Handling Uncertainty
Use phrases such as:
- "evidence suggests"
- "scholars debate"
- "one important transmission route was"
- "this tradition is often associated with"

Do not use hedging so often that the prose collapses, but do use it when truth requires it.

## Chapter-Level Requirements
Historical chapters should usually include:
- a brief note on source confidence
- named cultures, institutions, or periods rather than vague civilizational labels alone
- clear distinction between documented fact and interpretive framing

## Global Representation Rules
- include non-European mathematical traditions as structural contributors, not token side mentions
- avoid implying that mathematical progress belonged to only one civilization
- acknowledge transmission, translation, and adaptation

## Compression Rules
Because the product is educational, history will often be compressed.

Compression is acceptable if it:
- preserves the main truth
- does not reverse causality
- does not erase major contributors or cultures

## When to Add Source Notes
Add explicit source notes when:
- a chapter centers a historical claim or figure
- a quotation is used
- a date, priority claim, or cultural attribution is important to the lesson
- the narrative could otherwise sound more certain than the evidence warrants

## First-Version Standard
For the first version:
- avoid brittle claims that require specialist certainty
- prefer broadly supported historical framing
- use source notes for the sample chapter and future history-heavy chapters

## Final Rule
Math Journey should make history feel alive by being more careful, not less.
