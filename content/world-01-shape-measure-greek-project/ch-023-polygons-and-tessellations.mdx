# Polygons and Tessellations

## Story Hook
Imagine a tile maker in 9th-century Cordoba, sitting in the workshops of the Great Mosque. She has a batch of identical clay tiles—all the same shape, all the same size. Her task is to cover a floor with no gaps and no overlaps. She can use triangles, squares, or hexagons, but when she tries pentagons, something strange happens: there is always a gap. Always. No matter how she arranges them, the floor stays unfinished.

She doesn't know algebra or formal proof. But she knows, from trying, that not all shapes fit together. Some shapes are democratic: they play well with others. Some shapes are rebels. Understanding why is the work of this chapter.

## Why It Matters
Tessellations are not just pretty patterns. They matter because:
- architects and builders need to know which tiles fit together without waste
- honeycombs, snake skin, and rock formations all show tessellations in nature
- Islamic geometric art relied on deep understanding of which shapes work
- later mathematics—crystallography, coding theory, even quantum mechanics—depends on understanding how shapes pack together
- interior angles and angle sums connect to fundamental truths about flat space itself

When you understand polygons and tessellations, you see order hidden in everyday walls, pavements, and mosaics.

## Historical Context
The ancient Greeks classified shapes by the number of sides and studied their angles. Euclid, in his *Elements*, discussed regular polygons, though not with the explicit angle-sum formula we use today. That formula became clearer with later mathematicians.

But the deepest exploration of tessellations came during the Islamic Golden Age. From the 8th to 15th centuries, Muslim artisans and mathematicians created geometric patterns of extraordinary complexity. Palaces like the Alhambra in Granada, Spain, use intricate tessellations—not randomly, but based on deep geometric knowledge. Islamic tradition did not permit figurative images in religious spaces, so geometry and tessellation became a language of beauty and mathematical thought.

These artisans knew, through experimentation and reasoning, which shapes could tile a plane and which could not. Whether their understanding was written down algebraically or held as craft knowledge remains debated, but the patterns themselves are evidence of systematic geometric thinking.

## Core Concept
A **polygon** is a closed shape made of straight line segments. A **regular polygon** is a polygon where all sides are equal and all interior angles are equal.

A **tessellation** is a pattern of shapes that covers a flat surface completely, with no gaps and no overlaps.

The key question is: which regular polygons can tessellate the plane?

The answer depends on the interior angles. If the interior angles of a shape can be combined to make 360° exactly (a full turn around a point), then that shape can tessellate. If not, there will always be gaps.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Polygon Types

**Polygon**
A closed, flat shape with straight sides. Examples: triangle (3 sides), quadrilateral (4 sides), pentagon (5 sides), hexagon (6 sides).

**Regular Polygon**
A polygon with all sides of equal length and all interior angles of equal measure.

**Irregular Polygon**
A polygon where sides or angles are not all equal.

### Interior and Exterior Angles

**Interior Angle**
An angle inside a polygon, formed by two adjacent sides.

**Exterior Angle**
An angle formed between one side of a polygon and the extension of an adjacent side. The exterior angle and its neighboring interior angle always sum to 180°.

**Diagonal**
A line segment connecting two non-adjacent vertices of a polygon.

### The Angle Sum Formula

For any polygon with *n* sides, the sum of all interior angles is:

**(n − 2) × 180°**

This formula works because any polygon can be divided into triangles, and each triangle has angles summing to 180°.

For a triangle (n = 3): (3 − 2) × 180° = 180°
For a quadrilateral (n = 4): (4 − 2) × 180° = 360°
For a pentagon (n = 5): (5 − 2) × 180° = 540°
For a hexagon (n = 6): (6 − 2) × 180° = 720°

### Interior Angle of a Regular Polygon

If a regular polygon has *n* sides, each interior angle measures:

**Interior angle = [(n − 2) × 180°] ÷ n**

For a regular triangle: (180°) ÷ 3 = 60°
For a regular quadrilateral (square): (360°) ÷ 4 = 90°
For a regular pentagon: (540°) ÷ 5 = 108°
For a regular hexagon: (720°) ÷ 6 = 120°

### Exterior Angles

The exterior angles of any polygon always sum to 360°, regardless of the number of sides. For a regular polygon with *n* sides:

**Exterior angle = 360° ÷ n**

### Which Regular Polygons Tessellate?

A regular polygon can tessellate the plane if its interior angle divides evenly into 360°.

- **Equilateral triangle**: interior angle = 60°. Since 360° ÷ 60° = 6, six triangles fit perfectly around one point. ✓ Tessellates
- **Square**: interior angle = 90°. Since 360° ÷ 90° = 4, four squares fit perfectly around one point. ✓ Tessellates
- **Regular pentagon**: interior angle = 108°. Since 360° ÷ 108° = 3.33..., pentagons cannot fit exactly around one point. ✗ Does not tessellate
- **Regular hexagon**: interior angle = 120°. Since 360° ÷ 120° = 3, three hexagons fit perfectly around one point. ✓ Tessellates
- **Regular heptagons and beyond**: As *n* increases, the interior angle grows and approaches 180°. None of these produce whole-number divisions of 360°. ✗ Do not tessellate

**Only three regular polygons tessellate the plane: equilateral triangles, squares, and regular hexagons.**

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Find the Interior Angle Sum of a Hexagon

A regular hexagon tile is to be used in a floor. Find the sum of all interior angles in one hexagon.

**Solution**

Use the formula: (n − 2) × 180°

For a hexagon, n = 6.

(6 − 2) × 180° = 4 × 180° = **720°**

The sum of all six interior angles is 720°.

### Example 2: Find Each Interior Angle of a Regular Octagon

A regular octagon has 8 equal sides and 8 equal angles. Find the measure of each interior angle.

**Solution**

First, find the sum of all interior angles.

(n − 2) × 180° = (8 − 2) × 180° = 6 × 180° = 1080°

Now divide by the number of angles:

1080° ÷ 8 = **135°**

Each interior angle of a regular octagon measures 135°.

Can a regular octagon tessellate alone? Check: 360° ÷ 135° = 2.67..., which is not a whole number. So regular octagons alone do not tessellate. (However, octagons and squares together *can* tessellate, a pattern called the truncated square tiling.)

### Example 3: Verify Why Pentagons Don't Tessellate

Show why regular pentagons cannot tessellate by themselves.

**Solution**

Interior angle of a regular pentagon: [(5 − 2) × 180°] ÷ 5 = 540° ÷ 5 = 108°

Around a single point, place pentagons edge to edge:
- 1 pentagon: 108°
- 2 pentagons: 216°
- 3 pentagons: 324°
- 4 pentagons: 432° (too much!)

Three pentagons give 324°, leaving a gap of 36°. Four pentagons give 432°, which overlaps by 72°. There is no way to fit an exact whole number of pentagons around a point.

**Pentagons cannot tessellate alone.**

## Application
Tessellations appear everywhere in human design and nature:

**In Architecture and Art**
The Alhambra in Granada uses tessellations in tilework, mosaics, and decorative patterns. Honeycomb ceilings mimic the hexagonal structure of bee nests. Modern buildings use tessellations in flooring and wall panels.

**In Nature**
Honeycomb cells are hexagons—a shape that tessellates and uses the minimum material for maximum storage. Turtle shells, dragonfly wings, and certain rock formations show tessellation patterns. Even at the atomic level, some crystals arrange in tessellating patterns.

**In Coding and Computer Graphics**
Tessellation is used to render curved surfaces on computers by breaking them into many small polygons. Video games and 3D modeling rely on tessellation algorithms.

The tile maker of Cordoba may not have known these modern applications, but she understood something fundamental: the geometry of fit. When you understand which shapes tessellate and why, you see order built into the fabric of flat space itself.

- Tessellations appear in art, nature, and architecture.
