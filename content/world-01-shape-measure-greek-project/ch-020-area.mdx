# Area

## Story Hook
Imagine you are a farmer in ancient Egypt, and the Nile River has just flooded your fields. The water recedes, leaving mud and silt, but now the boundary of your land is unclear. The government must assess taxes. How much of your field is actually yours?

You could not simply walk the border and count steps. The land inside does not come in discrete packages like sheep or jars of oil. You need a way to measure the _space inside_ the boundary.

That is what area does. It answers a question that counting alone cannot: how much two-dimensional space is occupied?

## Why It Matters
Area matters because humans need to:
- measure land for farming, building, and taxation
- calculate how much material is needed to cover a surface
- design fields, rooms, and structures efficiently
- compare sizes of different regions
- solve practical problems in agriculture, construction, and resource planning

Without area, you cannot fairly tax farmland, design a house, or know how much paint to buy for a wall. Area is the language of two-dimensional space, just as length is the language of one dimension.

## Historical Context
The earliest systematic study of area appeared in Egypt and Mesopotamia around 3000 BCE. Egyptian surveyors used rope and geometry to measure land after Nile floods, as recorded in the Rhind Papyrus (c. 1650 BCE). The papyrus contains specific area problems: rectangles, triangles, and even a method for approximating the area of a circle.

Babylonian mathematicians also solved area problems on clay tablets. They knew how to calculate areas of standard shapes and used area formulas in land surveys and construction.

The Greeks, particularly Archimedes (c. 287–212 BCE), refined the theory of area. Archimedes proved that the area of a circle could be approximated by dividing it into many thin triangles—a technique that foreshadowed calculus by nearly 2000 years. For the purposes of this chapter, we focus on the practical formulas and the reasoning behind them.

## Core Concept
**Area** is the measure of two-dimensional space inside a shape.

If length measures one dimension (how long something is), area measures two dimensions at once: how long _and_ how wide.

The basic unit of area is the **square unit**: a square with sides of length 1. So:
- 1 cm² is a square with sides of 1 cm
- 1 m² is a square with sides of 1 m
- 1 inch² is a square with sides of 1 inch

When we say "the area is 12 square meters," we mean the space inside the shape could be covered by exactly 12 squares, each 1 meter on a side.

Why squares? Because squares tile the plane perfectly—they leave no gaps and do not overlap. Any other shape would be harder to count.

The most intuitive area formula is for a rectangle:
**A = length × width**

This formula works because a rectangle can be divided into rows and columns of unit squares. If a rectangle is 5 units long and 3 units wide, you can fit exactly 5 × 3 = 15 unit squares inside it.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Key Terms

**Area**
The measure of two-dimensional space inside a closed shape, typically expressed in square units.

**Square unit**
A square with sides of length 1, used as the standard unit for measuring area.

**Base**
Any side of a shape that we use as a reference for measuring height, often the bottom side.

**Height**
The perpendicular distance from the base to the opposite side (not necessarily the longest side).

### Rectangle Area

The area of a rectangle is the product of its length and width:

**A = l × w**

Or, using base and height:

**A = b × h**

This works because you can arrange unit squares in rows and columns to fill the rectangle exactly.

### Triangle Area

A triangle has exactly half the area of a rectangle with the same base and height:

**A = ½ × b × h**

**Why?** If you take a rectangle and draw a diagonal line, you create two triangles of equal size. Each triangle is half the rectangle. Therefore, if a rectangle with base _b_ and height _h_ has area _bh_, then a triangle with the same base and height has area ½_bh_.

### Parallelogram Area

A parallelogram is a four-sided shape where opposite sides are parallel. Unlike a rectangle, its corners are not right angles. However, its area formula is the same as a rectangle's:

**A = b × h**

**Why?** A parallelogram can be cut and rearranged into a rectangle. If you cut a triangle from one end and slide it to the other end, the base stays the same, the height stays the same, but now the shape is a rectangle. Since the rearrangement does not change the area, the parallelogram has the same area as that rectangle.

**Important:** height is the perpendicular distance between parallel sides, not the length of a slanted side.

### Trapezoid Area

A trapezoid has two parallel sides (called bases) of different lengths. The area formula uses the average of the two bases:

**A = ½ × (b₁ + b₂) × h**

where _b₁_ and _b₂_ are the lengths of the two parallel sides, and _h_ is the perpendicular height between them.

**Why?** A trapezoid can be divided into two triangles or viewed as a rectangle plus a triangle. The formula reflects that the trapezoid is "between" the two rectangles you could make from each base alone.

### Composite Shapes

A composite shape is made by joining two or more simpler shapes. To find its area, divide it into rectangles, triangles, or other known shapes, find the area of each, and add them together.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Area of a Rectangle
A farmer's field is 40 meters long and 25 meters wide. What is the area?

**Solution**

Using the rectangle formula:
A = l × w
A = 40 × 25
A = 1000 m²

The field covers 1000 square meters.

### Example 2: Area of a Triangle
A triangular sail has a base of 6 meters and a height of 8 meters. What is the area of the sail?

**Solution**

Using the triangle formula:
A = ½ × b × h
A = ½ × 6 × 8
A = ½ × 48
A = 24 m²

The sail covers 24 square meters of canvas.

### Example 3: Area of a Parallelogram
A parallelogram has a base of 10 cm and a perpendicular height of 7 cm. One of its slanted sides is 9 cm. What is the area?

**Solution**

The slanted side length (9 cm) is not used in the area formula. Only the base and the perpendicular height matter:

A = b × h
A = 10 × 7
A = 70 cm²

**Key point:** Do not confuse the slanted side with the height. The height is always measured perpendicular to the base.

### Example 4: Area of a Trapezoid
A trapezoid has parallel sides of 5 m and 9 m, with a perpendicular height of 4 m. What is the area?

**Solution**

Using the trapezoid formula:
A = ½ × (b₁ + b₂) × h
A = ½ × (5 + 9) × 4
A = ½ × 14 × 4
A = ½ × 56
A = 28 m²

### Example 5: Composite Shape
An L-shaped field can be divided into two rectangles. The top rectangle is 20 m wide and 10 m tall. The bottom rectangle is 15 m wide and 8 m tall. What is the total area?

**Solution**

Find the area of each rectangle:

Top rectangle:
A₁ = 20 × 10 = 200 m²

Bottom rectangle:
A₂ = 15 × 8 = 120 m²

Total area:
A = A₁ + A₂ = 200 + 120 = 320 m²

## Application
Area appears in countless real-world contexts:
- **Agriculture:** Farmers calculate field area to plan irrigation, estimate yield, and assess property tax.
- **Construction:** Builders need to know wall area to estimate paint, tile, or siding required.
- **Flooring:** When installing carpet or wood flooring, the room area determines the amount of material needed.
- **Graphics and design:** Digital images are measured in square pixels. A 1920 × 1080 screen has an area of 2,073,600 pixels.
- **Geography:** Countries, cities, and oceans are compared by area. The United States has an area of about 3.8 million square miles.

Even in modern contexts, area remains essential to planning, resource allocation, and fair measurement.

## Recap
- **Area** measures two-dimensional space inside a shape.
- **Square units** (cm², m², etc.) are the standard for expressing area.
- The **rectangle area formula** is A = l × w, the foundation for all other formulas.
- A **triangle's area** is half the area of a rectangle with the same base and height: A = ½ × b × h.
- A **parallelogram's area** equals base times height: A = b × h, because it can be rearranged into a rectangle.
- A **trapezoid's area** uses the average of its two bases: A = ½ × (b₁ + b₂) × h.
- **Composite shapes** are solved by dividing them into simpler shapes and adding their areas.
