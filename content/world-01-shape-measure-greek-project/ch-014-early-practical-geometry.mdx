# Early Practical Geometry

## Story Hook
A builder stands on a piece of land where a temple must be constructed. The foundation must be a perfect square, with corners that meet at right angles. If the corners are not square, the walls will lean, crack, and eventually collapse.

But how do you make a right angle without a modern protractor? You cannot see a right angle—it is not marked in the earth.

The solution came from a surprising place: rope. Tie a rope into a loop with 12 equally spaced knots. Create a triangle using the knots as vertices. If the sides are in the proportions 3, 4, and 5, the corner opposite the longest side will be exactly square. A builder could repeat this method thousands of times, creating perfect corners across Egypt and Mesopotamia, without ever understanding *why* it worked.

This is geometry as craft: learned through practice, passed hand to hand, trusted because it delivered results.

## Why It Matters
Practical geometry matters because it:
- allowed ancient peoples to build structures that lasted millennia
- created empirical knowledge about shapes and angles before formal mathematics
- shows that mathematics often comes *from* solving real problems, not from pure thought
- introduces ideas like the right angle and ratio that will later be proven rigorously
- reveals that you can use a mathematical relationship reliably even when you do not understand its deeper reason

This chapter honors a truth: you do not need a proof to use mathematics well. But proofs eventually let you see *why* the method works and how to invent new methods.

## Historical Context
Archaeological and cuneiform evidence shows that Mesopotamian builders and surveyors around 2000-1500 BCE knew and used specific number triples that create right angles. The clay tablet known as Plimpton 322, dated to around 1800 BCE, contains a systematic list of such triples: (3, 4, 5), (5, 12, 13), (8, 15, 17), and others. Whether the Babylonians understood these as Pythagorean triples or simply as practical construction tools remains debated, but they clearly knew their power.

Egyptian builders and pyramid architects also relied on right-angle construction, though surviving records are less explicit about the methods. Rope stretching remained a common technique, and evidence suggests that by the time Euclid wrote his Elements in Alexandria (around 300 BCE), the knowledge of these relationships was well established in both civilizations.

Historians disagree on some details. Did the Babylonians *discover* these triples through systematic trial, or through pure geometry? Did they see the relationship as mathematical, or simply as craft knowledge? The fragmentary nature of ancient sources makes certainty hard. But one fact is clear: these civilizations had *effective* geometry, even if they had not yet created formal proofs.

## Core Concept
A **right angle** is a corner where two lines or surfaces meet at exactly 90 degrees. It is the most fundamental angle in practical geometry because right angles allow you to build square foundations, align walls, divide land fairly, and construct objects that fit together.

The key insight of ancient builders was this: *certain number triples always create right angles*.

If you have three sticks or ropes of lengths 3, 4, and 5 (in the same units), and you arrange them into a triangle, the corner opposite the side of length 5 will be a right angle.

Later, in ancient Greece, this relationship was named after Pythagoras and eventually stated as a theorem:

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

where $c$ is the longest side of a right triangle. But long before anyone proved this, builders *used* it.

A **ratio** is a comparison between two quantities.

If a field is twice as long as it is wide, the ratio of length to width is 2 to 1, written as $2:1$.

**Slope** (or **gradient**) describes how steep something is. If a ramp rises 3 cubits for every 4 cubits of horizontal distance, the slope is $3:4$ or the rise-over-run is $\frac{3}{4}$.

Egyptian architects used slope ratios to design the faces of pyramids. The *seked* was the Egyptian measure of slope: the amount of horizontal distance per unit of vertical rise.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Key Terms

**Right angle**
An angle of exactly 90 degrees, formed when two straight lines or surfaces meet perpendicularly.

**Pythagorean triple**
A set of three whole numbers $(a, b, c)$ such that $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, meaning they can form the sides of a right triangle.

**Ratio**
A comparison between two quantities, often written as $a:b$ or $\frac{a}{b}$.

**Slope (or gradient)**
The steepness of an incline, usually expressed as rise over run: $\frac{\text{vertical rise}}{\text{horizontal distance}}$.

**Seked**
The Egyptian measure of slope for pyramids and ramps, typically expressed as horizontal distance per unit of vertical rise.

### The Three-Four-Five Rule

The simplest Pythagorean triple is (3, 4, 5).

If you arrange ropes or sticks in these proportions:
- one side of length 3
- another side of length 4
- a third side of length 5

the angle where the sides of length 3 and 4 meet will be exactly 90 degrees.

Check: $3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5^2$ ✓

**Why this works for any scale:**

The triple (3, 4, 5) is just one example. You can scale it:
- (6, 8, 10): multiply by 2
- (9, 12, 15): multiply by 3
- (15, 20, 25): multiply by 5

Each of these will also create a right angle.

### Other Common Pythagorean Triples

Ancient builders and mathematicians knew many triples:
- (5, 12, 13): $5^2 + 12^2 = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13^2$
- (8, 15, 17): $8^2 + 15^2 = 64 + 225 = 289 = 17^2$
- (7, 24, 25): $7^2 + 24^2 = 49 + 576 = 625 = 25^2$

Each triple appears on Plimpton 322, suggesting that Mesopotamian mathematicians were systematically generating and recording them—though whether they understood the underlying pattern is unknown.

### Using Ratios in Construction

When a builder needs to scale a design, ratios maintain the shape.

**Example:** A pyramid is designed with a slope ratio of 4:3 (rise to run). If the pyramid is to be 60 cubits high, and the slope ratio is 4:3, then for every 4 cubits of height, there are 3 cubits of horizontal distance.

Height = 60 cubits
Horizontal distance = $60 \times \frac{3}{4} = 45$ cubits

So the base of the pyramid (from center to edge) is 45 cubits.

### The Seked in Egyptian Architecture

The seked was expressed in "hands and fingers" and represented the horizontal distance (in units called "hands") per 1 cubit of vertical rise.

A common seked was 5 hands and 1 finger per cubit, which is roughly a slope of 1:1.25 or 4:5.

If you know the seked and the height, you can find the horizontal distance. If you know the height and horizontal distance, you can compute the seked.

This allowed Egyptian architects to design pyramid faces with precision and consistency across generations.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Constructing a Right Angle Using the 3-4-5 Rule

A builder needs to lay out a rectangular foundation for a storehouse. The desired dimensions are 12 cubits by 9 cubits.

First, she creates a right angle using ropes. She ties knots in a rope to mark lengths of 3, 4, and 5 cubits. She stakes the rope into a triangle and verifies that the corner opposite the longest side is square.

Once the right angle is established, she extends the 3-cubit side to 9 cubits and the 4-cubit side to 12 cubits.

What is the distance across the diagonal?

**Solution**

If the sides are 9 and 12 cubits, the diagonal $c$ satisfies:
$$c^2 = 9^2 + 12^2 = 81 + 144 = 225$$
$$c = 15 \text{ cubits}$$

Notice that (9, 12, 15) is the (3, 4, 5) triple scaled by 3. The diagonal is 15 cubits.

This diagonal check allows the builder to verify the rectangle is truly square at all four corners. If the diagonal is not exactly 15 cubits, something is off.

### Example 2: Finding a Pythagorean Triple

A surveyor measures a triangular field and finds that two sides are 5 and 12 cubits, and they meet at a right angle. What must the hypotenuse (longest side) be?

**Solution**

Use the relationship for right triangles:
$$c^2 = 5^2 + 12^2 = 25 + 144 = 169$$
$$c = 13 \text{ cubits}$$

The triple (5, 12, 13) is another Pythagorean triple. The hypotenuse is 13 cubits.

### Example 3: Using Slope Ratio for a Ramp

A builder is constructing a ramp to a temple platform. The platform is 8 cubits high. The desired slope ratio is 1:4 (rise to run), meaning 1 cubit of rise for every 4 cubits of horizontal distance.

How long must the ramp be horizontally?

**Solution**

If the rise is 8 cubits and the ratio is 1:4, then:
$$\text{horizontal distance} = 8 \times 4 = 32 \text{ cubits}$$

The ramp extends 32 cubits horizontally.

To find the length of the ramp itself (the hypotenuse):
$$\text{ramp length}^2 = 8^2 + 32^2 = 64 + 1024 = 1088$$
$$\text{ramp length} \approx 33 \text{ cubits}$$

(More precisely, $\sqrt{1088} \approx 32.98$ cubits, but ancient builders would round to 33.)

## Application
The practical geometry in this chapter is still used today.

Construction workers use the 3-4-5 rule (or multiples like 6-8-10) to square corners when building houses and buildings. Surveyors use slope ratios when designing roads, ramps, and terraces. Architects use proportional ratios when scaling designs. Any time you see a ramp with a specific steepness or a building with straight, square corners, you are looking at practical geometry descended directly from ancient Mesopotamian and Egyptian methods.

Modern tools (lasers, GPS, calculators) have made the work faster and more precise, but the mathematical reasoning is unchanged. A builder still needs to know that two specific measurements will create a right angle, and why slope matters for safety and function.

## Recap
- A **right angle** is a 90-degree corner, essential for building and surveying.
- **Pythagorean triples** are sets of whole numbers (like 3, 4, 5) that create right angles when arranged as triangle sides.
- Ancient builders used triples as empirical tools long before proofs existed.
- A **ratio** compares two quantities and allows designs to be scaled while maintaining shape.
- **Slope (or seked)** measures steepness as rise over run, critical for ramps, pyramids, and canals.
- Practical geometry was developed out of necessity: building, surveying, and construction cannot rely on approximation.
