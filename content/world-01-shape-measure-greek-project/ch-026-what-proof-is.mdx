# What Proof Is

## Story Hook
A farmer notices that his apple trees tend to produce more fruit in years when it rains heavily in spring. He observes this pattern for 10 years. It happens all 10 times. Is he certain that rain in spring always causes a larger apple harvest?

Most people would say: "Probably, but not definitely."

A mathematician sees 10 examples where a statement is true and asks a different question: "Is there a logical argument that shows it *must* be true in every case, without any exception?"

This is the difference between pattern and proof. A pattern is what you notice. A proof is a logical argument that shows why something cannot be otherwise.

A person in ancient Greece, whose name may have been Hippasus, discovered something that shocked the mathematical world: the square root of 2 cannot be written as a fraction. Not because no one had found such a fraction. But because it is *logically impossible*. A proof showed this. The proof did not check a million numbers. It showed that *any* fraction would lead to a logical contradiction.

This chapter is about that kind of certainty. It is the capstone of World 01.

## Why It Matters
Proof matters because it is how we know something is true not just today, but forever.

This matters because:
- a bridge built on gut feeling or luck will eventually collapse; it must be built on principles that are logically certain
- science makes predictions by reasoning from proven principles
- in a world of disagreement, proof is a way to resolve disputes with logic instead of opinion
- proof separated mathematics from measurement and guesswork
- proof is the only way to know that no counterexample will ever appear

When you understand proof, you understand the foundation of all mathematical knowledge.

## Historical Context
The ancient Greeks elevated proof to the heart of mathematics. Euclid's *Elements*, written around 300 BCE, is a towering example: it begins with simple statements (axioms) and builds more complex truths through logical argument. Every claim is either an axiom or a proof.

But one discovery shook this beautiful edifice: the Pythagoreans had believed all numbers were rational (could be written as fractions). Then came the proof that √2 is irrational. The legend, which may or may not be true, says that Hippasus of Metapontum discovered this proof. Some accounts claim the Pythagoreans were so disturbed by this discovery that they made him swear to secrecy. Others say they drowned him.

The proof itself survived, and it is elegant. It uses a logical technique called *reductio ad absurdum*—proof by contradiction. You assume the opposite of what you want to prove, and show that this assumption leads to an impossible conclusion. Since the assumption is contradictory, it must be false.

This method became central to mathematics. It is not the only way to prove something, but it is a powerful one. And the proof that √2 is irrational is one of the most beautiful proofs in all of mathematics.

## Core Concept
A **proof** is a logical argument that shows why something must be true.

A **theorem** is a statement that has been proven.

A **conjecture** is a statement that might be true but has not been proven.

A **counterexample** is a single example that shows a statement is false.

The deepest difference: checking 1 million examples is not a proof. One single counterexample can disprove a claim.

A proof works for all cases, forever. It uses logic to show that something cannot be otherwise. Once you have a proof, you are certain. No future discovery will overturn it.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Proof vs. Evidence

**Evidence** (Checking many examples)
If I check 10 cases and a statement is true in all of them, that is evidence the statement might be true in general. But it is not proof. The 11th case might be different.

**Proof** (Logical argument)
A step-by-step logical argument that shows a statement must be true in *all* cases. Once a proof exists, no exception is possible.

### Direct Proof

A **direct proof** assumes the starting conditions and uses logical steps to reach the conclusion.

Example structure:
- Assume: X is true.
- Therefore: Y is true (following logically from X).
- Therefore: Z is true (following logically from Y).
- Conclusion: Z must be true.

### Proof by Contradiction (Reductio ad Absurdum)

A **proof by contradiction** assumes the opposite of what you want to prove, then shows this assumption leads to a logical impossibility.

Example structure:
- We want to prove: Statement A is true.
- Instead, assume: Statement A is false.
- Following the logic of this assumption, we arrive at: Something impossible (like 2 = 3, or a number is both even and odd).
- Since the assumption leads to impossibility, the assumption must be wrong.
- Therefore: Statement A must be true.

### Counterexample

A **counterexample** is a single example that contradicts a claim.

If someone claims "All odd numbers are prime," one counterexample—9, which is odd but not prime—disproves the claim entirely.

### Conjecture vs. Theorem

**Conjecture**
A statement believed to be true but not yet proven. Famous examples: Goldbach's Conjecture (every even number greater than 2 is the sum of two primes—true for trillions of numbers but still not proven).

**Theorem**
A proven statement. Once proven, it is true forever.

### Rational and Irrational Numbers

**Rational number**
A number that can be written as a fraction of two integers: a/b, where a and b are integers and b ≠ 0.
Examples: 1/2, 3/4, 5, −7/3, 0.25

**Irrational number**
A number that cannot be written as a fraction of two integers.
Examples: √2, π, e, φ (the golden ratio)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Recognize Evidence vs. Proof

**Scenario**: A student checks 100 numbers and finds that the sum of any two prime numbers (except 2 + 2 = 4) is even.

Is this evidence? Is this a proof?

**Answer**

This is evidence, not proof. The student has found a pattern in 100 cases. But this does not prove the statement is true for all prime numbers. To prove it, you would need a logical argument that works for any two primes, not just the ones tested.

(In fact, the statement is true—the proof is: all primes except 2 are odd; the sum of two odd numbers is even—but checking examples alone is not the proof.)

### Example 2: Find a Counterexample

**Claim**: "If a number is divisible by 6, then it is divisible by 4."

Is this true? If not, give a counterexample.

**Answer**

This claim is false. A counterexample is 6 itself:
- 6 is divisible by 6 (6 ÷ 6 = 1).
- 6 is not divisible by 4 (6 ÷ 4 = 1.5).

One example is enough to disprove the claim entirely.

### Example 3: Direct Proof

**Claim**: If n is an even integer, then n² is even.

**Proof**

- Assume n is an even integer.
- By definition, an even integer can be written as n = 2k, where k is some integer.
- Then n² = (2k)² = 4k² = 2(2k²).
- Since n² = 2(2k²), and 2k² is an integer, n² is even (it is a multiple of 2).
- Therefore: If n is even, then n² is even.

## Worked Example: The Proof That √2 Is Irrational

This is the centerpiece of this chapter. We will prove that √2 cannot be written as a fraction.

### The Proof by Contradiction

**What we want to prove**: √2 is irrational (cannot be written as a fraction a/b where a and b are integers).

**The proof**:

**Step 1: Make an assumption (the opposite of what we want to prove)**

Assume, for the sake of contradiction, that √2 *can* be written as a fraction.

Assume: √2 = a/b, where a and b are integers, and the fraction is in lowest terms (meaning a and b share no common factors; we have reduced it as far as possible).

**Step 2: Square both sides**

√2 = a/b

Square both sides:

(√2)² = (a/b)²

2 = a²/b²

Multiply both sides by b²:

2b² = a²

**Step 3: Observe what we have**

We now have: 2b² = a²

This means a² is even (it equals 2 times something).

If a² is even, then a itself must be even. (We can prove this: if a were odd, then a² would be odd, since the square of an odd number is odd. But a² = 2b², which is even. So a must be even.)

**Step 4: Write a in its simplest form**

Since a is even, we can write: a = 2m, where m is some integer.

**Step 5: Substitute back**

Substitute a = 2m into 2b² = a²:

2b² = (2m)²

2b² = 4m²

Divide both sides by 2:

b² = 2m²

**Step 6: Reach a contradiction**

We now have: b² = 2m²

This means b² is even. By the same reasoning as before, if b² is even, then b itself must be even.

So we have concluded:
- a is even (from Step 3)
- b is even (from Step 6)

**But we assumed at the start that a/b was in lowest terms, meaning a and b share no common factors.**

If both a and b are even, then both are divisible by 2. They share a common factor of 2. This contradicts our assumption.

**Step 7: Conclusion**

Since our assumption that "√2 = a/b (in lowest terms)" leads to a logical contradiction, the assumption must be false.

Therefore: **√2 cannot be written as a fraction. √2 is irrational.**

### Why This Proof Works

The proof does not check a million numbers. It shows that *any attempt* to write √2 as a fraction leads to a logical impossibility. There is no escape. The proof covers all fractions, all at once, by showing that the very concept of "√2 as a fraction in lowest terms" is self-contradictory.

This is the power of proof by contradiction.

## Application
Proof is the foundation of everything mathematics builds:

**In Engineering**
A bridge or building is not designed by guessing or testing materials randomly. It is designed using proven principles of physics and mathematics. The safety of millions depends on proofs that were written centuries ago.

**In Computer Science**
When cryptography secures your bank account or online communications, it relies on proofs that certain mathematical problems are "hard"—that no shortcut exists. Algorithms are proven to be correct before they are trusted with your data.

**In Medicine**
Drug trials test thousands of cases, but doctors do not use a drug just because it worked in 1,000 examples. They demand statistical proofs that the drug works better than a placebo, accounting for probability and chance.

**In Law and Justice**
In legal reasoning, one piece of contradictory evidence can overturn a claim—like a counterexample in mathematics. The principle is the same: a single fact that contradicts an assertion can prove the assertion false.

**The Deeper Truth**
Proof separated mathematics from measurement. Before proof, you could measure and record. After proof, you could *know* something in a way that measurement alone never allows. A million correct measurements might be wrong on the 1,000,001st case. But a proof is certain. It is this certainty that gave humanity the confidence to build mathematics, science, and the modern world.

## Recap
- A **proof** is a logical argument showing why something must be true in all cases.
- A **theorem** is a proven statement; a **conjecture** is unproven.
- **Direct proof**: assume the starting conditions and reason to the conclusion.
- **Proof by contradiction**: assume the opposite, find a logical impossibility, conclude the original statement must be true.
- A single **counterexample** disproves a claim.
- **√2 is irrational**: proven by showing that assuming √2 = a/b (in lowest terms) leads to the logical impossibility that both a and b are even.
- Proof is the foundation of mathematical knowledge. It gives certainty in a way that checking examples never can.
