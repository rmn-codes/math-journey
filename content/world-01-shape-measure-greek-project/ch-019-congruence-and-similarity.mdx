# Congruence and Similarity

## Story Hook
An ancient surveyor stands before a river. She cannot cross it, and she cannot measure its width directly. But she can measure its width perfectly.

She places a marker on the far bank. Then she walks along her bank, places a stick in the ground, continues walking until the stick and the far marker appear to line up with a second stick. She measures the distance between her sticks. Then she measures her position and calculates the width.

She used similar triangles. The river formed one triangle; her setup formed a smaller triangle with the same proportions. What was true for the small triangle—if one side was twice another, for example—had to be true for the large one. Measurement became possible without crossing.

This is the power of similarity and congruence: shapes that look the same or share the same proportions carry the same information, even if one is much larger than the other.

## Why It Matters
Congruence and similarity matter because they:
- let us recognize when shapes are identical or have the same proportions
- form the foundation of logical proof in geometry
- allow indirect measurement of distances we cannot reach
- connect algebra (scale factors) to geometry
- are used in surveying, maps, models, and architecture
- show why angles and proportions are more informative than absolute size

If you understand congruence and similarity, you can measure a pyramid's height from the ground, design a house at scale, or prove a shape's properties without measuring every part.

## Historical Context
The ancient Egyptians built massive monuments and measured vast lands without calculus, computers, or steel tape measures. They used triangles.

The legend of Thales of Miletus (c. 600 BCE) states that he measured the height of a pyramid using the principle of similar triangles. He waited for the moment when his shadow was exactly as tall as his body, then measured the shadow of the pyramid. Because the proportions were the same, the pyramid's height equaled the length of its shadow.

Whether this story is literally true, it illustrates a real and powerful idea that the Greeks proved rigorously: if two triangles have the same angles, then their sides are proportional. Measure one triangle and the other scales predictably.

Euclid's *Elements* dedicates Books V and VI to proportions and similarity. His development of congruence and similarity was not just a collection of rules—it was a systematic proof that geometric shapes could be understood by their proportions rather than their absolute size.

## Core Concept
**Congruence** means exactly the same. Two congruent shapes have identical size and shape. If you cut one shape from paper, you could place it on top of the other with perfect overlap.

**Similarity** means proportionally the same. Two similar shapes have the same shape but different sizes. All corresponding angles are equal, and all corresponding side lengths are in the same ratio (called the **scale factor**).

For triangles, this distinction is powerful:
- **Congruent triangles** have three equal sides or angles sufficient to force all three pairs to be equal.
- **Similar triangles** have the same three angles; their sides are proportional.

The correspondence matters. When we say triangle ABC is congruent to triangle DEF, we mean:
- angle A = angle D
- angle B = angle E
- angle C = angle F
- side AB = side DE
- side BC = side EF
- side AC = side DF

The order of the letters reveals which parts correspond.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Key Terms

**Congruent**
Identical in size and shape. Two congruent figures have the same measurements and could be overlapped perfectly.

**Congruent Triangles**
Two triangles in which corresponding sides are equal in length and corresponding angles are equal in measure.

**Similar**
Having the same shape but not necessarily the same size. Corresponding angles are equal, and corresponding sides are proportional.

**Similar Triangles**
Two triangles in which all corresponding angles are equal and all corresponding sides are in the same proportion.

**Corresponding Parts**
Sides or angles in two triangles that match according to a stated correspondence. If triangle ABC ≅ triangle DEF, then side AB corresponds to side DE.

**Scale Factor**
The ratio of corresponding lengths in two similar figures. If the scale factor is 2, then every side of one triangle is twice the corresponding side of the other.

**CPCTC**
Abbreviation for "Corresponding Parts of Congruent Triangles are Congruent." If two triangles are congruent, then each pair of corresponding parts is also congruent.

### Triangle Congruence Criteria

There are four main ways to prove that two triangles are congruent. You do not need to check all six parts (three sides and three angles). These criteria let you prove congruence by checking only part of the information.

#### SSS (Side-Side-Side)
If the three sides of one triangle are equal to the three sides of another triangle, the triangles are congruent.

Example: Triangle ABC has sides 5, 7, 9. Triangle DEF has sides 5, 7, 9. Then ABC ≅ DEF.

#### SAS (Side-Angle-Side)
If two sides and the included angle (the angle between those two sides) of one triangle are equal to two sides and the included angle of another triangle, the triangles are congruent.

Example: Triangle ABC has sides AB = 6, AC = 8, and angle A = 50°. Triangle DEF has sides DE = 6, DF = 8, and angle D = 50°. Then ABC ≅ DEF.

#### ASA (Angle-Side-Angle)
If two angles and the included side (the side between those two angles) of one triangle are equal to two angles and the included side of another triangle, the triangles are congruent.

Example: Triangle ABC has angle A = 40°, side AB = 5, and angle B = 60°. Triangle DEF has angle D = 40°, side DE = 5, and angle E = 60°. Then ABC ≅ DEF.

#### AAS (Angle-Angle-Side)
If two angles and a non-included side (a side not between the two angles) of one triangle are equal to two angles and the corresponding non-included side of another triangle, the triangles are congruent.

Example: Triangle ABC has angle A = 35°, angle B = 65°, and side BC = 4. Triangle DEF has angle D = 35°, angle E = 65°, and side EF = 4. Then ABC ≅ DEF.

### Using CPCTC in Proofs
Once you prove two triangles are congruent, you can immediately conclude that all corresponding parts are congruent. This is abbreviated CPCTC.

Example: If you prove triangle ABC ≅ triangle DEF using SSS, then you can immediately conclude:
- angle A = angle D
- angle B = angle E
- angle C = angle F

### Similar Triangles and Scale Factor

If triangle ABC is similar to triangle DEF with a scale factor of k, then:
- Each side of DEF is k times the corresponding side of ABC.
- All corresponding angles are equal.
- The perimeter of DEF is k times the perimeter of ABC.
- The area of DEF is k² times the area of ABC.

### AA (Angle-Angle) Criterion for Similarity
If two angles of one triangle are equal to two angles of another triangle, the triangles are similar.

(The third angle must be equal because the angles of a triangle sum to 180°.)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Proving Congruence Using SSS
Triangle ABC has sides: AB = 6 cm, BC = 8 cm, AC = 10 cm.
Triangle DEF has sides: DE = 6 cm, EF = 8 cm, DF = 10 cm.

Prove that triangle ABC is congruent to triangle DEF.

**Solution**
AB = DE (both 6 cm)
BC = EF (both 8 cm)
AC = DF (both 10 cm)

By the SSS (Side-Side-Side) criterion, triangle ABC ≅ triangle DEF.

Therefore, by CPCTC:
- angle A = angle D
- angle B = angle E
- angle C = angle F

### Example 2: Proving Congruence Using SAS
Triangle PQR has: PQ = 5 m, angle P = 60°, and PR = 7 m.
Triangle STU has: ST = 5 m, angle S = 60°, and SU = 7 m.

Prove congruence and list all corresponding equal parts.

**Solution**
PQ = ST (both 5 m)
angle P = angle S (both 60°)
PR = SU (both 7 m)

The angle is between the two sides, so by SAS, triangle PQR ≅ triangle STU.

By CPCTC:
- PQ = ST ✓ (already given)
- PR = SU ✓ (already given)
- QR = TU (now congruent)
- angle P = angle S ✓ (already given)
- angle Q = angle T (now congruent)
- angle R = angle U (now congruent)

### Example 3: Finding Missing Sides Using Similar Triangles
Triangle ABC has sides: AB = 4 cm, BC = 5 cm, AC = 6 cm.
Triangle DEF is similar to triangle ABC with a scale factor of 3.

Find the sides of triangle DEF.

**Solution**
The scale factor is 3, so every side of DEF is 3 times the corresponding side of ABC.

DE = 3 × AB = 3 × 4 = 12 cm
EF = 3 × BC = 3 × 5 = 15 cm
DF = 3 × AC = 3 × 6 = 18 cm

### Example 4: Using Similar Triangles for Indirect Measurement
A tree casts a shadow 12 meters long. At the same time, a 2-meter vertical stick casts a shadow 3 meters long. Both cast shadows at the same angle to the sun. Find the height of the tree.

**Solution**
The triangle formed by the sun's rays on the stick is similar to the triangle formed on the tree.

Let h = height of the tree.

For the stick: height / shadow = 2 / 3
For the tree: height / shadow = h / 12

Since the triangles are similar, the ratios are equal:
h / 12 = 2 / 3

Cross-multiply:
3h = 2 × 12
3h = 24
h = 8 meters

The tree is 8 meters tall.

## Application
Congruence and similarity are used everywhere:

**Surveying and Land Measurement:** Ancient and modern surveyors use similar triangles to measure distances across water, valleys, or obstacles. By setting up proportional triangles on accessible land, they calculate inaccessible distances.

**Architecture and Construction:** Architects design buildings at scale (blueprints). A blueprint 1:100 scale means 1 cm on the blueprint represents 1 meter in reality. Congruence ensures that repeated elements (windows, columns, stairs) are identical.

**Maps and Navigation:** Every map is a similar representation of the land. A map at 1:10,000 scale has all regions proportionally smaller. Distances on the map scale predictably to real distances.

**Manufacturing and Engineering:** Parts must be congruent for machines to fit together. Tolerances ensure that identical parts are truly congruent within acceptable limits.

**Astronomy:** Astronomers measure stellar distances using similar triangles formed by Earth's orbit. By observing a star from opposite sides of the orbit, they create two sides of a triangle and calculate distance using similarity.

**Art and Design:** The golden ratio (a scale factor of approximately 1.618) appears in famous artwork and architecture because similar proportions are aesthetically pleasing.

## Recap
- **Congruence** means identical in size and shape; **similarity** means the same shape but possibly different sizes.
- Two congruent triangles have all three pairs of sides equal and all three pairs of angles equal.
- **Triangle congruence can be proven using:**
  - SSS (all three sides equal)
  - SAS (two sides and the included angle equal)
  - ASA (two angles and the included side equal)
  - AAS (two angles and a non-included side equal)
- **CPCTC** states that corresponding parts of congruent triangles are congruent.
- Two similar triangles have the same three angles and proportional sides.
- The **scale factor** is the ratio of corresponding lengths in similar figures.
- Similar triangles enable **indirect measurement**: measuring something inaccessible by measuring a proportional triangle that is accessible.
- Congruence and similarity connect measurement, proof, and practical surveying.
