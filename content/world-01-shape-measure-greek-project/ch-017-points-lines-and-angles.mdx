# Points, Lines, and Angles

## Story Hook
A surveyor stands on the edge of a valley at dawn. She has no compass, no instruments—only a stick, two markers, and her knowledge. She plants the stick upright and watches the shadow it casts. She measures the angle between the shadow and a distant peak. By that single angle, and others like it, she can measure a distance she can never walk.

Angles are how we measure direction, rotation, and separation. They are invisible, yet as measurable as distance. To understand them, we must first understand the simplest shapes: the point, the line, and the angle itself.

This chapter begins where the Greeks began: with the building blocks of all geometry.

## Why It Matters
Angles and lines matter because they:
- allow you to measure direction and rotation without walking or traveling
- let you describe the tilt and shape of everything you see
- form the basis for all polygons, which are made of line segments and angles
- appear everywhere in surveying, building, navigation, and astronomy
- connect geometry to trigonometry and advanced mathematics

If you understand points, lines, and angles, you have the foundation for everything else in geometry. Without them, shapes are just vague pictures. With them, shapes become measurable.

## Historical Context
Around 300 BCE, the Greek mathematician Euclid compiled the *Elements*, a 13-book compendium of geometry that would define mathematics for over 2000 years. He began not with complicated shapes but with definitions. The first definitions of the *Elements* are:

- A point is that which has no part.
- A line is a length without breadth.

These are idealized objects. A point on paper has a tiny dot with size. A line drawn on paper has width. But in geometry, we imagine perfect points with zero size and perfect lines with zero width extending infinitely in both directions.

This habit—of imagining ideal objects sharper and purer than anything we can draw—is central to how Greek geometry worked. The Greeks were not trying to describe drawings. They were describing ideas.

## Core Concept
Geometry begins with two things that seem like nothing: **the point** and **the line**.

A **point** is a location with no size. It has no length, width, or height. When you mark a dot on paper to represent a point, the dot itself is too big—it is just our way of pointing at the invisible location.

A **line** is straight and extends infinitely in both directions. It has length but no width or height. If you imagine a laser beam made infinitely thin, extending forever forward and forever backward, you have the idea of a line.

From these two primitives, we build everything else.

**Rays** and **line segments** are parts of a line:
- A **line segment** is the part of a line between two points, including those endpoints. It has finite length.
- A **ray** is a line that begins at a point and extends infinitely in one direction.

An **angle** is formed when two rays share the same starting point (called the **vertex**). The angle measures the rotation or separation between those rays.

**Angles are measured in degrees** (°). A full rotation is 360°. A rotation halfway around is 180°. A quarter rotation is 90°.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Key Terms

**Point**
A location with no size, no length, no width, no height. Represented by a dot on paper or the intersection of two lines.

**Line**
A straight path extending infinitely in both directions, with no thickness. Defined by any two points on it.

**Line Segment**
The part of a line between two points, including the endpoints. Has finite length.

**Ray**
A part of a line that begins at a point and extends infinitely in one direction.

**Plane**
A flat surface extending infinitely in all directions, with no thickness. All of Euclidean geometry happens on a plane.

**Angle**
The measure of rotation between two rays that share a starting point (the vertex). Measured in degrees.

**Vertex**
The point where two rays or line segments meet to form an angle.

**Degree (°)**
A unit of angle measurement. One full rotation = 360°.

### Naming Angles and Line Segments
When three points are collinear (on the same line), we can name an angle using three letters:
- The middle letter is the vertex.
- The first and third letters are points on the two rays.

Example: angle ABC is the angle at point B, formed by ray BA and ray BC.

We can also name an angle with a single letter or number: angle B, or ∠1.

### Classifying Angles by Measure

#### Acute Angle
An angle that measures more than 0° and less than 90°.

Example: 45°, 60°, 89°

#### Right Angle
An angle that measures exactly 90°. Often marked with a small square in the corner.

#### Obtuse Angle
An angle that measures more than 90° and less than 180°.

Example: 100°, 135°, 179°

#### Straight Angle
An angle that measures exactly 180°. The two rays form a straight line.

#### Reflex Angle
An angle that measures more than 180° and less than 360°.

Example: 200°, 270°, 359°

### Complementary and Supplementary Angles

**Complementary Angles**
Two angles that add up to 90°.

If angle A = 35°, then its complement is 90° − 35° = 55°.

**Supplementary Angles**
Two angles that add up to 180°.

If angle A = 100°, then its supplement is 180° − 100° = 80°.

### Vertical Angles
When two lines intersect, they form four angles. The angles opposite each other (not touching) are called **vertical angles**, and they are always equal.

Example: If two lines cross, and one angle is 50°, then the angle directly opposite it is also 50°. The other two angles are each 130°.

### The Angle Sum at a Point
All angles formed around a single point on one side of a line add up to 180°.
All angles formed around a complete point add up to 360°.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Identifying Angle Types
Classify each angle by its measure:
- Angle 1: 35°
- Angle 2: 90°
- Angle 3: 145°
- Angle 4: 180°

**Solution**
- Angle 1 (35°): acute (less than 90°)
- Angle 2 (90°): right (exactly 90°)
- Angle 3 (145°): obtuse (more than 90°, less than 180°)
- Angle 4 (180°): straight (exactly 180°)

### Example 2: Finding Complementary and Supplementary Angles
One angle measures 42°.

a) Find its complement.
b) Find its supplement.

**Solution**
a) Complement: 90° − 42° = 48°

b) Supplement: 180° − 42° = 138°

### Example 3: Vertical Angles and Missing Angles
Two lines intersect. One of the angles formed is 65°. Find all four angles.

**Solution**
When two lines intersect, they form two pairs of vertical angles.
- Angle 1: 65° (given)
- Angle 2: 65° (vertical to Angle 1)
- Angle 3: 180° − 65° = 115° (supplementary to Angle 1)
- Angle 4: 115° (vertical to Angle 3)

Check: 65° + 115° + 65° + 115° = 360° ✓

### Example 4: Using Complementary Angles
Two angles are complementary. One angle is twice the other. Find both angles.

**Solution**
Let the smaller angle = x
Then the larger angle = 2x

Since they are complementary: x + 2x = 90°
3x = 90°
x = 30°

The smaller angle is 30°.
The larger angle is 2(30°) = 60°.

Check: 30° + 60° = 90° ✓

## Application
Angles appear everywhere in the real world:

**Surveying and Navigation:** A surveyor uses angles to measure land and distances. By measuring angles to distant peaks or buildings, a surveyor can determine distances without traveling them.

**Architecture and Construction:** A builder must measure angles to ensure walls are perpendicular (90° angles) and roofs slope correctly. A leaning roof collapses; the angles must be precise.

**Astronomy:** Astronomers measure the angles between stars to map the sky and predict celestial motion.

**Sports and Games:** In billiards, golf, or bowling, the angle of impact determines where an object will go. Players understand angles intuitively, even if they do not think of degrees.

**Clock and Time:** The hands of a clock form angles that change as time passes. At 3:00, the hands form a 90° angle. At 6:00, they form a 180° angle.

## Recap
- A **point** is a location with no size.
- A **line** extends infinitely in both directions.
- A **line segment** is a finite piece of a line.
- A **ray** begins at a point and extends infinitely in one direction.
- An **angle** measures the rotation between two rays that share a vertex.
- Angles are measured in **degrees**, where a full rotation is 360°.
- **Acute angles** measure less than 90°; **right angles** measure exactly 90°; **obtuse angles** measure between 90° and 180°.
- **Complementary angles** add to 90°; **supplementary angles** add to 180°.
- **Vertical angles** (opposite angles formed by intersecting lines) are always equal.
- Understanding angles is the foundation for measuring direction, rotation, and all geometric shapes.
