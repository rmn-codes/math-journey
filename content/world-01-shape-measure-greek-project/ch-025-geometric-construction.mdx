# Geometric Construction

## Story Hook
Imagine a mathematician in ancient Athens with a compass and a straightedge—that is, a ruler with no markings. No measurements. No numbers written down. The straightedge cannot tell you distances; it only draws straight lines. The compass, when two legs are set, can draw a circle of that radius, but it cannot measure distances either.

With only these two tools, could you bisect an angle? Yes. Could you construct a regular hexagon? Yes. Could you divide any angle into three equal parts? The Greeks could not, despite trying for centuries. Could you construct a square with the same area as a given circle? Impossible—though it took until the 19th century to prove why.

This chapter is about the power and limits of pure geometric reasoning. It is also about how the Greeks saw mathematics: not as computation, but as construction. When you construct something with compass and straightedge, you create a proof. The act of building reveals why the thing exists.

## Why It Matters
Geometric construction matters because:
- it reveals which geometric facts are logically possible and which are not
- it separates what can be done by the rules from what cannot, showing the boundaries of geometry itself
- Euclid used construction as proof—if you can construct it, it exists and you have shown why
- the impossibility proofs took 2,000 years but ultimately proved profound truths about algebra and geometry
- understanding limits is as important as understanding possibilities

When you learn what cannot be constructed, you learn something deep about the structure of space and number.

## Historical Context
Euclid's *Elements*, written around 300 BCE, begins not with numbers or formulas but with construction. Proposition 1 asks: Given a line segment, construct an equilateral triangle on it. Proposition 2: Copy a distance from one place to another. For Euclid, geometry meant construction.

For nearly 2,000 years, mathematicians tried to solve three famous problems:
1. **Squaring the circle**: Construct a square with area equal to a given circle.
2. **Trisecting an angle**: Divide any angle into three equal parts.
3. **Doubling the cube**: Construct a cube with twice the volume of a given cube.

All three are impossible with compass and straightedge alone. But it took until the 19th century to prove this rigorously. Carl Friedrich Gauss determined which regular polygons can be constructed (the number of sides must be of a specific form). Pierre Wantzel proved in 1837 that angle trisection and cube doubling are impossible. The transcendence of π was proved by Ferdinand von Lindemann in 1882, making the impossibility of squaring the circle complete.

These proofs revealed a deep truth: geometry and algebra are intertwined. Some shapes cannot be constructed because the algebra required is impossible within the constraints of compass and straightedge.

## Core Concept
A **compass-and-straightedge construction** is a geometric figure created using only these two tools, starting with a given line segment or angle. No measurements. No markings. Only the rules of geometry.

A **constructible figure** is one that can be built this way. An **impossible construction** is one that cannot be built, no matter how cleverly you try.

The key insight: if you can construct something, you have proved it exists. If no construction exists after 2,000 years of trying, it is probably impossible—and later mathematicians can prove why.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### The Tools

**Straightedge**
A ruler with no markings. It can draw a straight line through two points, but it cannot measure distance.

**Compass**
A tool with two legs. You can place one leg at a point and adjust the other leg until it touches another point, then draw a circle. The compass cannot measure distance in absolute terms, but it can copy a distance from one place to another.

### Basic Construction: Bisecting a Segment

**Given**: A line segment AB

**To construct**: The midpoint M of AB (the point that divides AB into two equal parts)

**Steps**:
1. Place the compass point at A. Open the compass to more than half the distance to B.
2. Draw a circle centered at A.
3. Without changing the compass width, place the compass point at B and draw another circle.
4. The two circles intersect at two points, one above and one below the segment AB.
5. Use the straightedge to draw a line through these two intersection points. This line is the perpendicular bisector of AB.
6. Mark the point where this line crosses AB. This is the midpoint M.

### Basic Construction: Bisecting an Angle

**Given**: An angle with vertex O and two rays OA and OB

**To construct**: A ray OC that divides the angle into two equal parts

**Steps**:
1. Place the compass point at O. Draw a circle with any radius. Mark where it crosses ray OA (call this point P) and ray OB (call this point Q).
2. Place the compass point at P. Open the compass to more than half the distance from P to Q.
3. Draw a circle centered at P.
4. Without changing the compass width, place the compass point at Q and draw another circle.
5. The two circles intersect at a point. (Choose the intersection on the side of the angle you want to bisect.)
6. Use the straightedge to draw a line from O through this intersection point. This is the angle bisector.

### Basic Construction: Perpendicular to a Line

**Given**: A line and a point on that line

**To construct**: A line perpendicular to the given line at the given point

**Steps**:
1. Place the compass point at the given point. Draw a circle with any radius to mark two points on the line, equidistant from the given point.
2. These two points define a segment. Now bisect this segment using the perpendicular bisector method (draw circles from each endpoint, find the intersection, draw the line through the intersections).
3. This perpendicular bisector is perpendicular to the original line.

### Basic Construction: Regular Hexagon

**Given**: A line segment that will be one side of the hexagon

**To construct**: A regular hexagon with that side length

**Steps**:
1. Place the compass point at one endpoint of the segment. Open it to the length of the segment (copy the distance).
2. Draw two circles: one centered at each endpoint, both with radius equal to the segment length.
3. Mark where these circles intersect. These two points are vertices of the hexagon.
4. Continue this process: each vertex of a regular hexagon is separated from adjacent vertices by a distance equal to the side length, and the six vertices lie on a circle.
5. By drawing circles of this fixed radius centered at each new vertex, you can locate all six vertices.
6. Use the straightedge to connect them.

### Impossible Constructions

**Squaring the circle**
Construct a square with area equal to a given circle. This is impossible because π is transcendental—it cannot be expressed as a root of any polynomial equation with rational coefficients. The compass-and-straightedge method can only produce algebraic numbers.

**Trisecting an arbitrary angle**
Divide any given angle into three equal parts. This is impossible (in general) because it requires solving a cubic equation that cannot be reduced to the quadratic equations that compass-and-straightedge constructions can handle. (You can trisect some special angles, like 90°, but not all angles.)

**Doubling the cube**
Construct a cube with twice the volume of a given cube. This is impossible because it requires constructing the cube root of 2, which is not constructible with compass and straightedge.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Bisect a Line Segment (Written Description)

**Given**: A segment from point A to point B

**Goal**: Find the midpoint

**Construction steps**:

1. Open the compass to more than half the distance AB (you can guess—no measurement needed).
2. Place the compass point on A and draw an arc above the segment AB.
3. Place the compass point on B, keep the compass width the same, and draw another arc above AB. The two arcs intersect.
4. Place the compass point on A and draw an arc below AB.
5. Place the compass point on B and draw another arc below AB. The two arcs intersect below.
6. Use the straightedge to draw a line through the two intersection points (one above, one below).
7. This line crosses AB at the midpoint. Call this point M.

**Why it works**: The two intersection points are equidistant from both A and B (they lie on circles of the same radius centered at A and B). A point equidistant from two points lies on the perpendicular bisector. Where this bisector crosses AB is the midpoint.

### Example 2: Bisect an Angle (Written Description)

**Given**: An angle with vertex O, one ray pointing to A, one ray pointing to B

**Goal**: Construct a ray from O that divides the angle in half

**Construction steps**:

1. Place the compass point at O. Draw a circle with any convenient radius (the radius does not matter). This circle intersects ray OA at point P and ray OB at point Q.
2. Now the problem reduces to: bisect segment PQ (which lies in the interior of the angle).
3. Place the compass point at P. Open it to more than half the distance from P to Q.
4. Draw an arc in the interior of the angle.
5. Place the compass point at Q, keep the compass width, and draw another arc in the interior. The two arcs intersect at point R.
6. Use the straightedge to draw a line from O through R. This line is the angle bisector.

**Why it works**: Since the two arcs have the same radius and are centered at P and Q (which are equidistant from O), the intersection point R is equidistant from P and Q. The line OR passes through the midpoint of arc PQ, dividing the angle symmetrically.

### Example 3: Why You Cannot Trisect All Angles

Some angles can be trisected. For example, 90° can be divided into three 30° angles using compass and straightedge. But a general angle cannot.

**Why**: Constructing a 30° angle from a 90° angle is possible. But constructing a 1° angle from a 3° angle (trisecting 3°) requires solving a cubic equation. Compass-and-straightedge constructions can only solve quadratic equations (equations where the highest power is 2). A cubic equation (highest power is 3) is beyond their reach, except in special cases.

This was finally proved rigorously by Pierre Wantzel in 1837, but mathematicians had suspected it for 2,000 years.

## Application
Compass-and-straightedge construction may seem like an ancient curiosity, but the ideas behind it matter today:

**In Computer Graphics**
When a computer constructs geometric figures, it follows rules similar to compass-and-straightedge. The concept of what is "constructible" applies to algorithmic geometry.

**In Architecture and Design**
Architects sometimes use classical constructions because they are elegant and carry aesthetic weight. A regular hexagon constructed geometrically has a purity that approximations lack.

**In Pure Mathematics**
The study of what is constructible led to Galois theory and modern algebra. Understanding why certain constructions are impossible opened entirely new branches of mathematics.

**In Logic and Computer Science**
The question "What can be computed?" has roots in "What can be constructed?" Computability theory and complexity theory inherit the spirit of asking about limits.

**Euclid's Legacy**
Euclid believed that geometry meant construction—the act of building a figure *was* the proof of its existence. In a sense, modern computer science carries on this tradition. An algorithm is a construction. When we ask whether a problem is computable, we ask whether it can be constructed by a finite set of rules.

## Recap
- **Compass-and-straightedge construction** uses only two tools: a straightedge (unmarked) and a compass.
- **Basic constructions include**: bisecting a segment, bisecting an angle, constructing perpendiculars, and constructing regular polygons like the hexagon.
- **Impossible constructions** (proven in the 19th century) include: squaring the circle, trisecting an arbitrary angle, and doubling the cube.
- The impossibility of these constructions reveals deep truths about algebra and geometry.
- A construction is a proof: if you can build it, it exists.
