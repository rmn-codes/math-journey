# Triangles

## Story Hook
Build a square frame from four sticks. Push on one corner, and it collapses. The square is not rigid.

Build a triangle frame from three sticks. Push on any corner, and the structure holds. The triangle is rigid.

This is why ancient builders understood triangles before they understood them mathematically. A three-legged stool does not wobble. A tripod is stable. An isosceles roof does not sag. When engineers need strength and stability—a bridge, a crane, a building—they frame it with triangles.

The triangle is not just the simplest polygon. It is the strongest.

## Why It Matters
Triangles matter because they:
- are the only polygon rigid enough to hold its shape under pressure
- can be broken down into simpler pieces to measure complex shapes
- appear in all advanced geometry and trigonometry
- determine the angles and proportions of all other polygons
- are the basis for engineering, surveying, and architecture

Every other polygon can be divided into triangles. If you understand triangles deeply, you can understand all shapes.

## Historical Context
The ancient Greeks and Egyptians were master builders and surveyors. They knew from experience that triangles were special. But it was the Greek mathematician Euclid who proved why.

In his *Elements* (Book I, Proposition 32), Euclid proved that the sum of the angles in any triangle is always 180°. This is not a rule of thumb or an observation—it is a logical proof that follows from the definitions and axioms of geometry.

Egyptian surveyors had used triangles to measure land and find distances they could not walk. The legend says that Thales of Miletus (c. 600 BCE) used similar triangles to measure the height of a pyramid by comparing the shadow of a stick to the shadow of the pyramid. Whether or not the story is true, it illustrates a real idea: you can measure things indirectly using triangles.

## Core Concept
A **triangle** is a polygon with three vertices and three sides.

That is the simplest definition. But from this simple shape, a vast world of geometry unfolds.

Every triangle has three interior angles. These angles have a remarkable property: they always add to 180°. This is true for a tiny triangle drawn on paper, a massive triangular field, and any triangle ever drawn.

**All triangles can be classified in two ways:**

By their **sides:**
- Scalene: all three sides have different lengths
- Isosceles: two sides have equal length
- Equilateral: all three sides have equal length

By their **angles:**
- Acute: all three angles are less than 90°
- Right: one angle is exactly 90°
- Obtuse: one angle is greater than 90°

A triangle can be both. A triangle can be isosceles and acute, scalene and right, or equilateral and acute.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Key Terms

**Triangle**
A polygon with three vertices, three sides, and three interior angles.

**Vertex** (plural: vertices)
A corner point of the triangle where two sides meet.

**Side**
A line segment connecting two vertices of the triangle.

**Interior Angle**
An angle inside the triangle, formed by two sides meeting at a vertex.

**Angle Sum Theorem**
The sum of the three interior angles of any triangle is always 180°.

**Acute Triangle**
A triangle where all three angles are less than 90°.

**Right Triangle**
A triangle with one angle equal to 90°. The side opposite the right angle is called the **hypotenuse**.

**Obtuse Triangle**
A triangle with one angle greater than 90°.

**Scalene Triangle**
A triangle with no two sides of equal length.

**Isosceles Triangle**
A triangle with exactly two sides of equal length. The angles opposite the equal sides are also equal.

**Equilateral Triangle**
A triangle with all three sides of equal length. All three angles are also equal, measuring 60° each.

**Exterior Angle**
An angle formed between one side of a triangle and the extension of another side, outside the triangle.

**Triangle Inequality**
For any three lengths to form a triangle, the sum of any two sides must be greater than the third side.

### The Angle Sum Theorem
In any triangle, if the three angles are A, B, and C, then:

A + B + C = 180°

This is true regardless of the size or shape of the triangle.

### Classification by Sides

#### Scalene
No two sides are equal.
No two angles are equal.

#### Isosceles
Exactly two sides are equal. These are called the **legs**.
The third side is called the **base**.
The two angles at the base are equal (called **base angles**).
The angle between the legs is called the **vertex angle**.

#### Equilateral
All three sides are equal.
All three angles are equal.
Each angle measures 60°.

### Classification by Angles

#### Acute Triangle
All three angles measure less than 90°.
The sum of the three angles still equals 180°.

#### Right Triangle
One angle measures exactly 90°.
The other two angles are acute and add to 90° (they are complementary).
The side opposite the right angle (the longest side) is called the **hypotenuse**.

#### Obtuse Triangle
One angle measures greater than 90°.
The other two angles are acute and sum to less than 90°.

### The Exterior Angle Theorem
An exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles (the two interior angles not touching that exterior angle).

### The Triangle Inequality
For three lengths a, b, and c to form a triangle:
- a + b > c
- b + c > a
- a + c > b

All three conditions must be true. If any one fails, the three lengths cannot form a triangle.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Finding a Missing Angle
In triangle ABC, angle A = 50° and angle B = 65°. Find angle C.

**Solution**
Using the angle sum theorem: A + B + C = 180°

50° + 65° + C = 180°
115° + C = 180°
C = 180° − 115°
C = 65°

Angle C measures 65°. ✓

### Example 2: Classifying a Triangle by Sides and Angles
A triangle has sides measuring 5 cm, 5 cm, and 8 cm.

a) Classify it by sides.
b) If the two equal sides form a 40° angle, find the other two angles.
c) Classify it by angles.

**Solution**
a) Two sides are equal (5 cm and 5 cm), so this is an **isosceles triangle**.

b) The angle between the equal sides is the vertex angle = 40°.
The other two angles are the base angles, and they are equal.
Let each base angle = x

40° + x + x = 180°
40° + 2x = 180°
2x = 140°
x = 70°

Each base angle measures 70°.

c) All three angles are less than 90° (40°, 70°, 70°), so this is an **acute triangle**.

Classification: **isosceles acute triangle**

### Example 3: Using the Triangle Inequality
Determine whether three line segments with lengths 6, 8, and 15 can form a triangle.

**Solution**
Check all three conditions:
- 6 + 8 > 15? → 14 > 15? **No.** ✗

Since the first condition fails, these three lengths cannot form a triangle.

If you try to connect three sides of lengths 6, 8, and 15, the two shorter sides (6 + 8 = 14) are not long enough to reach across the longest side (15). The triangle would be deformed and open.

### Example 4: Using the Exterior Angle Theorem
In triangle DEF, an exterior angle at vertex F measures 120°. The two non-adjacent interior angles are D = 40° and E = 80°. Verify the exterior angle theorem.

**Solution**
The exterior angle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles:
D + E = 40° + 80° = 120° ✓

This matches the exterior angle of 120°.

As a bonus, the interior angle at F is:
F = 180° − 120° = 60°

Check: 40° + 80° + 60° = 180° ✓

## Application
Triangles are everywhere in the real world because they are structurally strong:

**Building and Construction:** Roof trusses, bridge beams, and crane structures use triangles for strength. A rectangle collapses under stress, but a triangle holds.

**Surveying and Navigation:** Surveyors divide land into triangles and use triangle properties to measure distances. Ships and airplanes navigate using triangular pathways.

**Engineering:** Every rigid structure—from bicycle frames to towers to spacecraft—uses triangles to maintain shape and withstand forces.

**Art and Design:** Triangles create visual balance and tension. Many logos and architectural features use triangular proportions.

**Astronomy:** Astronomers use triangles to measure the distance to stars. By observing the same star from two different positions on Earth's orbit, they form a triangle and calculate stellar distance.

## Recap
- A **triangle** is a polygon with three vertices, three sides, and three angles.
- The **angle sum theorem** states that the three interior angles of any triangle always sum to 180°.
- Triangles are classified **by sides**: scalene (no equal sides), isosceles (two equal sides), equilateral (three equal sides).
- Triangles are classified **by angles**: acute (all angles less than 90°), right (one 90° angle), obtuse (one angle greater than 90°).
- In an **isosceles triangle**, the base angles (opposite the equal sides) are equal.
- In an **equilateral triangle**, all three angles measure 60°.
- The **triangle inequality** states that the sum of any two sides must be greater than the third side.
- An **exterior angle** equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.
- Triangles are rigid and structurally strong, which is why they appear in buildings, bridges, and engineering.
