## Story Hook

Picture this: René Descartes lies in bed, fevered and restless, in the winter of 1637. His eyes drift upward to the wooden ceiling of his Paris apartment. A fly lands on the plaster, crawls left, crawls up, circles, lands again.

In that moment of feverish observation, something clicks.

Descartes reaches for paper. He doesn't think: "There's a fly." Instead he thinks: *If I measure from that corner, the fly is three feet right and two feet up. Now it's four feet right and three feet up.*

Two numbers. Just two numbers, measured from a fixed corner, described every position the fly reached.

By the time Descartes recovered, he had written *La Géométrie* — "The Geometry" — a short book that would change mathematics forever. In it, he showed how to use two numbers to locate any point in a flat space. He married algebra and geometry. He made it possible to *draw* an equation and *write* a picture.

That marriage is called the **coordinate plane**, and it is the foundation of everything you'll do from this chapter forward.

## Why It Matters

Before Descartes, algebra and geometry lived in separate kingdoms. Algebra dealt with equations: *x* plus *y* equals 5. Geometry dealt with shapes: circles, triangles, and lines. Rarely did they speak.

Descartes's insight was radical: *Describe a geometric shape as an algebraic equation, and you can solve geometry problems with algebra — and visualize algebra as pictures.*

This fusion unlocked the modern world. When engineers design a bridge, they write equations. When economists predict a market, they graph data. When you program a video game, you move characters through coordinate space. When a rocket launches, its trajectory is plotted on a coordinate system.

The coordinate plane is the lens through which we see relationships between quantities, the language we use to turn numbers into images and images into numbers.

## Historical Context

René Descartes (1596–1650) was a French philosopher and mathematician who spent much of his life in the Dutch Netherlands, partly for freedom of thought and partly to avoid the watchful eye of the Church. He was famous for his motto: "I think, therefore I am."

But Descartes was also a practical inventor. He designed grinding lenses for telescopes. He theorized about the nature of light. And in 1637, while battling illness in Paris, he completed *La Géométrie* as an appendix to his philosophical work *Discourse on Method*.

It was not an instant sensation. The book was written in French (not Latin, the scholarly language), and Descartes's prose was dense. But mathematicians of the time — including Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz — quickly recognized its power. The coordinate system became known as the "Cartesian" plane (Cartesian from *Cartesius*, the Latinized version of Descartes's name).

By the 1700s, the coordinate plane had become the backbone of calculus and the scientific method. It remains so today.

## Core Concept

Imagine a flat piece of graph paper. Draw a horizontal line across the middle and a vertical line down the center. These lines divide the plane into four regions.

- The **horizontal line** is the **x-axis**.
- The **vertical line** is the **y-axis**.
- The point where they meet is the **origin**, labeled $O$ or $(0, 0)$.

The origin is your reference point. It is "zero and zero" — zero distance right or left, zero distance up or down.

Now, pick any point on the plane. To locate it, you answer two questions:

1. How far right (or left) are you from the y-axis? This distance is your **x-coordinate**.
2. How far up (or down) are you from the x-axis? This distance is your **y-coordinate**.

These two numbers, written in order as $(x, y)$, form an **ordered pair**. The order matters: $(3, 5)$ is not the same as $(5, 3)$.

**Right is positive** on the x-axis. **Left is negative**. **Up is positive** on the y-axis. **Down is negative**.

The plane is divided into four quadrants:

- **Quadrant I** (upper right): $x > 0, y > 0$
- **Quadrant II** (upper left): $x < 0, y > 0$
- **Quadrant III** (lower left): $x < 0, y < 0$
- **Quadrant IV** (lower right): $x > 0, y < 0$

Points on the axes themselves do not belong to any quadrant.

Two powerful tools let you measure distances and find midpoints:

The **distance formula** finds the straight-line distance between two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$$

This comes directly from the Pythagorean theorem: the distance is the hypotenuse of a right triangle whose legs are the horizontal and vertical separations.

The **midpoint formula** finds the center point between two points:

$$M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$$

Just average the x-coordinates and average the y-coordinates.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Coordinate Plane:** A two-dimensional surface formed by two perpendicular number lines (the x-axis and y-axis) intersecting at the origin.

**Ordered Pair:** A pair of numbers $(x, y)$ that specifies the location of a point. The first number is the x-coordinate; the second is the y-coordinate.

**x-Coordinate:** The first number in an ordered pair; indicates horizontal position (distance right or left from the y-axis).

**y-Coordinate:** The second number in an ordered pair; indicates vertical position (distance up or down from the x-axis).

**Origin:** The point $(0, 0)$ where the x-axis and y-axis intersect; the reference point for all other locations.

**Quadrant:** One of the four regions created by the intersection of the x-axis and y-axis.

**Distance Formula:** The formula $d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$ that calculates the straight-line distance between two points on a coordinate plane.

**Midpoint Formula:** The formula $M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$ that finds the center point between two given points.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Plotting Points**

Plot the points $A = (3, 2)$, $B = (-2, 4)$, $C = (-1, -3)$, and $D = (4, -1)$ on a coordinate plane, and name the quadrant of each.

*Solution:*

- $A = (3, 2)$: Start at the origin. Move 3 units right, then 2 units up. This point is in **Quadrant I**.
- $B = (-2, 4)$: Start at the origin. Move 2 units left, then 4 units up. This point is in **Quadrant II**.
- $C = (-1, -3)$: Start at the origin. Move 1 unit left, then 3 units down. This point is in **Quadrant III**.
- $D = (4, -1)$: Start at the origin. Move 4 units right, then 1 unit down. This point is in **Quadrant IV**.

**Example 2: Finding Distance Between Two Points**

Find the distance between $P = (1, 2)$ and $Q = (4, 6)$.

*Solution:*

Use the distance formula with $(x_1, y_1) = (1, 2)$ and $(x_2, y_2) = (4, 6)$:

$$d = \sqrt{(4 - 1)^2 + (6 - 2)^2} = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$

The distance is **5 units**.

**Example 3: Finding the Midpoint**

Find the midpoint of the segment joining $R = (2, 3)$ and $S = (8, 7)$.

*Solution:*

Use the midpoint formula:

$$M = \left(\frac{2 + 8}{2}, \frac{3 + 7}{2}\right) = \left(\frac{10}{2}, \frac{10}{2}\right) = (5, 5)$$

The midpoint is **(5, 5)**.

**Example 4: Reading Coordinates from a Graph**

Suppose a point is located 5 units left of the origin and 2 units above it. What is its ordered pair? Which quadrant?

*Solution:*

Left means negative x; above means positive y. The ordered pair is **(-5, 2)**, and it lies in **Quadrant II**.

## Reasoning Task

**The Fly's Path**

Imagine Descartes watching his fly again. The fly starts at position $(2, 3)$ relative to the corner of the ceiling. It crawls to position $(8, 7)$.

How far did the fly travel? (Assume it crawls in a straight line.)

If Descartes marks a point exactly halfway along the fly's path, what are its coordinates?

If the fly had instead crawled from $(2, 3)$ to $(6, 3)$ (moving only horizontally), how far would it have traveled? What about if it crawled from $(2, 3)$ to $(2, 9)$ (moving only vertically)?

Explain why the distance formula becomes simpler when a line is purely horizontal or purely vertical.

## Application

**Bridge Design**

A civil engineer is designing a footbridge over a river. She establishes a coordinate system with the origin at a concrete pier. One support beam runs from point $A = (0, 10)$ to point $B = (24, 0)$, measured in meters.

The engineer needs to know:
1. What is the length of the support beam?
2. Where should the midpoint reinforcement be placed?
3. If the beam runs from lower right to upper left, which quadrants does it pass through?

*Solution:*

1. Length: $d = \sqrt{(24-0)^2 + (0-10)^2} = \sqrt{576 + 100} = \sqrt{676} = 26$ meters.

2. Midpoint: $M = \left(\frac{0+24}{2}, \frac{10+0}{2}\right) = (12, 5)$. The reinforcement goes 12 meters horizontally and 5 meters vertically from the pier.

3. The beam starts at $(0, 10)$ (on the positive y-axis), passes through Quadrant I, and ends at $(24, 0)$ (on the positive x-axis). It stays entirely in Quadrant I and on its boundary.

## Recap

The coordinate plane is a system for locating points using two perpendicular axes and ordered pairs. The x-coordinate measures horizontal distance; the y-coordinate measures vertical distance. The origin $(0, 0)$ is the reference point. The four quadrants divide the plane based on the signs of $x$ and $y$.

The distance formula, derived from the Pythagorean theorem, calculates the straight-line distance between any two points. The midpoint formula finds the center point between two locations.

These tools, born from Descartes's observation of a fly, form the foundation of coordinate geometry and every graph you'll encounter from here forward.
