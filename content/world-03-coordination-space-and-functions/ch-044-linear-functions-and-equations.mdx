## Story Hook

A shopkeeper in 18th-century Paris keeps careful records of her business. She buys fabric and sells it by the bolt.

For every bolt sold, she earns 4 gold coins after costs. But to open her shop each morning, she must pay 20 coins for rent and supplies.

On a day when she sells zero bolts, she is down 20 coins. After selling 5 bolts, she has broken even. After 10 bolts, she has earned 20 coins profit.

She notices a pattern and draws a line on paper: the horizontal axis is bolts sold, the vertical axis is profit. The line starts at the point $(0, -20)$ and climbs steadily.

She realizes something profound: *This line is my business. Its slope is my earnings rate. Its starting point is my cost. If I know the equation, I can predict the future.*

By writing the equation $y = 4x - 20$, she can answer any question about profit and planning. This is the power of linear functions: they are models of the world, tools for prediction and understanding.

## Why It Matters

Linear equations are the first truly powerful mathematical models you'll learn. They describe relationships in business (profit, revenue, cost), science (distance and time, temperature and heating), medicine (drug concentration over time), and countless other fields.

There are three standard ways to write a linear equation, and each form is useful in different contexts. Slope-intercept form is perfect for graphing. Point-slope form is perfect when you know one point and the slope. Standard form is often used in systems of equations.

Being fluent in all three forms — and converting between them — is like being fluent in multiple languages. It gives you flexibility, insight, and power. It is the professional mathematician's approach.

Moreover, a linear equation is often called a linear function, and this chapter bridges algebra and the concept of functions, which will become central as your mathematical journey continues.

## Historical Context

By the 1700s, the coordinate plane was well established, and mathematicians had developed systematic ways to represent lines algebraically. The three main forms emerged organically from different problem contexts.

Slope-intercept form ($y = mx + b$) became the standard for graphing and visualization, especially as calculus developed and the focus shifted to understanding functions.

Point-slope form ($y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$) proved invaluable in calculus and advanced analysis, where you often know a point on a curve and its rate of change (slope) at that point.

Standard form ($Ax + By = C$) remained useful in systems of linear equations and linear algebra, which became fundamental tools in engineering, economics, and the social sciences by the 20th century.

All three forms coexist today because all three are useful. There is no single "best" form — context determines which to use.

## Core Concept

A **linear function** is a function of the form $f(x) = mx + b$ (or $y = mx + b$) where $m$ and $b$ are numbers. It is called a function because for every input $x$, there is exactly one output $y$.

There are three standard forms for writing linear equations:

### Slope-Intercept Form

$$y = mx + b$$

where:
- $m$ is the slope
- $b$ is the y-intercept (the value of $y$ when $x = 0$)

This form is ideal for graphing because you immediately see the y-intercept (plot it first) and the slope (which tells you how the line rises or falls).

### Point-Slope Form

$$y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$$

where:
- $(x_1, y_1)$ is a point on the line
- $m$ is the slope

This form is ideal when you know a specific point on the line and the slope. It's also the natural form in calculus when studying rates of change.

### Standard Form

$$Ax + By = C$$

where $A$, $B$, and $C$ are integers (whole numbers), and typically $A$ and $B$ are not both zero. Often, $A$ is positive.

This form is useful in systems of equations and in situations where both variables are treated symmetrically (neither one is explicitly isolated).

### Converting Between Forms

**From slope-intercept to standard form:**

$y = 2x + 3$

Rearrange: $2x - y = -3$ or $-2x + y = 3$ (standard form allows multiple equivalent representations).

**From standard form to slope-intercept:**

$3x + 2y = 6$

Solve for $y$: $2y = -3x + 6 \Rightarrow y = -\frac{3}{2}x + 3$

**From point-slope to slope-intercept:**

$y - 4 = 2(x - 1)$

Expand: $y - 4 = 2x - 2$

Rearrange: $y = 2x + 2$ (slope-intercept form)

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Linear Function:** A function of the form $f(x) = mx + b$ where $m$ and $b$ are constants; the graph is a straight line.

**Slope-Intercept Form:** The form $y = mx + b$, where $m$ is the slope and $b$ is the y-intercept. Ideal for graphing and identifying these two key values.

**Point-Slope Form:** The form $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$, where $(x_1, y_1)$ is a known point on the line and $m$ is the slope. Ideal when you have a point and slope.

**Standard Form:** The form $Ax + By = C$, where $A$, $B$, and $C$ are real numbers (often integers). Useful for systems of equations and when variables need equal treatment.

**y-Intercept:** The value $b$ in $y = mx + b$; the point where the line crosses the y-axis; the output when input is zero.

**Linear Model:** A linear equation or function used to represent a real-world relationship or make predictions.

**Function Notation:** Writing $f(x)$ instead of $y$; $f(x)$ represents the output of function $f$ when the input is $x$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Writing Equation from Slope and y-Intercept**

Write the equation of the line with slope $\frac{1}{2}$ and y-intercept $-3$.

*Solution:*

Use slope-intercept form directly:

$$y = \frac{1}{2}x - 3$$

**Example 2: Writing Equation from Slope and One Point**

A line has slope 3 and passes through the point $(2, 5)$. Write its equation in slope-intercept form.

*Solution:*

Use point-slope form first:

$$y - 5 = 3(x - 2)$$

Expand:

$$y - 5 = 3x - 6$$

Solve for $y$:

$$y = 3x - 1$$

In slope-intercept form: $y = 3x - 1$.

**Example 3: Writing Equation from Two Points**

Write the equation of the line through $(1, 4)$ and $(3, 10)$.

*Solution:*

First, find the slope:

$$m = \frac{10 - 4}{3 - 1} = \frac{6}{2} = 3$$

Use point-slope form with point $(1, 4)$ and slope 3:

$$y - 4 = 3(x - 1)$$

$$y - 4 = 3x - 3$$

$$y = 3x + 1$$

In slope-intercept form: $y = 3x + 1$.

**Example 4: Converting Between Forms**

Convert $y = -\frac{1}{2}x + 4$ to standard form.

*Solution:*

Multiply both sides by 2 to eliminate fractions:

$$2y = -x + 8$$

Rearrange to standard form:

$$x + 2y = 8$$

**Example 5: Interpreting a Linear Model**

A cell phone plan charges $15 per month for unlimited texting plus $0.10 per data gigabyte used. Write the equation and interpret its parts.

*Solution:*

Let $x$ = gigabytes of data, $y$ = total monthly cost.

$$y = 0.10x + 15$$

Interpretation:
- The slope is $0.10$: each gigabyte costs $0.10$.
- The y-intercept is $15$: even if you use zero data, you pay $15 per month (the base fee).
- If you use 50 GB, the cost is $y = 0.10(50) + 15 = 5 + 15 = 20$ dollars.

## Reasoning Task

**The Business Model**

A photographer charges $150 for a shoot plus $5 per printed photo.

1. Write the equation $C = 5p + 150$ relating total cost to photos, then identify the slope (cost per photo) and y-intercept (session fee).

2. If a customer's budget is $400, how many photos can they order?

3. Suppose a discount changes the price to $4 per photo. How does the slope change, and what does this mean? At what photo count are both models equal in cost?

## Application

**Smartphone Adoption Forecast**

A study shows smartphone adoption was 5% in 2010 and 35% in 2015. Assuming linear growth, find the equation $P = mt + b$ where $t$ = years since 2010.

**Slope:** $m = \frac{35 - 5}{5} = 6$ percent per year.

**Equation:** $P = 6t + 5$

**Predictions:** In 2012 ($t=2$): $P = 17\%$. For 65% adoption: $65 = 6t + 5 \Rightarrow t = 10$, so year 2020.

Linear models work well in a limited range but eventually fail (percentages can't exceed 100%).

## Recap

A linear function is any relationship of the form $y = mx + b$. There are three standard ways to write such equations:

- **Slope-intercept form** ($y = mx + b$) reveals the slope and y-intercept at a glance, ideal for graphing.
- **Point-slope form** ($y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$) is useful when you know a point and the slope.
- **Standard form** ($Ax + By = C$) is useful for systems of equations and other applications.

You can convert freely between these forms. You can write the equation of a line given:
- A slope and y-intercept
- A slope and one point
- Two points

In real-world contexts, a linear equation becomes a linear model: a tool to describe, predict, and understand relationships. The slope tells the rate of change; the intercepts tell you starting values.

Master these three forms, and you have a powerful toolkit for problem-solving in mathematics and beyond.
