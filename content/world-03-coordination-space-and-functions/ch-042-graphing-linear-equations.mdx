## Story Hook

It is 1750, somewhere in rural France. A civil engineer named Marguerite stands at the edge of a vast, muddy plot of land. Her task: build a perfectly straight road through forest and field to connect two towns.

No GPS. No laser level. But she has her notebook and a surveying instrument.

Marguerite picks a starting point and begins to measure. "From this post," she calls to her assistant, "if we go one kilometer east, we must go two kilometers north." She plants a stake. "From here, one kilometer east, two kilometers north again." Another stake.

By the time she has ten stakes planted, the pattern is unmistakable. The stakes form a perfect line.

Later, back in her office, Marguerite writes this rule: "For every kilometer east, rise two kilometers north." In modern notation, this is an equation: $y = 2x$.

She realizes something profound: *The equation is the line. The line is the equation. Draw one and you have the other.*

This is the great discovery of this chapter. An equation in two variables is not just an algebraic puzzle. It is a picture.

## Why It Matters

Most relationships in the real world involve more than one quantity. A company's profit depends on how many items it sells. The distance you've traveled depends on how long you've been driving. The temperature of soup depends on how long it's been cooling.

These relationships often follow rules that, when plotted, form straight lines. Linear relationships are everywhere: in business, science, medicine, engineering.

When you can turn an equation into a graph, you gain intuition. You can see where a line crosses the axes. You can estimate values that aren't explicitly calculated. You can compare two different linear relationships at a glance.

The ability to graph an equation is not a mere arithmetic skill. It is a way of thinking. It is how mathematicians, engineers, and data scientists understand the world.

## Historical Context

The coordinate plane was Descartes's gift, but the practical art of graphing equations developed slowly over the 18th and 19th centuries. As scientists and engineers used mathematics to model reality — to predict planetary motion, design machines, manage agriculture — graphing became essential.

The terms "x-intercept" and "y-intercept" gained currency in the 1800s, particularly in the work of French and German mathematicians who were systematizing algebra and geometry. The idea of setting a variable to zero to find an intercept was so elegant and useful that it became standard practice.

By the early 1900s, graphing was taught in schools as a fundamental mathematical skill. Today, graphing technology (from pencil and paper to computer software) remains one of the most powerful tools for understanding data and relationships.

## Core Concept

A **linear equation in two variables** is an equation of the form:

$$ax + by = c$$

where $a$, $b$, and $c$ are numbers (and $a$ and $b$ are not both zero).

When you solve this equation for $y$, you often get:

$$y = mx + b$$

where $m$ and $b$ are numbers. (We'll explore this form more in the next chapter; for now, think of it as a way to find $y$ given $x$.)

An equation like $y = 2x + 1$ or $2x + 3y = 12$ is a *relationship* between $x$ and $y$. Every ordered pair $(x, y)$ that satisfies the equation is a *solution* to that equation.

The graph of a linear equation is the set of all points $(x, y)$ that satisfy it. And this graph is always a straight line.

### Making a Table of Values

To graph an equation, one reliable method is to:

1. **Choose several values for $x$** (any values you like; zero is often convenient).
2. **Substitute each $x$-value into the equation** to find the corresponding $y$-value.
3. **Write the $(x, y)$ pairs as ordered pairs.**
4. **Plot these points on a coordinate plane.**
5. **Draw a straight line through the points.**

### Using Intercepts

A quicker method is to find the points where the line crosses the axes.

The **x-intercept** is the point where the line crosses the x-axis. At this point, $y = 0$. To find it, set $y = 0$ in the equation and solve for $x$.

The **y-intercept** is the point where the line crosses the y-axis. At this point, $x = 0$. To find it, set $x = 0$ in the equation and solve for $y$.

Two points are enough to draw a line, so if you find both intercepts, you can sketch the graph immediately.

### Special Cases: Horizontal and Vertical Lines

A **horizontal line** has the form $y = k$ for some constant $k$. Every point on this line has the same $y$-coordinate.

- The equation $y = 3$ represents a horizontal line 3 units above the x-axis.
- The equation $y = 0$ is the x-axis itself.

A **vertical line** has the form $x = h$ for some constant $h$. Every point on this line has the same $x$-coordinate.

- The equation $x = -2$ represents a vertical line 2 units to the left of the y-axis.
- The equation $x = 0$ is the y-axis itself.

Vertical lines are tricky because they don't represent functions (more on that later), and they don't have a y-intercept unless they are the y-axis itself.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Linear Equation in Two Variables:** An equation of the form $ax + by = c$, where $a$, $b$, and $c$ are real numbers and $a$ and $b$ are not both zero.

**Solution of an Equation:** An ordered pair $(x, y)$ that makes the equation true when the values are substituted.

**Table of Values:** A systematic list of input (x) and output (y) values for an equation, used to identify points on the graph.

**x-Intercept:** The point where a line crosses the x-axis; found by setting $y = 0$ and solving for $x$. The ordered pair has the form $(a, 0)$.

**y-Intercept:** The point where a line crosses the y-axis; found by setting $x = 0$ and solving for $y$. The ordered pair has the form $(0, b)$.

**Horizontal Line:** A line parallel to the x-axis, represented by the equation $y = k$ for some constant $k$.

**Vertical Line:** A line parallel to the y-axis, represented by the equation $x = h$ for some constant $h$.

**Graph of an Equation:** The set of all points in the coordinate plane whose coordinates satisfy the equation.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Graphing Using a Table of Values**

Graph the equation $y = 2x - 1$.

*Solution:*

Choose $x$-values: $-1, 0, 1, 2, 3$.

Substitute into $y = 2x - 1$:

| $x$ | $y = 2x - 1$ | $(x, y)$ |
|-----|-------------|----------|
| $-1$ | $-3$ | $(-1, -3)$ |
| $0$ | $-1$ | $(0, -1)$ |
| $1$ | $1$ | $(1, 1)$ |
| $2$ | $3$ | $(2, 3)$ |
| $3$ | $5$ | $(3, 5)$ |

Plot these points and draw a straight line through them. The line passes through all points satisfying the equation.

**Example 2: Graphing Using Intercepts**

Graph the equation $2x + 3y = 6$.

*Solution:*

**x-intercept:** Set $y = 0$: $2x + 3(0) = 6 \Rightarrow 2x = 6 \Rightarrow x = 3$.
The x-intercept is $(3, 0)$.

**y-intercept:** Set $x = 0$: $2(0) + 3y = 6 \Rightarrow 3y = 6 \Rightarrow y = 2$.
The y-intercept is $(0, 2)$.

Plot these two points and draw the line. This line completely represents the equation $2x + 3y = 6$.

**Example 3: Horizontal Line**

Graph $y = 4$.

*Solution:*

This equation says: $y$ is always 4, no matter what $x$ is. So $(-5, 4)$, $(0, 4)$, $(3, 4)$, and $(100, 4)$ are all solutions.

The graph is a horizontal line 4 units above the x-axis. It never crosses the x-axis, so there is no x-intercept. The y-intercept is $(0, 4)$.

**Example 4: Vertical Line**

Graph $x = -3$.

*Solution:*

This equation says: $x$ is always $-3$, no matter what $y$ is. So $(-3, -10)$, $(-3, 0)$, $(-3, 5)$, and all other points with $x = -3$ are solutions.

The graph is a vertical line 3 units to the left of the y-axis. It never crosses the y-axis (so no y-intercept) unless the line is the y-axis itself. The x-intercept is $(-3, 0)$.

## Reasoning Task

**The Road Revisited**

Recall Marguerite's road: for every kilometer east, rise two kilometers north.

If we place the origin at her starting point, the equation is $y = 2x$ (where $y$ is kilometers north and $x$ is kilometers east).

1. What is the y-intercept of this equation? What does it mean in the context of the road?

2. Does this equation have an x-intercept (other than the origin)? Why or why not?

3. If Marguerite wants to extend the road into the opposite direction (west and south), does the equation $y = 2x$ still apply? Which quadrants would the road pass through?

4. Suppose a second engineer builds a road with the rule "for every kilometer east, rise one kilometer north" (equation $y = x$). Sketch both roads on the same coordinate plane. Which road is steeper?

## Application

**Tree Growth**

A young oak tree is planted in a garden. A botanist measures its height each year:

| Year | Height (meters) |
|------|---|
| 0 | 2 |
| 1 | 2.5 |
| 2 | 3 |
| 3 | 3.5 |

The height follows a linear pattern. If $x$ is the number of years and $y$ is the height in meters:

1. What is the equation relating $x$ and $y$?
2. How tall will the tree be after 5 years?
3. How many years until the tree reaches 5 meters?

*Solution:*

The tree grows 0.5 meters per year. At year 0, the height is 2 meters. So:

$$y = 0.5x + 2$$

or equivalently

$$y = \frac{1}{2}x + 2$$

For year 5: $y = 0.5(5) + 2 = 2.5 + 2 = 4.5$ meters.

For height 5 meters: $5 = 0.5x + 2 \Rightarrow 3 = 0.5x \Rightarrow x = 6$ years.

## Recap

A linear equation in two variables describes a relationship between two quantities. By creating a table of values or finding the x- and y-intercepts, we can plot points and sketch the graph, which is always a straight line.

The intercept method is often the quickest: set one variable to zero, find the other, plot the point, and repeat. Two points determine a line.

Horizontal lines have the form $y = k$; vertical lines have the form $x = h$. These special cases help us recognize when an equation represents a function and when it doesn't.

Graphing is the bridge between algebra and geometry, between equation and picture. Master it, and you can understand any linear relationship.
