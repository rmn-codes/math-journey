## Story Hook

The night before a duel he expected to lose, Évariste Galois sat at his desk and wrote frantically. He was 20 years old, a revolutionary and a brilliant mathematician, about to be killed in a dispute over a woman. With hours remaining, he scribbled down ideas that would take mathematicians 60 years to fully understand.

Galois was trying to solve an ancient problem: Why do quadratic equations have a formula? Why do cubic and quartic equations have formulas, even though they are complicated? And why—this was the mystery—does there exist no formula for quintic (fifth-degree) equations?

Paolo Ruffini had shown it was impossible in 1799. Niels Henrik Abel independently proved it again in 1824. But why? What was the deep reason?

Galois found the answer by studying symmetry.

He considered the symmetries of the roots of a polynomial. For a quadratic $x^2 + px + q = 0$ with roots $r$ and $s$, there is a symmetry: you can swap $r$ and $s$ and the equation looks the same. This symmetry structure, Galois realized, held the key.

For a general polynomial of degree 5 or higher, the symmetries of the roots form a group that is too complicated. There is no way to "unravel" it into a formula. The abstract structure of symmetry determines whether a solution formula exists.

Galois called these systems of symmetries *groups*. He barely published anything in his lifetime. After his death, mathematicians finally understood. Group theory became the language of symmetry.

Today, group theory unifies physics. Physicists use group theory to understand the symmetries of particles. The Standard Model of particle physics is built on group theory. Every known particle, every known force, is a consequence of the symmetries of the quantum fields.

The dying insight of a 20-year-old revolutionary turned out to describe the deepest laws of nature.

## Why It Matters

Symmetry is everywhere. A snowflake has six-fold rotational symmetry. A circle has infinitely many symmetries. A molecule's chemical properties are determined by its symmetry. A crystal's structure is encoded by its symmetry group.

But symmetry is not just an aesthetic property. Emmy Noether proved in 1915 that *every symmetry corresponds to a conservation law*. If a physical system is symmetric under time translation (it works the same way tomorrow as today), then energy is conserved. If it is symmetric under spatial translation (it works the same way here as there), then momentum is conserved. If it is rotationally symmetric, then angular momentum is conserved.

This means that the deepest laws of physics—conservation of energy, conservation of momentum, conservation of angular momentum—are not separate fundamental laws. They are consequences of symmetries. Physics is the study of symmetry.

## Historical Context

The concept of symmetry goes back to ancient geometry. A square has eight symmetries: four rotations (including the identity) and four reflections. These can be composed (a rotation followed by a reflection), and each can be reversed.

But for thousands of years, symmetry was a descriptive property. People noticed it and appreciated it. Only in the 19th century did mathematicians begin to study the structure of symmetries themselves.

Carl Friedrich Gauss and others worked with permutations—rearrangements of a set. Joseph-Louis Lagrange, in the 1770s, noted that permutations of roots of polynomials had a special structure. Augustin-Louis Cauchy developed the theory of permutations further in the 1840s.

But it was Galois who made the revolutionary leap. He showed that the *group of symmetries of a polynomial's roots* determines whether the polynomial can be solved by radicals (by a formula). If the group has a certain structure (called "solvable"), then a formula exists. If not, no formula can exist—it is a mathematical fact, not a limitation of human cleverness.

Galois's ideas were published posthumously by Joseph Liouville in 1846. Over the next 30 years, mathematicians like Camille Jordan worked to understand and extend the theory. By the 1890s, group theory was established as a central tool in algebra.

Then physicists discovered something stunning: the laws of nature exhibit symmetry. In 1905, Einstein used symmetry under Lorentz transformations to derive special relativity. In 1915, Emmy Noether proved that continuous symmetries correspond to conservation laws. In the 20th century, physicists realized that the interactions of particles are determined by gauge symmetries—abstract symmetries of quantum fields.

Group theory became the language of physics.

## Core Concept

**A symmetry** is a transformation that leaves an object unchanged. For example:
- Rotating a square by 90° is a symmetry.
- Reflecting a square across a diagonal is a symmetry.
- Rotating a circle by any amount is a symmetry.

**A group** is a set $G$ with an operation $*$ (usually multiplication or composition) that satisfies four axioms:

1. **Closure:** If $a, b \in G$, then $a * b \in G$.
2. **Associativity:** $(a * b) * c = a * (b * c)$ for all $a, b, c \in G$.
3. **Identity:** There exists an element $e \in G$ such that $e * a = a * e = a$ for all $a \in G$.
4. **Inverses:** For each $a \in G$, there exists $a^{-1} \in G$ such that $a * a^{-1} = a^{-1} * a = e$.

**Example: The symmetries of a square.**
The square has 8 symmetries:
- Four rotations: identity (0°), 90°, 180°, 270°.
- Four reflections: across vertical, horizontal, and two diagonal axes.

These form a group called the dihedral group $D_4$. The operation is composition: apply one symmetry, then another. The identity is "do nothing." The inverse of a 90° rotation is a 270° rotation. Closure holds: composing any two symmetries gives another symmetry.

**Example: Integers under addition ($\mathbb{Z}, +$).**
- Closure: $3 + 5 = 8 \in \mathbb{Z}$.
- Associativity: $(1 + 2) + 3 = 1 + (2 + 3) = 6$.
- Identity: $0$ (since $0 + a = a$).
- Inverses: For $a \in \mathbb{Z}$, the inverse is $-a$ (since $a + (-a) = 0$).

**Cyclic groups:** A cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}_n$ is the set $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots, n-1\}$ with addition modulo $n$. For example, $\mathbb{Z}_5 = \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$ with addition mod 5. In $\mathbb{Z}_5$, we have $3 + 4 = 7 \equiv 2 \pmod{5}$.

**Abelian vs. non-Abelian groups:**
- **Abelian (commutative):** $a * b = b * a$ for all $a, b \in G$. Example: $\mathbb{Z}$ under addition.
- **Non-Abelian:** There exist $a, b \in G$ such that $a * b \neq b * a$. Example: The symmetries of a square; a rotation followed by a reflection is different from a reflection followed by a rotation.

**Galois theory** tells us why quintic equations have no general formula. The group of symmetries of a quintic polynomial's roots is (generically) the full symmetric group $S_5$, which permutes 5 objects in all possible ways. This group is *not solvable*—it cannot be decomposed into a hierarchy of simple groups—and therefore no formula exists to solve the polynomial by radicals.

**Noether's theorem** states: Every continuous symmetry of a physical system corresponds to a conservation law.

Examples:
- Time-translation symmetry $\implies$ conservation of energy.
- Spatial-translation symmetry $\implies$ conservation of momentum.
- Rotational symmetry $\implies$ conservation of angular momentum.
- Gauge symmetry (internal symmetry) $\implies$ conservation of charge.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

- **Symmetry:** A transformation that leaves an object or system unchanged.
- **Group:** A set with an operation satisfying closure, associativity, identity, and inverses.
- **Closure:** The set is closed under the operation; combining any two elements gives an element in the set.
- **Associativity:** The operation satisfies $(a * b) * c = a * (b * c)$.
- **Identity element:** The element $e$ such that $e * a = a * e = a$ for all $a$.
- **Inverse element:** For each $a$, an element $a^{-1}$ such that $a * a^{-1} = e$.
- **Order of a group:** The number of elements in the group.
- **Abelian group:** A group where $a * b = b * a$ (commutative).
- **Non-Abelian group:** A group where the operation is not commutative.
- **Cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}_n$:** The group of integers modulo $n$ under addition.
- **Dihedral group $D_n$:** The group of symmetries of a regular $n$-gon.
- **Solvable group:** A group that can be decomposed into a hierarchy of simple groups.
- **Gauge symmetry:** An internal symmetry of a quantum field that determines the interactions of particles.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Verifying Group Axioms**
Show that $(\mathbb{Z}_4, +) = \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ under addition modulo 4 is a group.

Solution:
- **Closure:** For any $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}_4$, their sum mod 4 is in $\mathbb{Z}_4$. E.g., $3 + 2 = 5 \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$.
- **Associativity:** Addition is associative.
- **Identity:** $0$ is the identity: $0 + a = a$ for all $a$.
- **Inverses:** Each element has an inverse: $0^{-1} = 0$, $1^{-1} = 3$, $2^{-1} = 2$, $3^{-1} = 1$. (Check: $3 + 1 = 4 \equiv 0 \pmod{4}$.)

Therefore, $(\mathbb{Z}_4, +)$ is a group. It is cyclic (generated by 1) and Abelian.

**Example 2: Symmetries of a Rectangle**
List all symmetries of a rectangle (not a square) and verify they form a group.

Solution:
- Identity: do nothing.
- Rotation by 180°: swap opposite vertices.
- Reflection across the vertical axis: flip left to right.
- Reflection across the horizontal axis: flip top to bottom.

Total: 4 symmetries. These form the Klein four-group $V_4$.

Check closure: Compose any two symmetries; the result is another symmetry.
- Identity composed with anything gives that thing.
- Rotation by 180° composed with itself is the identity.
- The two reflections composed give the 180° rotation.
- Reflection composed with 180° rotation gives the other reflection.

The group is Abelian and has order 4.

**Example 3: Cyclic Group Generator**
In $\mathbb{Z}_5$ under addition mod 5, show that 1 is a generator (meaning powers of 1 generate all elements).

Solution:
- $1^0 = 0$ (identity)
- $1^1 = 1$
- $1^2 = 1 + 1 = 2$
- $1^3 = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3$
- $1^4 = 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 4$
- $1^5 = 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 0$ (back to identity)

So the powers of 1 give all five elements. Therefore, 1 generates $\mathbb{Z}_5$, and $\mathbb{Z}_5$ is cyclic.

**Example 4: Noether's Theorem**
A system invariant under spatial translation has conserved momentum.

Explanation:
- Spatial translation: shifting the entire system by a distance $\delta x$ does not change the physics.
- By Noether's theorem, this symmetry implies the total momentum is conserved.
- This is why an isolated object in space maintains constant velocity (Newton's first law).

## Application

**Particle Physics:** The Standard Model of particle physics is built on gauge symmetries. The electromagnetic force arises from a $U(1)$ symmetry. The weak and strong nuclear forces arise from $SU(2)$ and $SU(3)$ symmetries. Each symmetry determines the particles and their interactions. When physicists discovered the Higgs boson in 2012, they were confirming a prediction made by symmetry principles decades earlier.

**Crystal Structure:** The atomic arrangement in a crystal exhibits symmetry. There are exactly 230 distinct crystallographic space groups—symmetries that can be realized by atomic arrangements in 3D. The properties of a crystal (optical, electrical, mechanical) are determined by its symmetry group. X-ray crystallography uses symmetry to deduce crystal structures.

**Quantum Mechanics:** In quantum mechanics, the state of a particle is a vector in a Hilbert space, and symmetries act as unitary operators. The rotation group $SO(3)$ describes spatial rotations. Particles are classified by irreducible representations of symmetry groups. For instance, electrons have spin-1/2, which corresponds to a specific representation of the rotation group.

## Recap

We learned that a group is an abstract algebraic structure: a set with an operation satisfying closure, associativity, identity, and inverses. We studied examples like integers under addition, cyclic groups like $\mathbb{Z}_n$, and the dihedral groups describing symmetries of geometric figures. We distinguished Abelian groups (commutative) from non-Abelian groups (non-commutative). We learned Galois's insight: the group of symmetries of a polynomial's roots determines whether the polynomial can be solved by a formula. We learned Noether's theorem: every continuous symmetry of a physical system corresponds to a conservation law. These principles unify mathematics and physics: the deepest laws of nature are consequences of symmetry.
