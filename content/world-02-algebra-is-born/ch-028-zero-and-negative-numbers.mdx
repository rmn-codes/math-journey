# Zero and Negative Numbers

## Story Hook

It is 7th-century India. A merchant named Harsha sits in his counting house, tallying accounts with a scribe named Devendra.

"The rice account," Devendra says, "we received 50 sacks from the farmer. We sold 50 sacks to the baker. How many sacks remain?"

"Zero," Harsha says without hesitation.

Devendra writes it down. But then he pauses. "How do you write zero?" he asks. "We have no symbol for it."

In ancient Greece, Rome, Egypt, and Babylon, "nothing" was not a number. It was the *absence* of a number. You did not write it. You left a blank space, which caused endless confusion.

Then Devendra does something radical. He draws a small circle on the tablet. "I will use this symbol to mean 'nothing.'"

Harsha stares at it. "But what if I owe 50 coins and I have given none back? Do I have zero debts, or do I have a debt of 50?"

This question—how to represent what you *owe* versus what you *own*—will lead to an even stranger invention: negative numbers. Numbers less than zero. Numbers that represent debt, loss, or owing.

These ideas seemed absurd. And yet they would transform mathematics forever.

## Why It Matters

Without zero, numbers have no natural resting place. Without negative numbers, you cannot represent debt, loss, or direction. Your mathematics is forever bounded by "what you have."

Think about it: if you can only use positive numbers, how do you solve $x + 5 = 2$? The answer is a number less than zero—a negative number. Without it, you are stuck.

With zero and negatives, the entire number system becomes *complete*. You can move freely backward and forward. You can represent any situation: profit or loss, height above or below sea level, temperature above or below zero.

And practically speaking: business, science, and engineering all require the ability to count debt, downward motion, and deficit. A modern economy would be impossible without negative numbers.

## Historical Context

The story of zero and negative numbers is one of the strangest in mathematics. For thousands of years, civilizations computed without them. Then, within a few centuries, the idea emerged—simultaneously, in different parts of the world.

The Babylonians used a symbol (two small wedges) as a placeholder in the middle of numbers, like 305. But they did not think of it as a number itself. It was a marker.

In ancient China, around the 1st century BCE, mathematicians and accountants began using red rods to represent positive quantities and black rods to represent debts. This was not yet "negative numbers," but it was thinking about debt and credit as opposites. A Chinese mathematical text, the "Jiuzhang Suanshu" (Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art), describes how to add and subtract with red and black rods.

But the revolution came from India. Indian mathematicians, particularly Brahmagupta (598–668 CE) and later Bhaskara II (1114–1185 CE), did something previously unthinkable: they treated zero as a *number*—something you could add, subtract, and multiply. They also developed rules for negative numbers. A negative number, they reasoned, could represent a debt or a loss.

By the 9th century, Islamic scholars like al-Khwarizmi carried these ideas from India to the Arabic world and beyond. Al-Khwarizmi's work treated negative numbers as legitimate mathematical objects, not just accounting tricks.

Europe was slow to accept these ideas. Even in the 1500s and 1600s, European mathematicians were suspicious of negatives and called them "false" or "imaginary" numbers. It took centuries of practical use in accounting and science before they were fully accepted.

The irony is this: what seemed strange and impossible in 600 CE became so normal by the modern era that we forget how revolutionary it was.

## Core Concept

**Zero** is the number that represents nothing, or a balance between opposites. It is neither positive nor negative. Zero can be added to any number without changing it: $n + 0 = n$ for all numbers $n$.

Mathematically, zero is the *additive identity*. Every other number has an additive inverse—a partner that, when added to it, gives zero. The additive inverse of 7 is $-7$, because $7 + (-7) = 0$.

**Negative numbers** are numbers less than zero. They represent debt, loss, moving backward, or being below a reference point. Negative 5, written as $-5$, means "5 units in the opposite direction from positive 5."

Negative numbers seem strange because we cannot "hold" them in our hands. We cannot have $-5$ apples. But we *can* owe 5 apples. We *can* be 5 meters below sea level. We *can* lose $5 from our account.

The **number line** is a way to visualize all integers—the whole numbers and their negatives—arranged in order:

$$\ldots -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

On the number line, moving right means adding (going toward more positive). Moving left means subtracting (going toward more negative).

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Key Terms

**Zero**
The number 0, which represents nothing or a balance. Adding zero to any number leaves it unchanged: $n + 0 = n$.

**Negative number**
A number less than zero. Written with a minus sign, like $-3$. Negative numbers can represent debt, loss, or direction opposite to positive.

**Integer**
Any whole number, including zero and all negative whole numbers: $\ldots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \ldots$

**Additive inverse**
Two numbers are additive inverses if they add to zero. The additive inverse of $a$ is $-a$, because $a + (-a) = 0$.

**Absolute value**
The distance a number is from zero on the number line, always non-negative. Written as $|a|$. For example, $|-5| = 5$ and $|5| = 5$.

**Number line**
A visual representation of numbers arranged in order, with zero at the center, positive numbers to the right, and negative numbers to the left.

### The Number Line and Movement

The number line is the key to understanding integers. Imagine you stand at 0 on the line.

- To add a positive number, move *right*.
- To subtract a positive number (or add a negative number), move *left*.
- To add a negative number, move *left*.
- To subtract a negative number (or add its opposite), move *right*.

**Example:** $3 + (-5)$

Start at 3. Add $-5$ means move left 5 units. You land on $-2$.

$$3 + (-5) = -2$$

**Example:** $-4 - (-2)$

Start at $-4$. Subtract $-2$ means move right 2 units (subtract a negative = add a positive). You land on $-2$.

$$-4 - (-2) = -4 + 2 = -2$$

### Adding and Subtracting Integers

**Same sign:** Add the absolute values and keep the sign.
- $5 + 3 = 8$
- $(-5) + (-3) = -(5 + 3) = -8$

**Different signs:** Subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger, and use the sign of the number with the larger absolute value.
- $5 + (-3) = 5 - 3 = 2$
- $(-5) + 3 = -(5 - 3) = -2$

**Subtraction:** Subtracting is the same as adding the opposite.
$$a - b = a + (-b)$$

So $3 - 8 = 3 + (-8) = -5$.

### Multiplication and Division with Negatives

**Positive × Positive = Positive**
$$4 \times 3 = 12$$

**Positive × Negative = Negative**
$$4 \times (-3) = -12$$

**Negative × Negative = Positive**
$$(-4) \times (-3) = 12$$

The rule: if the signs are the same, the result is positive. If the signs are different, the result is negative.

Division follows the same rule:
$$\frac{-12}{3} = -4$$
$$\frac{-12}{-3} = 4$$

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: A Merchant's Debt Account

A merchant starts with 30 gold coins. He spends 50 gold coins on goods. How many coins does he have now?

**Solution**

Starting amount: 30 coins.
Spending 50 coins is the same as adding $-50$:
$$30 + (-50) = 30 - 50 = -20$$

The merchant has $-20$ coins—meaning he is in debt by 20 coins. He owes 20 coins to his supplier.

### Example 2: Temperature Change

The temperature in a city is $-5°C$ in the morning. By midday, it has risen by 12 degrees. What is the midday temperature?

**Solution**

Temperature rises by 12 means add 12:
$$-5 + 12 = 7$$

The midday temperature is $7°C$.

### Example 3: A Number Line Subtraction

Calculate $-3 - (-6)$ using the number line.

**Solution**

Start at $-3$. Subtract $-6$ means add 6 (subtract a negative is add a positive). Move right 6 units:
$$-3 - (-6) = -3 + 6 = 3$$

We land on 3.

Alternatively:
$$-3 - (-6) = -3 + 6 = 6 - 3 = 3$$

## Application

Negative numbers are everywhere in the modern world, so common we forget they were once revolutionary.

**Finance:** Banks use negative numbers daily. An overdraft is a negative balance. A company's debt is a negative asset. When you look at a financial statement and see "Loss: $(50,000)," that is a negative 50,000.

**Science:** Temperatures below zero exist everywhere. In Celsius, water freezes at 0°C, so February in Moscow at $-20°C$ is common. In physics, elevation above or below sea level uses positive and negative numbers. A submarine at 200 meters depth is at $-200$ meters elevation.

**Engineering:** When designers build bridges, they track load (positive) and stress (which can be negative). Electrical engineers work with positive and negative charges. Carpenters measure above and below reference lines.

**Games and Sports:** In golf, a score of $-5$ (5 under par) is better than $+3$ (3 over par). In poker, a player's running total can go negative.

Without negative numbers, all these fields would be impossible.

## Recap

- **Zero** is the additive identity: adding it to any number leaves the number unchanged.
- **Negative numbers** represent debt, loss, or direction opposite to positive quantities.
- An **integer** is any whole number, positive, negative, or zero.
- The **number line** visualizes all integers in order.
- The **additive inverse** of any number $a$ is $-a$; they add to zero.
- Addition and subtraction of integers can be understood as movement on the number line.
- Negative numbers were developed in India and spread through the Islamic world, but were not widely accepted in Europe until centuries later.
