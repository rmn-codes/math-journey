## Story Hook

Around 250 CE, in Alexandria, Egypt, lived a mathematician named Diophantus. He's famous for leaving behind a puzzle inscribed on his tombstone—a puzzle *about his own life*, written as an algebraic equation.

The puzzle goes like this:

"Diophantus passed one-sixth of his life in boyhood, one-twelfth in youth, and one-seventh as a bachelor. Five years after his marriage, he had a son. The son lived half as long as his father. After his son's death, Diophantus lived four more years."

How long did Diophantus live?

Today, we'd write this as a linear equation and solve it systematically. Back then, Diophantus had to work through it by hand, inventing much of what we'll learn in this chapter as he went.

Let $x$ be Diophantus's age:
$$\frac{x}{6} + \frac{x}{12} + \frac{x}{7} + 5 + \frac{x}{2} + 4 = x$$

We don't need to be ancient mathematicians. We have a systematic method. And we're about to learn it.

## Why It Matters

Solving equations is the central skill of algebra. Every time you use mathematics to answer a real question—from business problems to physics to medicine—you're setting up and solving an equation.

- **A pharmacist** needs to solve: "If a dose is 2.5 mg per kg of body weight, how much medicine for a 70 kg patient?" → $\text{dose} = 2.5 \times 70$
- **An engineer** needs to solve: "If a bridge can support 50,000 pounds per beam, and we have 8 beams, how much total weight?" → Still multiplication, but the reverse problem: "How many beams do we need?" is solving an equation.
- **A parent** needs to solve: "If I have $100 and need $15 gas money, how much can I spend on groceries?" → $100 - 15 = x$

Every one of these is solving an equation. Master this chapter and you can answer almost any "how many?" question the world throws at you.

## Historical Context

Diophantus lived in the 3rd century, centuries before al-Khwarizmi systematized algebra. Yet Diophantus was already solving complex equations—without notation, without symbols, just pure thought and words.

Al-Khwarizmi, writing in 820 CE, gave us the systematic methods. He showed that every equation can be solved by a series of steps:
1. Simplify both sides
2. Get all unknowns on one side
3. Get all constants on the other side
4. Isolate the variable

This method works because of the balance principle. Each step maintains equality while bringing us closer to the answer.

Later mathematicians in Persia, the Islamic world, and Europe refined these techniques. By the 1600s, equations were being solved with remarkable speed and confidence. The methods hadn't changed—the *notation* had improved, making it all clearer.

That notation is what we'll use now.

## Core Concept

A **linear equation** is an equation where the variable appears to the first power only (no $x^2$, no $x^3$, etc.). The graph of a linear equation is a straight line.

Examples of linear equations:
- $x + 5 = 12$
- $3x = 21$
- $2x + 7 = 15$
- $4x - 3 = 2x + 9$

Examples that are NOT linear:
- $x^2 + 2 = 6$ (second power)
- $\frac{1}{x} = 5$ (variable in denominator)
- $2^x = 8$ (variable in exponent)

To **solve** a linear equation means to find the value of the variable that makes the equation true.

The goal is to **isolate the variable**—get it alone on one side of the equals sign, with all numbers on the other side.

### Strategy: Reverse the Operations

Think of operations as a journey:
- Start with $x$
- Apply operations: multiply by 2, add 5
- You get: $2x + 5 = 13$

To reverse the journey and find $x$, undo operations in reverse order:
- Start with 13
- Undo the "add 5" by subtracting 5 → get 8
- Undo the "multiply by 2" by dividing by 2 → get 4
- So $x = 4$

### The Distributive Property

Sometimes you'll see: $3(x + 2) = 18$

The distributive property says: $a(b + c) = ab + ac$

So: $3(x + 2) = 3x + 6$

Now solve: $3x + 6 = 18$ by subtracting 6, then dividing by 3.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Linear Equation**: An equation where the variable appears to the first power only.

**Solution**: The value of the variable that makes the equation true.

**Isolate**: To get the variable alone on one side.

**Distributive Property**: $a(b + c) = ab + ac$ and $a(b - c) = ab - ac$.

### One-Step Equations

**Form**: $x + a = b$ or $ax = b$

**Method**: Use the inverse operation once.

**Example**: Solve $x + 7 = 12$
- Subtract 7 from both sides: $x = 12 - 7 = 5$
- Check: $5 + 7 = 12$ ✓

**Example**: Solve $3x = 24$
- Divide both sides by 3: $x = 8$
- Check: $3(8) = 24$ ✓

### Two-Step Equations

**Form**: $ax + b = c$

**Method**:
1. Add or subtract to remove the constant from the variable side
2. Multiply or divide to isolate the variable

**Example**: Solve $2x + 5 = 17$
- Subtract 5: $2x = 12$
- Divide by 2: $x = 6$
- Check: $2(6) + 5 = 12 + 5 = 17$ ✓

### Multi-Step Equations

**Form**: May require distributive property, combining like terms, variables on both sides

**Strategy**:
1. Distribute if needed
2. Combine like terms on each side
3. Get variables on one side, constants on the other
4. Isolate the variable

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Solve $x - 8 = 3$**

Add 8 to both sides:
$$x - 8 + 8 = 3 + 8$$
$$x = 11$$

Check: $11 - 8 = 3$ ✓

**Example 2: Solve $\frac{x}{4} = 6$**

Multiply both sides by 4:
$$4 \cdot \frac{x}{4} = 4 \cdot 6$$
$$x = 24$$

Check: $\frac{24}{4} = 6$ ✓

**Example 3: Solve $5x - 3 = 22$**

Add 3 to both sides:
$$5x - 3 + 3 = 22 + 3$$
$$5x = 25$$

Divide both sides by 5:
$$x = 5$$

Check: $5(5) - 3 = 25 - 3 = 22$ ✓

**Example 4: Solve $2(x + 3) = 16$**

Use the distributive property:
$$2x + 6 = 16$$

Subtract 6:
$$2x = 10$$

Divide by 2:
$$x = 5$$

Check: $2(5 + 3) = 2(8) = 16$ ✓

**Example 5: Solve $3x + 5 = 2x + 12$**

Subtract $2x$ from both sides:
$$3x - 2x + 5 = 2x - 2x + 12$$
$$x + 5 = 12$$

Subtract 5:
$$x = 7$$

Check: $3(7) + 5 = 21 + 5 = 26$ and $2(7) + 12 = 14 + 12 = 26$ ✓

**Example 6: Solve the Diophantus puzzle (simplified version)**

If Diophantus lived $x$ years, and the equation is:
$$\frac{x}{6} + \frac{x}{12} + \frac{x}{7} + 5 + \frac{x}{2} + 4 = x$$

This is complex (fractions!), but the principle is the same: isolate $x$.

Combining the fractions on the left (common denominator is 84):
$$\frac{14x + 7x + 12x + 42x}{84} + 9 = x$$
$$\frac{75x}{84} + 9 = x$$

Subtract $\frac{75x}{84}$ and solve (details omitted):
$$x = 84$$

Diophantus lived 84 years.

## Application

**Real-World Scenario: Cell Phone Plans**

Company A charges $30 per month plus $0.10 per text.
Company B charges $50 per month with unlimited texts.

When are the companies equally expensive?

Let $t$ be the number of texts per month.

Company A: $30 + 0.10t$
Company B: $50$

Set them equal:
$$30 + 0.10t = 50$$

Solve:
$$0.10t = 20$$
$$t = 200$$

After 200 texts, Company A and Company B cost the same. This is how consumers decide: set up equations, solve them, compare.

## Recap

A **linear equation** is solved by isolating the variable through a series of inverse operations. The order matters: undo addition/subtraction first (to isolate terms with the variable), then undo multiplication/division.

Always **check your solution** by substituting it back into the original equation. Both sides should equal.

Key techniques:
- **Distributive property**: Expand $a(b + c)$ before solving
- **Combine like terms**: Simplify each side before applying inverse operations
- **Variables on both sides**: Move all variable terms to one side, all constants to the other
- **Fractions**: Either multiply everything by the common denominator at the start, or work through carefully

Solving equations is a skill that improves with practice. The more you do, the faster you'll recognize patterns and spot shortcuts. But the fundamental principle never changes: balance. Keep both sides equal, and you'll always find the solution.
