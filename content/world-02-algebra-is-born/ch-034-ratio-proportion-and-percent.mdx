## Story Hook

In 1095 CE, a Moroccan scholar named Al-Biruni was asked a nearly impossible question: "How large is the Earth?"

Nobody could travel all the way around it. Nobody could measure it directly. But Al-Biruni used a technique that would seem like magic—and yet it's based on the simplest idea in mathematics: scaling.

He measured the angle of the sun at two different locations. He knew the distance between those locations. From the angle, he could determine what fraction of Earth's circumference the distance represented. Then he used a proportion:

$$\frac{\text{distance}}{\text{full circumference}} = \frac{\text{angle measured}}{\text{full circle}}$$

He set up a ratio, solved a proportion, and calculated Earth's circumference to within 0.5% of the true value—using nothing but geometry and the principle of scaling.

This is the power of proportions. They let you scale *any* measurement up or down. They're the reason you can enlarge a photograph without distortion, scale a recipe for 300 people instead of 12, or measure something impossible.

---

## Why It Matters

Ratios and proportions are everywhere:
- **Recipes**: Scale ingredients for different serving sizes
- **Maps**: 1 inch on the map represents 10 miles in reality
- **Medicine**: Doses are often given per kilogram of body weight
- **Finance**: Interest rates, discounts, profits are often expressed as percents
- **Photography**: Aspect ratios determine whether your image looks right
- **Statistics**: Survey results ("52% of voters") are fundamentally percentages

If you understand ratios, proportions, and percents, you can make sense of most of the numerical claims the world throws at you. You can check whether a sale is actually a good deal. You can scale a recipe without guessing. You can understand why two similar triangles have the same angles. You can calculate a tip.

These aren't abstract skills. They're the mathematics of living.

## Historical Context

The ancient Greeks understood ratios deeply. Euclid's *Elements* (c. 300 BCE) devoted entire books to proportions and scaling. The Greeks noticed that if two shapes have the same proportions—the same ratios between their sides—then they look identical, just bigger or smaller. This is similar triangles, one of the most beautiful ideas in mathematics.

Islamic mathematicians, particularly in the 9th–12th centuries, expanded these ideas. Al-Biruni didn't just understand proportions theoretically—he used them to measure the Earth. This is mathematics applied to knowledge itself.

In medieval Europe, merchants used proportions constantly. A trader in Venice needed to scale recipes for spice mixtures. An architect in Florence needed to scale a design from a small model to a massive cathedral. Proportions are how the real world works.

Then came commerce and taxation, which introduced the percent. "Per centum" means "per hundred," a natural unit for comparing quantities when you don't have common denominators. A tax of 10% makes immediate sense: 10 per 100, or 1 per 10. Percentages became the language of finance, and they remain so today.

## Core Concept

A **ratio** compares two quantities. It answers the question: "How much of one thing compared to how much of another?"

A ratio can be written three ways:
- As a fraction: $\frac{3}{5}$
- With a colon: $3:5$
- In words: "3 to 5"

All three mean the same thing: three of something for every five of something else.

**Equivalent ratios** are different fractions that represent the same comparison:
$$\frac{2}{3} = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{6}{9}$$

A **unit rate** is a ratio where the denominator is 1:
- "50 miles per hour" is the ratio $\frac{50 \text{ miles}}{1 \text{ hour}}$
- "3 dollars per pound" is the ratio $\frac{3 \text{ dollars}}{1 \text{ pound}}$

A **proportion** is an equation stating that two ratios are equal:
$$\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$$

This reads: "$a$ is to $b$ as $c$ is to $d$."

A **percent** is a ratio where the denominator is always 100:
$$\text{percent} = \frac{\text{number}}{100}$$

So: 25% = $\frac{25}{100}$ = $0.25$

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Ratio**: A comparison of two quantities, written as $\frac{a}{b}$ or $a:b$.

**Equivalent Ratios**: Ratios that represent the same comparison (same value when simplified).

**Unit Rate**: A ratio with 1 in the denominator.

**Proportion**: An equation stating two ratios are equal: $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$.

**Cross-Multiplication**: In a proportion $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$, multiply diagonally: $ad = bc$. This gives you an equation to solve.

**Percent**: A ratio where the denominator is 100; "per hundred."

### Solving Proportions

If $\frac{3}{5} = \frac{x}{20}$, use cross-multiplication:

$$3 \times 20 = 5 \times x$$
$$60 = 5x$$
$$x = 12$$

### Converting Percents

**Percent to Decimal**: Divide by 100
- $25\% = 0.25$
- $150\% = 1.50$

**Decimal to Percent**: Multiply by 100
- $0.75 = 75\%$
- $0.03 = 3\%$

**Percent to Fraction**: Write as $\frac{\text{percent}}{100}$ and simplify
- $25\% = \frac{25}{100} = \frac{1}{4}$
- $60\% = \frac{60}{100} = \frac{3}{5}$

### Three Types of Percent Problems

**Type 1: Find the percent of a number**
- "What is 20% of 50?" → $0.20 \times 50 = 10$

**Type 2: Find what percent one number is of another**
- "What percent is 12 of 40?" → $\frac{12}{40} = 0.30 = 30\%$

**Type 3: Find the whole, given a percent**
- "12 is 20% of what number?" → $12 = 0.20 \times x$ → $x = 60$

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Simplify the ratio 12:18**

Write as a fraction and simplify:
$$\frac{12}{18} = \frac{2}{3}$$

So the ratio is $2:3$.

**Example 2: Find the unit rate: 300 miles in 5 hours**

$$\frac{300 \text{ miles}}{5 \text{ hours}} = \frac{60 \text{ miles}}{1 \text{ hour}}$$

The unit rate is 60 miles per hour.

**Example 3: Solve the proportion $\frac{4}{7} = \frac{x}{28}$**

Cross-multiply:
$$4 \times 28 = 7 \times x$$
$$112 = 7x$$
$$x = 16$$

Check: $\frac{4}{7} = \frac{16}{28} = \frac{4}{7}$ ✓

**Example 4: A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for 12 cookies. How much flour for 30 cookies?**

Set up a proportion:
$$\frac{2 \text{ cups}}{12 \text{ cookies}} = \frac{x \text{ cups}}{30 \text{ cookies}}$$

Cross-multiply:
$$2 \times 30 = 12 \times x$$
$$60 = 12x$$
$$x = 5$$

You need 5 cups of flour for 30 cookies.

**Example 5: What is 15% of 80?**

Convert 15% to decimal: $15\% = 0.15$

Multiply: $0.15 \times 80 = 12$

15% of 80 is 12.

**Example 6: A store sells 200 items. 45 of them are on sale. What percent are on sale?**

Set up the ratio:
$$\frac{45}{200} = \frac{x}{100}$$

Cross-multiply:
$$45 \times 100 = 200 \times x$$
$$4500 = 200x$$
$$x = 22.5$$

22.5% are on sale.

**Example 7: A book is on sale for 30% off. The original price was $40. What is the sale price?**

First, find 30% of $40:
$$0.30 \times 40 = 12$$

The discount is $12.

Sale price: $40 - 12 = 28$

The sale price is $28.

**Example 8: Al-Biruni's Earth Measurement (Simplified)**

At one location, the sun is directly overhead (angle = 0°).
At another location 100 km away, the sun's angle from vertical is about 1.2°.

If 100 km corresponds to 1.2° of Earth's circumference:
$$\frac{100 \text{ km}}{1.2°} = \frac{\text{circumference}}{360°}$$

Cross-multiply and solve:
$$100 \times 360 = 1.2 \times \text{circumference}$$
$$36000 = 1.2 \times \text{circumference}$$
$$\text{circumference} = 30,000 \text{ km}$$

(The actual circumference is about 40,075 km, so this is remarkably close!)

## Application

**Real-World Scenario: Sale and Markup Calculations**

A store buys a jacket wholesale for $40.

**Scenario 1**: They mark it up 50% to sell it. What's the selling price?
- Markup = 50% of $40 = $0.50 \times 40 = $20
- Selling price = $40 + $20 = $60

**Scenario 2**: Later, they put it on sale for 30% off the selling price. What's the final price?
- Discount = 30% of $60 = $0.30 \times 60 = $18
- Final price = $60 - $18 = $42

The store still makes a profit even after the sale! This is how retail works: markups and discounts are ratios and percents.

## Recap

**Ratios** compare two quantities. **Proportions** say two ratios are equal. **Cross-multiplication** is the method to solve proportions.

**Unit rates** make comparisons easy: "miles per hour," "price per pound." **Percents** are ratios with denominator 100, describing how much out of a total of 100.

The three percent problems—finding a percent of a number, finding what percent one is of another, and finding the whole given a percent—cover almost all real-world percentage questions.

Proportions are the key to scaling: recipes, maps, models, surveys, medicine dosages. They let you scale anything while maintaining the correct relationships. This is why Al-Biruni could measure Earth without traveling around it, why an architect can design a building from a tiny model, why a pharmacist can calculate a dose for a patient of any weight.

Ratios, proportions, and percents are the mathematics of comparison and scaling. Learn them well, and you can understand almost any relationship between quantities.
