## Story Hook

In 628 CE, the Indian mathematician **Brahmagupta** wrote a Sanskrit verse that described, in poetic prose, the solution to a general quadratic equation. Translated into English, it reads approximately:

"To the absolute number multiplied by four times the square, add the square of the middle term; the square root of the same, less the middle term, being divided by twice the square is the value."

This is cryptic—almost incomprehensible without explanation. But unwrap it, translate it to modern symbols, and you get:

$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

The quadratic formula. One of the most important equations in all of mathematics.

It means: **For any quadratic equation, there is a formula that gives the solution.** No guessing, no geometry, no clever tricks. Just plug in the numbers and calculate. This is the power and elegance of algebra.

---

## Why It Matters

Not every quadratic factors nicely over the integers. Sometimes factoring is slow or impossible. But the quadratic formula *always works*—for any quadratic, real or imaginary solutions included.

This is why it's one of the most important formulas in mathematics:

- **Physics:** Calculating when a projectile lands.
- **Economics:** Finding break-even points.
- **Engineering:** Designing optimal structures.
- **Medicine:** Dosage and response curves.

And here's a bonus: the **discriminant**—the part under the square root, $b^2 - 4ac$—tells you *before* you solve whether solutions exist, and *how many*.

---

## Historical Context

Ancient Babylonians (1800 BCE) had procedures for solving specific types of quadratics geometrically. They couldn't generalize because they lacked symbolic notation.

Greek mathematicians (Euclid, ~300 BCE) solved quadratics using geometric constructions—exact and beautiful, but impractical for large numbers.

Islamic scholars refined the algebraic approach. Al-Khwarizmi (~820 CE) classified quadratics into six cases and gave solving procedures for each. Al-Karaji (~1000 CE) made progress on more complex equations.

The breakthrough came with **Indian mathematicians**. Brahmagupta (628 CE) and later Bhaskara II (1114–1185 CE) gave solutions that were *general*—they worked for any quadratic. Bhaskara explicitly acknowledged that quadratics can have two solutions, even negative ones (though he debated their validity).

Islamic mathematicians in the medieval period refined these ideas further. By the 1500s–1600s, European mathematicians (Cardano, Vieta, Descartes) had fully developed the formula and recognized the role of the discriminant.

## Core Concept

### Completing the Square

Before we state the formula, let's see where it comes from. The method is called **completing the square**, and it's a beautiful algebraic technique.

**Goal:** Rewrite $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ in the form $(x - h)^2 = k$.

**Steps (for $x^2 + bx + c = 0$):**

1. Move $c$ to the right: $x^2 + bx = -c$

2. Take half of $b$, square it, and add to both sides:
   $$x^2 + bx + \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 = -c + \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2$$

3. The left side is now a perfect square:
   $$\left(x + \frac{b}{2}\right)^2 = -c + \frac{b^2}{4}$$

4. Simplify the right side:
   $$\left(x + \frac{b}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{b^2 - 4c}{4}$$

5. Take the square root:
   $$x + \frac{b}{2} = \pm\frac{\sqrt{b^2 - 4c}}{2}$$

6. Solve for $x$:
   $$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4c}}{2}$$

When $a \neq 1$, divide the entire equation by $a$ first. The result is the **quadratic formula**.

---

### The Quadratic Formula

For any quadratic equation in the form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$:

$$\boxed{x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}}$$

The $\pm$ means there are typically *two solutions*:
$$x_1 = \frac{-b + \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \quad \text{and} \quad x_2 = \frac{-b - \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

---

### The Discriminant

The expression under the square root is called the **discriminant**:

$$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$$

The discriminant tells us crucial information *before* we solve:

| Discriminant | Meaning | Solutions |
|---|---|---|
| $\Delta > 0$ | Positive | Two distinct real solutions |
| $\Delta = 0$ | Zero | One real solution (repeated root) |
| $\Delta < 0$ | Negative | No real solutions (two complex conjugates) |

---

### Vertex Form

Completing the square gives us the **vertex form** of a quadratic:

$$y = a(x - h)^2 + k$$

where $(h, k)$ is the vertex (the turning point of the parabola).

For example, $y = (x - 2)^2 + 3$ has vertex at $(2, 3)$.

---

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Completing the Square:** A technique to rewrite a quadratic in the form $(x - h)^2 = k$ by adding a constant to both sides.

**Quadratic Formula:** $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$, which gives solutions to $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.

**Discriminant:** $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$; determines the number and type of solutions.

**Vertex Form:** $y = a(x - h)^2 + k$, where $(h, k)$ is the vertex.

**Real Roots:** Solutions that are real numbers.

**Complex Roots:** Solutions involving imaginary numbers (occur when $\Delta < 0$).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Using the Quadratic Formula (Two Real Solutions)

**Solve:** $x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0$

**Identify:** $a = 1$, $b = 5$, $c = 6$

**Calculate the discriminant:**
$$\Delta = 5^2 - 4(1)(6) = 25 - 24 = 1$$

Since $\Delta > 0$, there are two real solutions.

**Apply the formula:**
$$x = \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{1}}{2(1)} = \frac{-5 \pm 1}{2}$$

**Two solutions:**
$$x_1 = \frac{-5 + 1}{2} = \frac{-4}{2} = -2$$
$$x_2 = \frac{-5 - 1}{2} = \frac{-6}{2} = -3$$

**Check:**
- $(-2)^2 + 5(-2) + 6 = 4 - 10 + 6 = 0$ ✓
- $(-3)^2 + 5(-3) + 6 = 9 - 15 + 6 = 0$ ✓

(Note: This quadratic factors as $(x + 2)(x + 3) = 0$, so we could have solved it by factoring.)

---

### Example 2: One Real Solution (Perfect Square)

**Solve:** $x^2 - 6x + 9 = 0$

**Identify:** $a = 1$, $b = -6$, $c = 9$

**Discriminant:**
$$\Delta = (-6)^2 - 4(1)(9) = 36 - 36 = 0$$

Since $\Delta = 0$, there is one repeated real solution.

**Apply the formula:**
$$x = \frac{-(-6) \pm \sqrt{0}}{2(1)} = \frac{6 \pm 0}{2} = 3$$

**Solution:** $x = 3$ (a repeated root)

**Note:** This factors as $(x - 3)^2 = 0$.

---

### Example 3: More Complex Quadratic

**Solve:** $2x^2 - 7x + 3 = 0$

**Identify:** $a = 2$, $b = -7$, $c = 3$

**Discriminant:**
$$\Delta = (-7)^2 - 4(2)(3) = 49 - 24 = 25$$

Since $\Delta > 0$ and is a perfect square, there are two rational solutions.

**Apply the formula:**
$$x = \frac{-(-7) \pm \sqrt{25}}{2(2)} = \frac{7 \pm 5}{4}$$

**Two solutions:**
$$x_1 = \frac{7 + 5}{4} = \frac{12}{4} = 3$$
$$x_2 = \frac{7 - 5}{4} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$$

**Check:**
- When $x = 3$: $2(3)^2 - 7(3) + 3 = 18 - 21 + 3 = 0$ ✓
- When $x = 1/2$: $2(1/4) - 7/2 + 3 = 1/2 - 7/2 + 3 = 0$ ✓

(This factors as $(x - 3)(2x - 1) = 0$.)

---

### Example 4: Irrational Solutions

**Solve:** $x^2 - 4x - 2 = 0$

**Identify:** $a = 1$, $b = -4$, $c = -2$

**Discriminant:**
$$\Delta = (-4)^2 - 4(1)(-2) = 16 + 8 = 24$$

Since $\Delta > 0$ but is not a perfect square, there are two irrational solutions.

**Apply the formula:**
$$x = \frac{-(-4) \pm \sqrt{24}}{2(1)} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{24}}{2}$$

**Simplify:** $\sqrt{24} = \sqrt{4 \cdot 6} = 2\sqrt{6}$

$$x = \frac{4 \pm 2\sqrt{6}}{2} = 2 \pm \sqrt{6}$$

**Solutions:** $x = 2 + \sqrt{6}$ or $x = 2 - \sqrt{6}$

(Approximately $x \approx 4.45$ or $x \approx -0.45$)

---

### Example 5: No Real Solutions

**Solve:** $x^2 + 2x + 5 = 0$

**Identify:** $a = 1$, $b = 2$, $c = 5$

**Discriminant:**
$$\Delta = 2^2 - 4(1)(5) = 4 - 20 = -16$$

Since $\Delta < 0$, there are no real solutions. The parabola doesn't cross the x-axis.

**Conclusion:** This equation has no real solutions. (It has complex solutions: $x = -1 \pm 2i$, but those are beyond this course.)

---

### Example 6: Completing the Square

**Rewrite in vertex form:** $y = x^2 - 6x + 8$

**Steps:**

1. Group the first two terms: $y = (x^2 - 6x) + 8$

2. Complete the square inside the parentheses:
   - Take half the coefficient of $x$: $-6 / 2 = -3$
   - Square it: $(-3)^2 = 9$
   - Add and subtract: $y = (x^2 - 6x + 9) - 9 + 8$

3. Write as a perfect square: $y = (x - 3)^2 - 1$

**Vertex form:** $y = (x - 3)^2 - 1$

**Vertex:** $(3, -1)$ (the parabola's turning point)

---

### Example 7: Physical Application

**Problem:** An object is launched upward from the ground with initial velocity 48 ft/s. Its height at time $t$ is:
$$h(t) = -16t^2 + 48t$$

When does it hit the ground?

**Solution:**

The object hits the ground when $h(t) = 0$:
$$-16t^2 + 48t = 0$$

**Identify:** $a = -16$, $b = 48$, $c = 0$

**Discriminant:**
$$\Delta = 48^2 - 4(-16)(0) = 2304 - 0 = 2304$$
$$\sqrt{\Delta} = 48$$

**Apply the formula:**
$$t = \frac{-48 \pm 48}{2(-16)} = \frac{-48 \pm 48}{-32}$$

**Two solutions:**
$$t_1 = \frac{-48 + 48}{-32} = \frac{0}{-32} = 0 \quad \text{(launch time)}$$
$$t_2 = \frac{-48 - 48}{-32} = \frac{-96}{-32} = 3 \quad \text{(landing time)}$$

**Answer:** The object lands after 3 seconds.

(Note: We could have factored: $-16t(t - 3) = 0$.)

## Recap

The quadratic formula $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$ is the universal tool for solving quadratic equations. It's derived by completing the square—a technique that also reveals the vertex form of a parabola.

**Key insights:**

- **Discriminant ($\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$) tells you:**
  - If $\Delta > 0$: two distinct real solutions
  - If $\Delta = 0$: one repeated real solution
  - If $\Delta < 0$: no real solutions (complex solutions exist)

- **Choose your method:**
  - If the quadratic factors nicely: factor and use zero product property (faster).
  - Otherwise: use the quadratic formula (always works).

- **Completing the square:**
  - Rewrites the quadratic in vertex form.
  - Shows the geometric center of the parabola.
  - Is the foundation for deriving the quadratic formula itself.

From ancient Babylonian clay tablets to modern applications in physics and economics, the quadratic formula is one of humanity's most important mathematical discoveries. It represents the power of algebra: a single formula that solves *any* quadratic, for any values of $a$, $b$, and $c$.
