## Story Hook

Picture yourself at a bustling market in 17th-century Europe. A stall-keeper sells dates and figs—beautiful fruit, expensive and coveted. She charges 3 coins per date and 5 coins per fig, but here's the problem: she doesn't know how many of each she'll sell today.

Will someone buy 10 dates and 8 figs? Or 20 dates and 3 figs? Or nothing at all? She can't know in advance, yet she needs to plan her profits.

So she takes a piece of chalk and writes on a slate:

$$3d + 5f$$

Two letters—*d* for dates, *f* for figs—representing quantities she doesn't yet know. With this one simple notation, she can calculate her earnings for *any* combination of sales. This is the birth of variables: a way to name the unknown and work with it as if we already know what it is.

Variables are among the most powerful inventions in the history of thought. They let us write rules, not just for one situation, but for *all* situations of the same kind.

---

## Why It Matters

Before variables, mathematics was trapped in specific numbers. A baker might write: "If I mix 2 cups of flour with 1 cup of water, I get dough." That's true, but only for those exact quantities.

With variables, the baker writes: "If I mix $f$ cups of flour with $w$ cups of water, I get dough." Now the formula applies to *any* batch—small or large, and the baker can adjust quantities while keeping proportions correct.

Variables are the reason we can write formulas, solve puzzles, build bridges that won't collapse, design medicines that actually work, and launch rockets to the moon. They let us think in generalities rather than special cases.

---

## Historical Context

The letter *x* for the unknown didn't appear by accident. In the 9th century, mathematicians in the Islamic world solved equations but expressed them in words—no symbols at all. An equation might read: "A quantity, when increased by five, equals twelve."

François Viète (1540–1603), a French mathematician, was among the first to use letters consistently to represent unknowns. His work opened a new era. Then René Descartes (1596–1650) popularized the convention of using letters *near the end* of the alphabet ($x$, $y$, $z$) for unknowns and letters *near the beginning* ($a$, $b$, $c$) for known quantities.

This seemingly small choice—which letters to use—made algebra readable, shareable, and powerful. A mathematician in Rome could now read Descartes' work from France without learning a different verbal tradition.

## Core Concept

A **variable** is a symbol (usually a letter) that represents an unknown or changeable quantity.

An **algebraic expression** is a collection of numbers, variables, and operations (like $+$, $-$, $\times$, $\div$) combined together.

When we write $3d + 5f$, we're building an expression from:
- **Terms**: the parts being added or subtracted
- **Coefficients**: the numbers multiplying the variables
- **Constants**: numbers that stand alone

In $3d + 5f$:
- First term: $3d$ (coefficient 3, variable $d$)
- Second term: $5f$ (coefficient 5, variable $f$)
- No constant term here

In the expression $2x + 7$:
- First term: $2x$ (coefficient 2, variable $x$)
- Second term: $7$ (this is the constant—a number with no variable)

**Like terms** are terms that have the same variable raised to the same power. In $3x + 5x$, both terms are "like terms" because they're both $x$ to the first power. We can combine them: $3x + 5x = 8x$.

But $3x$ and $5x^2$ are *not* like terms—the variables have different powers.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Variable**: A symbol representing a number whose value is unknown or can change.

**Algebraic Expression**: A mathematical phrase using numbers, variables, and operations.

**Term**: A single part of an expression, consisting of a coefficient and its variables (or just a constant).

**Coefficient**: The numerical factor in a term. In $7y$, the coefficient is 7. In $-3a$, the coefficient is $-3$. If we write just $x$, the coefficient is 1.

**Constant**: A term that contains only a number, no variables.

**Like Terms**: Terms that have identical variable parts (same letter, same exponent). Examples: $5a$ and $3a$, or $2xy$ and $-7xy$.

**Evaluating an Expression**: Finding the value of an expression by replacing variables with specific numbers and performing the arithmetic.

### Writing Expressions from Words

- "Three more than $x$" → $x + 3$
- "Five times $y$" → $5y$
- "Four less than twice $p$" → $2p - 4$
- "The sum of $m$ and $n$" → $m + n$
- "The product of $a$, $b$, and $c$" → $abc$

### Combining Like Terms

$$5x + 3x = 8x$$

$$2y + 6 + 3y - 2 = (2y + 3y) + (6 - 2) = 5y + 4$$

$$4a + 2b + 3a - b = (4a + 3a) + (2b - b) = 7a + b$$

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Evaluate $3x + 5$ when $x = 2$**

Substitute $x = 2$:
$$3(2) + 5 = 6 + 5 = 11$$

**Example 2: Evaluate $2a - 3b$ when $a = 4$ and $b = 1$**

Substitute $a = 4$ and $b = 1$:
$$2(4) - 3(1) = 8 - 3 = 5$$

**Example 3: Simplify $4m + 2 - m + 5$**

Group like terms:
$$(4m - m) + (2 + 5) = 3m + 7$$

**Example 4: Write an expression for the perimeter of a rectangle with length $l$ and width $w$**

Perimeter = $l + w + l + w = 2l + 2w$

**Example 5: A coffee shop charges $3 per latte and $2 per pastry. Write an expression for the cost of $c$ lattes and $p$ pastries. Then find the cost if you order 2 lattes and 3 pastries.**

Expression: $3c + 2p$

Substitute $c = 2$ and $p = 3$:
$$3(2) + 2(3) = 6 + 6 = 12$$

The cost is 12 dollars.

## Application

**Real-World Scenario: Planning a School Trip**

Your class is planning a trip. The bus costs $300 total. Each student pays an equal share. If there are $s$ students, the cost per student is:

$$\frac{300}{s}$$

- If there are 25 students, what is the cost per student?
- If there are 30 students, what is the cost per student?
- Why does increasing $s$ make the cost per student go down?

This is how real planners use expressions: to understand how changing one quantity affects another.

## Recap

A **variable** is a letter representing a number we don't yet know. An **expression** combines variables with numbers and operations. By substituting numerical values for variables, we can **evaluate** the expression to find a specific answer.

Variables let us write rules that work in many situations. The stall-keeper's formula $3d + 5f$ works for any number of dates and figs. A builder's formula for materials works for any size structure. Doctors use expressions to calculate medicines for patients of different weights.

Learning to write and work with expressions is learning to think mathematically—to see patterns, to generalize, and to describe the world in a language that numbers and symbols understand.
