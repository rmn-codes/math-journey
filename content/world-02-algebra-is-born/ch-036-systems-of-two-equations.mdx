## Story Hook

Imagine a scribe in ancient China, around 200 BCE, consulting the "Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art" — a mathematical masterpiece older than algebra as we know it. One problem: a farmer buys 2 roosters and 2 hens for 5 silver coins. Another farmer buys 1 rooster and 3 hens for 4 silver coins.

How much does a rooster cost? How much does a hen?

This is not one equation. This is *two* equations with *two* unknowns. And there's only one pair of prices that satisfies *both* conditions at the same time. The Chinese mathematicians had methods for finding that pair — long before Europeans invented the symbolic algebra we use today.

---

## Why It Matters

Most interesting problems in the real world aren't about *one* unknown. They're about *two* or more.

- A store carries two types of goods. Profit depends on how many of each to stock.
- An engineer designs a structure with two forces acting on it.
- A chemist mixes two solutions with different concentrations.
- An economist studies supply and demand — two curves that must meet.

One equation can't capture these situations. You need *two* equations to pin down *two* unknowns uniquely. And the methods for solving systems of equations are among the most powerful tools in mathematics and science.

---

## Historical Context

The Chinese "Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art" (成書 ~200 BCE) contains the oldest known systematic approach to systems of equations. Chinese mathematicians used a rectangular grid of numbers (a proto-matrix) to organize equations and solve them methodically.

Islamic scholars like al-Khwarizmi and later al-Karaji refined methods for solving systems algebraically, using prose and poetic forms before symbolic notation existed.

In medieval Europe, mathematicians like Cardano (1501–1576) and Vieta (1540–1603) developed systematic notations. By the 1600s, the substitution and elimination methods had become standard tools in every algebra text.

The *graphical* interpretation—that the solution is where two lines intersect—emerged as symbolic and geometric approaches merged in the 1600s and 1700s.

## Core Concept

### Why One Equation with Two Unknowns Isn't Enough

Consider: $x + y = 10$

This has *infinitely many solutions*:
- $(0, 10)$, $(1, 9)$, $(2, 8)$, $(3, 7)$, $(5, 5)$, $(10, 0)$, etc.

Graphically, this is a *line*. Any point on the line satisfies the equation.

But if we add a *second* condition, say $x - y = 2$:

Now we need *both* equations to be true. The solution is the point where the two lines *intersect*. For most pairs of lines, there's exactly *one* such point.

$$x + y = 10$$
$$x - y = 2$$

Adding these: $2x = 12$, so $x = 6$. Then $y = 4$. Solution: $(6, 4)$.

This is the core insight: **Two equations with two unknowns typically give us one unique solution.**

---

### The Three Cases

**Case 1: Consistent (Unique Solution)**

The two lines intersect at exactly one point.

Example:
$$x + y = 10$$
$$x - y = 2$$

Solution: $(6, 4)$.

**Case 2: Inconsistent (No Solution)**

The two lines are parallel (same slope, different y-intercepts). They never meet.

Example:
$$x + y = 10$$
$$x + y = 5$$

These equations contradict each other. No solution exists.

**Case 3: Dependent (Infinitely Many Solutions)**

The two equations describe the *same line*. Every point on the line is a solution.

Example:
$$x + y = 10$$
$$2x + 2y = 20$$

The second equation is just the first multiplied by 2. They're the same line. Infinitely many solutions: every point $(x, y)$ on the line $x + y = 10$.

---

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**System of Equations:** Two or more equations involving the same variables. A solution must satisfy *all* equations simultaneously.

**Solution of a System:** Values for the variables that make every equation in the system true.

**Consistent System:** A system with at least one solution.

**Inconsistent System:** A system with no solution.

**Dependent System:** A consistent system with infinitely many solutions (the equations represent the same line/plane/object).

---

### Method 1: Substitution

Choose one equation and solve it for one variable. Substitute that expression into the other equation.

**Steps:**
1. Solve one equation for one variable.
2. Substitute into the other equation.
3. Solve the resulting single-variable equation.
4. Back-substitute to find the other variable.

---

### Method 2: Elimination (Addition Method)

Multiply one or both equations by a constant so that one variable has opposite coefficients. Add the equations to eliminate that variable.

**Steps:**
1. Arrange both equations in standard form.
2. Multiply one or both equations so a variable has opposite coefficients.
3. Add the equations to eliminate that variable.
4. Solve the resulting equation.
5. Substitute back to find the other variable.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Substitution Method

**Problem:** Solve the system:
$$x + y = 7$$
$$2x - y = 5$$

**Step 1: Solve the first equation for $y$**
$$y = 7 - x$$

**Step 2: Substitute into the second equation**
$$2x - (7 - x) = 5$$

**Step 3: Solve for $x$**
$$2x - 7 + x = 5$$
$$3x = 12$$
$$x = 4$$

**Step 4: Find $y$**
$$y = 7 - 4 = 3$$

**Check:** $x + y = 4 + 3 = 7$ ✓ and $2x - y = 8 - 3 = 5$ ✓

**Solution:** $(4, 3)$

---

### Example 2: Elimination Method

**Problem:** Solve the system:
$$3x + 2y = 13$$
$$2x - 2y = 2$$

**Step 1:** Notice that $2y$ and $-2y$ are opposites. Add directly.

$$3x + 2y + 2x - 2y = 13 + 2$$
$$5x = 15$$
$$x = 3$$

**Step 2: Substitute to find $y$**

From the second equation: $2(3) - 2y = 2$

$$6 - 2y = 2$$
$$-2y = -4$$
$$y = 2$$

**Check:** $3(3) + 2(2) = 9 + 4 = 13$ ✓ and $2(3) - 2(2) = 6 - 4 = 2$ ✓

**Solution:** $(3, 2)$

---

### Example 3: Systems Needing Multiplication

**Problem:** Solve the system:
$$2x + 3y = 11$$
$$3x + 2y = 14$$

**Step 1:** Multiply to create opposite coefficients. Multiply the first by 3 and the second by -2:

$$6x + 9y = 33$$
$$-6x - 4y = -28$$

**Step 2: Add**
$$5y = 5$$
$$y = 1$$

**Step 3: Substitute into the first original equation**
$$2x + 3(1) = 11$$
$$2x = 8$$
$$x = 4$$

**Check:** $2(4) + 3(1) = 8 + 3 = 11$ ✓ and $3(4) + 2(1) = 12 + 2 = 14$ ✓

**Solution:** $(4, 1)$

---

### Example 4: Real-World Application (Two Prices)

**Problem:** A store sells pens and notebooks. 3 pens and 2 notebooks cost $8. 1 pen and 4 notebooks cost $10. Find the price of each.

**Step 1: Define variables**
- Let $p$ = price of a pen
- Let $n$ = price of a notebook

**Step 2: Write equations**
$$3p + 2n = 8$$
$$p + 4n = 10$$

**Step 3: Solve using substitution**

From the second equation: $p = 10 - 4n$

Substitute into the first:
$$3(10 - 4n) + 2n = 8$$
$$30 - 12n + 2n = 8$$
$$-10n = -22$$
$$n = 2.2$$

Then: $p = 10 - 4(2.2) = 10 - 8.8 = 1.2$

**Check:**
- $3(1.2) + 2(2.2) = 3.6 + 4.4 = 8$ ✓
- $1.2 + 4(2.2) = 1.2 + 8.8 = 10$ ✓

**Answer:** A pen costs $1.20; a notebook costs $2.20.

---

### Example 5: Recognizing an Inconsistent System

**Problem:** Solve the system:
$$2x + y = 5$$
$$4x + 2y = 8$$

**Step 1:** Multiply the first equation by 2:
$$4x + 2y = 10$$
$$4x + 2y = 8$$

**Step 2:** Subtract the second from the first:
$$0 = 2$$

This is a *contradiction*. No solution exists.

**Reason:** The first equation says $4x + 2y = 10$. The second says $4x + 2y = 8$. These can't both be true.

Graphically, these are parallel lines with the same slope but different y-intercepts.

**Answer:** No solution (inconsistent system).

## Recap

When you have *two* unknowns and *two* conditions, a system of two equations lets you find a unique solution (or determine if no solution exists).

**Key methods:**
- **Substitution:** Solve one for a variable; substitute into the other.
- **Elimination:** Multiply and add to eliminate one variable.

**Three cases:**
- **Consistent:** One solution (lines intersect at one point).
- **Inconsistent:** No solution (parallel lines).
- **Dependent:** Infinitely many solutions (same line).

Systems are everywhere in applied mathematics. Once you master solving them, you can tackle real-world problems involving multiple constraints and unknowns.
