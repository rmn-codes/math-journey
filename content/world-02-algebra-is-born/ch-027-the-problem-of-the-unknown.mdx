# The Problem of the Unknown

## Story Hook

Imagine you are a merchant in Babylon, around 2000 BCE. You stand in your grain storehouse, and you face a problem that keeps you awake at night.

A farmer comes to you asking for a loan. He needs grain *now*, before the harvest. In six months, he promises, he will pay you back in grain—with interest. But how much grain should you lend him today? You know what the interest rate is. You know how much he must repay. But the amount you need to loan him *now*—that is unknown.

You have no way to write it down. Your counting system has numbers: 1, 2, 3, and so on. But there is no symbol for "the thing I do not yet know." So you do what every merchant did: you describe it in words. "Lend the farmer some amount. Call it 'the thing.' Add the interest. The result must equal the repayment." You solve it by trial and error, using your intuition and experience.

The moment you realize you need a *placeholder* for what you don't know—that is the birth of algebra.

## Why It Matters

For thousands of years, humans solved problems through arithmetic alone: adding, subtracting, multiplying, dividing numbers they could see and count. But arithmetic cannot answer questions like:

- What number, when tripled, gives 24?
- A field costs 50 coins. I have 20 coins. How many more do I need?
- A merchant owes a debt and then earns wages. When will his account balance zero?

These problems require thinking about *unknown quantities*—numbers we must discover. Once you have a way to represent the unknown, you can write these problems down, reason about them, and solve them systematically. That is the birth of algebra.

Understanding the unknown is the first step toward a mathematics that can solve any problem, not just ones where all the numbers are already known.

## Historical Context

The concept of an unknown quantity did not arrive suddenly. It emerged across cultures over many centuries because people faced the same practical problems everywhere.

In ancient Egypt, around 1600 BCE, scribes wrote mathematical texts that included problems about unknowns. The Rhind Mathematical Papyrus contains problems phrased like: "A quantity and its seventh make 19. What is the quantity?" The Egyptians had no symbol for the unknown. They wrote everything in words. Yet they developed techniques for solving these problems, which they called "aha" problems—the word for "finding" or "discovering."

In Babylon, clay tablets show that scribes solved problems about unknown areas, unknown weights, and unknown portions. They worked with what amounts to equations, though they described everything in rhetorical form—pure words, no symbols.

By the time of ancient India, mathematicians like Aryabhata (476–550 CE) and Bhaskara II (1114–1185 CE) worked with unknowns more explicitly. They gave the unknown a name: "yavattavat," which means "as much as." They even developed techniques for working with multiple unknowns in the same problem.

The Islamic world, particularly during the Baghdad Renaissance (8th–10th centuries), synthesized these traditions. Scholars like al-Khwarizmi wrote systematically about solving problems with unknowns, organizing the methods that had evolved piecemeal across cultures.

## Core Concept

An **equation** is a statement that two quantities are equal. When one of those quantities is unknown, the equation becomes a *question* you must answer.

Consider the simple sentence: "A number plus 5 equals 12." This is an equation. The unknown number is what we seek.

Thinking about unknowns is the heart of algebra. Instead of just computing with known numbers, you now *reason about* numbers you don't know yet. You ask: what number makes this sentence true?

The unknown is sometimes called a **variable**—a placeholder that can stand for any number. In ancient times, it was expressed through words: "the thing," "the unknown," "what we seek." Later, it became a symbol: $x$, $n$, or another letter.

The power of this idea is enormous. Once you have a name for the unknown, you can:
- Write down the problem clearly
- Manipulate it without losing track
- Find the answer systematically
- Check your answer

Without the unknown, each problem feels like a puzzle with a unique trick. With the unknown, patterns emerge. You begin to see *structure* in problems that seem different on the surface.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Key Terms

**Unknown**
A number that is not yet known, but that satisfies certain conditions in a problem. In ancient texts, the unknown was often called "the thing" or "the aha."

**Variable**
A symbol (often a letter) that represents an unknown quantity in an equation. The variable can take on different values depending on the equation.

**Equation**
A mathematical statement that two expressions are equal. An equation often contains an unknown and represents a question to be solved.

**Rhetorical algebra**
An early form of algebra in which all problems and solutions are expressed using words, with no symbolic notation.

**Aha**
A term from Egyptian mathematics meaning "finding" or "discovering"—used for problems about unknown quantities.

### Thinking About Unknowns

When ancient scribes faced a problem, they reasoned like this:

"A merchant has some amount of grain. He sells half of it. Then he sells 20 more bushels. He has 50 bushels left. How much did he start with?"

Today, we might write: $\frac{x}{2} + 20 = 50$... no wait, let me rewrite this more carefully.

If he starts with $x$ bushels, sells half (leaving $\frac{x}{2}$), then sells 20 more, he has $\frac{x}{2} - 20$ left. So:

$$\frac{x}{2} - 20 = 50$$

But an ancient Egyptian scribe would have written: "Let the unknown amount be called 'the thing.' Take away half the thing. Then take away 20. You are left with 50. The thing must be..."

Both approaches solve the same problem. The ancient rhetorical method is longer and easier to confuse, but it works. The symbolic method is compact and clear.

### From Words to Equations

The first step in solving an unknown-quantity problem is *translating* the words into an equation. Here are patterns:

| English phrase | Equation |
|---|---|
| "A number plus 5" | $x + 5$ |
| "Twice a number" | $2x$ |
| "A number divided by 3" | $\frac{x}{3}$ |
| "A number minus 7" | $x - 7$ |
| "A number equals 20" | $x = 20$ |

Once you have written the equation, finding the unknown becomes a matter of systematic steps—a process that does not depend on clever guessing.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: An Ancient Egyptian Aha Problem

A quantity and its half make 12. What is the quantity?

**Solution**

Let the unknown quantity be $x$. The problem says "$x$ and its half make 12," so:
$$x + \frac{x}{2} = 12$$

Combine the terms with $x$:
$$\frac{2x}{2} + \frac{x}{2} = 12$$
$$\frac{3x}{2} = 12$$

Multiply both sides by 2:
$$3x = 24$$

Divide both sides by 3:
$$x = 8$$

Check: $8 + 4 = 12$ ✓

The quantity is 8.

### Example 2: A Babylonian Problem

A farmer owns a rectangular field. The length is twice the width. The perimeter is 60 cubits. What are the length and width?

**Solution**

Let the width be $w$. Then the length is $2w$.

The perimeter of a rectangle is $P = 2(\text{length} + \text{width})$, so:
$$2(2w + w) = 60$$
$$2(3w) = 60$$
$$6w = 60$$
$$w = 10$$

The width is 10 cubits. The length is $2 \times 10 = 20$ cubits.

Check: $P = 2(20 + 10) = 60$ ✓

### Example 3: A Merchant's Dilemma

A merchant sells cloth. After selling 15 bolts, he has 27 bolts left. How many bolts did he start with?

**Solution**

Let $x$ be the number of bolts he started with. After selling 15, he has $x - 15$ left. This equals 27:
$$x - 15 = 27$$

Add 15 to both sides:
$$x = 42$$

The merchant started with 42 bolts.

Check: $42 - 15 = 27$ ✓

## Reasoning Task

An ancient scribe and a modern student face the same problem: "I have a certain amount of money. I spend 12 coins. I have 18 coins left. How much did I start with?"

The scribe solves it by writing: "Let the unknown amount be called 'the thing.' Take away 12. The result is 18. The thing must be 30."

The modern student writes: $x - 12 = 18$, so $x = 30$.

Both get the right answer. Write 5–7 sentences explaining:
- Why both methods work
- What advantage the symbolic equation has
- Why a scribe would still have found the rhetorical method useful even without symbols

## Application

The ancient problem of unknowns is not merely historical. Every day, we solve problems that require finding an unknown.

A baker knows her dough recipe requires flour, water, and yeast in certain proportions. She wants to bake 20 loaves. Her recipe makes 5 loaves. How much flour does she need? This is an equation with an unknown.

A parent wants to buy a bicycle. It costs $250. She has saved $180. How much more does she need to save? Unknown: the additional amount.

An engineer designs a bridge. It must span a river that is 150 meters wide. The bridge will have a central support column and two equal side spans. Each side span is 40 meters. How wide must the central section be? Unknown: the width of the central section.

Every such problem is, at its heart, an ancient Babylonian or Egyptian problem dressed in modern clothes. Once you know how to work with unknowns, you can solve these problems systematically. You do not have to guess or rely on intuition. You set up an equation and solve it.

## Recap

- An **unknown** is a number that is not yet known but satisfies conditions in a problem.
- An **equation** is a statement that two expressions are equal; it represents a question about an unknown.
- **Rhetorical algebra** uses words to describe problems with unknowns. It works but is cumbersome.
- Ancient Egypt, Babylon, India, and the Islamic world all developed methods for solving problems with unknowns, long before symbolic notation existed.
- The key insight is that once you name the unknown, you can reason about it systematically and find its value.
