## Story Hook

Imagine an archaeologist holding a clay tablet from ancient Babylon, circa 2000 BCE. On it, cuneiform script reads: "I have two fields. The total area is 1800 square cubits. One field is 3 times the other. Find each field."

This isn't a riddle in a book. This is a real farmer's problem — the kind that kept ancient civilizations fed. And the scribe who solved it did something extraordinary: they *translated* the messy world into clean symbols, solved a puzzle in that symbolic realm, and translated the answer back to reality.

That translation is the heart of every word problem. It's also the birth of algebra as a practical tool.

---

## Why It Matters

Word problems are where mathematics *comes alive*. They answer the question every student asks: "When am I ever going to use this?"

Every time an engineer designs a bridge, a pharmacist measures a dose, or a business owner breaks even, they're solving a word problem. But here's the secret: the problem-solving method is always the same. Once you master the *translation process*, any new scenario becomes manageable.

Word problems teach you to:
- **Think clearly** under uncertainty
- **Organize information** from messy situations
- **Communicate** your thinking in equations
- **Check** whether your answer makes sense

This is the real mathematician's work.

---

## Historical Context

The Babylonians (1800–1600 BCE) left us clay tablets with dozens of word problems. They had no symbolic algebra — no $x$ or $=$. Yet they solved problems about fields, water clocks, and brick production using procedures that *were* algebraic in spirit.

The Greeks (Euclid, ~300 BCE) translated many Babylonian problems into geometry, proving answers with diagrams rather than equations.

Islamic scholars like al-Khwarizmi (~820 CE) refined the methods into the systematic *al-jabr* (restoration) — which gave us the name "algebra." They solved word problems about inheritance, trade, and land with prose but also with a logical structure we'd recognize today.

By the medieval period, European mathematicians had adopted symbolic notation and turned word problems into the centerpiece of arithmetic education — where they remain today.

## Core Concept

Every word problem follows the same **modeling process**:

### The Modeling Process (5 steps)

**1. Read and understand**
- Read the problem twice.
- Underline the question.
- Identify what you're looking for.

**2. Define variables**
- Choose a letter (usually $x$, $y$, $a$) for each unknown quantity.
- Write it down clearly: "Let $x$ = the width of the field (in cubits)."

**3. Write an equation (or equations)**
- Use the facts given to build an equation.
- Pay attention to *signal words* (below).

**4. Solve**
- Solve the equation using all the algebra you've learned.

**5. Check and interpret**
- Does your answer *make sense* in the original story?
- Is it the right units? Is it positive when it should be?
- State your answer in words.

---

### Signal Words

When you see these words, they're signaling an operation:

| Word(s) | Operation | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| more than, added to, sum | addition | "5 more than $x$" = $x + 5$ |
| less than, subtract, fewer | subtraction | "3 fewer than $x$" = $x - 3$ |
| times, product, of | multiplication | "3 times $x$" = $3x$ |
| divided by, half, per | division | "$x$ divided by 2" = $\frac{x}{2}$ |
| is, equals, was | equals | "is 10" = $= 10$ |

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Word Problem:** A real-world situation described in words, where you must find one or more unknown quantities using mathematical reasoning.

**Mathematical Model:** The equation (or system of equations) that represents the relationship described in words. It's a *translation*.

**Variable:** A letter representing an unknown quantity. It must be defined clearly.

**Constraint:** A fact from the problem that limits the value of the variables. It becomes an equation.

### Common Problem Types

**Type 1: Age Problems**

Setup: Two people's ages are related.

Example: "Maria is 4 years older than her brother. Together, they are 26. How old is each?"

Let $m$ = Maria's age, $b$ = brother's age.

$$m = b + 4$$
$$m + b = 26$$

Substitute: $(b + 4) + b = 26$, so $2b + 4 = 26$, thus $b = 11$ and $m = 15$.

**Type 2: Rate-Time-Distance**

Core formula: $d = rt$ (distance = rate × time)

Example: "A train leaves at 60 mph. A car leaves the same place 2 hours later at 75 mph. How long until the car catches up?"

Let $t$ = time the train travels.
- Train distance: $60t$
- Car time: $t - 2$
- Car distance: $75(t - 2)$

When they meet: $60t = 75(t - 2)$

Solve: $60t = 75t - 150$, so $-15t = -150$, thus $t = 10$ hours.

**Type 3: Mixture Problems**

Setup: Two substances with different properties are mixed.

Example: "How much 20% salt solution and 50% salt solution should we mix to make 10 liters of 35% solution?"

Let $x$ = liters of 20% solution.

Then $10 - x$ = liters of 50% solution.

Salt from first: $0.20x$

Salt from second: $0.50(10 - x)$

Total salt: $0.35(10)$

Equation: $0.20x + 0.50(10 - x) = 0.35(10)$

Simplify: $0.20x + 5 - 0.50x = 3.5$, so $-0.30x = -1.5$, thus $x = 5$ liters.

**Type 4: Number Problems**

Setup: Relationships between numbers.

Example: "A number is 5 more than another. Their product is 24. Find the numbers."

Let $x$ = first number.

Then $x + 5$ = second number.

Equation: $x(x + 5) = 24$, which is $x^2 + 5x - 24 = 0$. (We'll solve this type in the quadratic chapters.)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: A Simple Age Problem

**Problem:** Anna is 12 years old. Her mother is 3 times as old. In how many years will Anna be half her mother's age?

**Step 1: Define variables**
- Let $y$ = number of years from now.

**Step 2: Set up ages after $y$ years**
- Anna's age then: $12 + y$
- Mother's age then: $36 + y$

**Step 3: Write the condition**
- "Anna will be half her mother's age"
$$12 + y = \frac{1}{2}(36 + y)$$

**Step 4: Solve**
$$12 + y = 18 + \frac{y}{2}$$
$$y - \frac{y}{2} = 18 - 12$$
$$\frac{y}{2} = 6$$
$$y = 12$$

**Step 5: Check and interpret**
- In 12 years, Anna will be $12 + 12 = 24$.
- Her mother will be $36 + 12 = 48$.
- Is 24 half of 48? Yes. ✓

**Answer:** In 12 years.

---

### Example 2: Rate-Time-Distance

**Problem:** Two cyclists start from the same point. One travels at 12 mph, the other at 15 mph, in the same direction. After how long are they 12 miles apart?

**Step 1: Define**
- Let $t$ = time (in hours).

**Step 2: Set up distances**
- Slower cyclist: $12t$ miles
- Faster cyclist: $15t$ miles

**Step 3: Difference condition**
$$15t - 12t = 12$$

**Step 4: Solve**
$$3t = 12$$
$$t = 4$$

**Step 5: Check**
- In 4 hours: slow cyclist goes $12 \times 4 = 48$ miles, fast goes $15 \times 4 = 60$ miles.
- Difference: $60 - 48 = 12$. ✓

**Answer:** After 4 hours.

---

### Example 3: Consecutive Integer Problem

**Problem:** The sum of three consecutive integers is 48. Find them.

**Step 1: Define**
- Let $n$ = the first integer.
- Then $n + 1$ and $n + 2$ are the next two.

**Step 2: Write the equation**
$$n + (n + 1) + (n + 2) = 48$$

**Step 3: Solve**
$$3n + 3 = 48$$
$$3n = 45$$
$$n = 15$$

**Step 4: Answer and check**
- The integers are 15, 16, and 17.
- Sum: $15 + 16 + 17 = 48$. ✓

## Practice Set

Solve each problem completely. Define your variable, write your equation, solve, and check.

1. **Age problem:** Tom is 5 years younger than Jade. Together they are 31 years old. How old is each?

2. **Simple arithmetic:** When you add 8 to a number and then divide by 2, you get 10. What is the number?

3. **Rate-time-distance:** A cyclist travels at 18 mph. How far does she travel in 2.5 hours?

4. **Consecutive integers:** The sum of two consecutive even integers is 66. What are they?

5. **Money problem:** Tickets cost $5 for adults and $3 for children. A group spent $100 on 24 tickets total. How many adult tickets were sold? (Let $a$ = adult tickets.)

6. **Mixture:** A 30% juice solution is mixed with a 70% juice solution. How much of each is needed to make 20 liters of a 50% solution?

## Reasoning Task

**Problem:** A rectangle has a perimeter of 56 cm. Its length is 4 cm more than twice its width. Find the dimensions.

**Your task:**
1. Define variables for length and width.
2. Write two equations (one for perimeter, one for the relationship).
3. Solve the system. (Hint: use substitution.)
4. Verify your answer makes geometric sense.
5. Explain why you chose the variable(s) you did.

## Application

**Real-world scenario:** A small business sells handmade candles for $15 each. The owner's monthly costs are $300 (materials, supplies, etc.). How many candles must they sell to make $200 profit in a month?

Set this up as a word problem:
- Define a variable.
- Write an equation for profit.
- Solve.
- Interpret: what does your answer tell the owner?

## Recap

Word problems are where algebra meets reality. The **modeling process**—read, define, write, solve, check, interpret—is your toolkit for turning messy situations into clean mathematics and back again.

Key steps:
1. **Understand** what you're looking for.
2. **Define variables** for unknowns.
3. **Use signal words** to translate to equations.
4. **Solve** carefully.
5. **Check** your answer in the original context.

Every problem type (age, rate-distance, mixture, money) follows this same structure. Once you master the process, new problems become manageable puzzles rather than mysteries.
