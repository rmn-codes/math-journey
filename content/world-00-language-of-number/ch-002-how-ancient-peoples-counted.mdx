## Story Hook

Imagine walking into the Royal Belgian Institute of Natural Sciences in Brussels. In a climate-controlled case sits a small bone—ochre-colored, worn smooth with age. It's called the Ishango bone, discovered in 1960 in the Congo, and it is 20,000 years old. Its surface is covered with carved notches, arranged in patterns.

Look closely, and you'll see groups of marks, rows separated by tiny ridges. What was someone recording with these marks? A count of animals from the hunt? A record of lunar cycles? A tally of days passed? A mathematical table? We still don't know for certain. But we know they were *counting*. Before any civilization built pyramids or temples, before anyone invented the symbols we use today, humans were taking a simple idea—a mark for each thing—and turning it into the foundation of everything we do with numbers.

This chapter is the story of how that simple idea—one mark, one thing—spread across the world and took a thousand different forms.

## Why It Matters

Counting is perhaps humanity's greatest invention. It allowed us to manage grain stores, trade fairly, track time, and navigate the stars. But counting didn't come from nowhere. It required a leap of imagination: the understanding that a mark, a knot, a body part, a word could stand for a real thing you couldn't see.

When you count on your fingers today, you're doing what humans have done for tens of thousands of years. When you use tally marks to keep score, you're using the same system the Ishango bone's creator used. These ancient methods aren't just museum pieces—they're the bedrock of how we think about quantity. Understanding them helps us understand *why* our modern number system works the way it does.

## Historical Context

Twenty thousand years ago, long before writing, long before cities, the people of Central Africa kept records using bone. We don't know their names or their language, but we know they counted.

By 10,000 BCE, humans had begun farming in the Fertile Crescent. Farmers needed to track seeds, harvests, and herds—this is where systematic counting became crucial for survival. In Mesopotamia, priests in temples used clay tokens—tiny shaped pieces that stood for different quantities of grain or livestock. A cone meant 1 measure of grain; a sphere meant 10. Each token was a concrete counter, a physical stand-in for something real.

In the Andes, the Inca Empire developed a completely different system: the quipu, or "knot." Knotted cords of different colors recorded everything from tribute records to astronomical observations. In Papua New Guinea, some cultures didn't use objects or marks at all—they counted by touching body parts in a fixed sequence: thumb, index finger, middle finger... all the way to the nose and ears. Each culture solved the same problem in its own way.

## Core Concept

At the heart of all these systems lies one beautiful idea: **one-to-one correspondence**. For every sheep, one mark. For every day, one knot. For every item to count, one unit of counting.

This is the fundamental principle of counting. You don't need to know the concept of "five" to count five things—you just need to match one item to one mark, over and over, until all items are matched. The mark isn't the number; the mark *represents* the number.

**Tally marks** are the simplest form. A vertical line for each item: $\parallel$ for two, $|||$ for three. To make large quantities easier to read, cultures grouped marks in fives, crossing four lines with a fifth: $\cancel{||||} \,\parallel\parallel$ for seven. This grouping doesn't change the principle—it still rests on one-to-one correspondence—but it makes the count readable at a glance.

**Body counting** is another path. In many cultures, people counted by touching body parts in sequence, always in the same order. In some systems, you'd count on both hands; in others, you'd continue up the arms, across the chest, down the legs. Once you reached the toes, you'd start over with a helper, or switch to using objects. This method doesn't leave a permanent record, but it works perfectly for counts you need only in the moment.

**The quipu** is an ingenious record-keeper. The Incas tied knots into knotted cords. The position of the knot on the cord—near the top, middle, or bottom—carried meaning, similar to how our digits mean different things depending on position. Different colored cords recorded different things. A quipu could hold vast records: military rosters, tribute owed by subject peoples, demographic information. Without writing, the Incas used knots to remember.

The key distinction: A **number** is a concept—the quantity of things. A **numeral** is a symbol or system for writing or recording that number. The number four exists whether you write it as $4$, $IV$, $||||$, or four knots on a cord. The numeral is just the language we use to speak about it.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**One-to-One Correspondence**: A pairing of items in one set with items in another set, such that each item in one set is paired with exactly one item in the other. When counting, we pair each object with a mark or word in the counting sequence.

**Tally Mark**: A mark (usually a short vertical line) used to record a count. Groups of five tally marks (with the fifth line crossing the first four) are called a **tally group** and make larger quantities easier to read.

**Numeral**: A symbol or system of symbols used to represent a number.

**Number**: The abstract concept of quantity; what we are counting.

**Body Counting System**: A counting method that uses a fixed sequence of body parts as a reference for "counting words" or touchpoints. The sequence is always the same, allowing multiple people to share the same numbering system.

**Quipu** (or Quipu): A device made of knotted cords used by the Incas and their predecessors to record numerical and possibly narrative information. Position, color, and knot type encode different values.

**Grouping**: Organizing multiple units into clusters (often groups of five or ten) to make large quantities easier to read, count, and remember—without changing the underlying one-to-one correspondence.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Reading Tally Marks**

Your grandmother wants to keep track of how many eggs the hens laid each day. She uses tally marks:

Day 1: $\cancel{||||} \, ||$ → one group of five, plus two more = 7 eggs

Day 2: $\cancel{||||} \, \cancel{||||} \, ||| $ → two groups of five, plus three more = 13 eggs

Day 3: $\cancel{||||} \, |$ → one group of five, plus one more = 6 eggs

Notice how grouping in fives makes it easy to count without checking each mark individually.

**Example 2: Converting Between Systems**

Suppose you have a count of 12 sheep, and you want to represent it three different ways.

- **Tally marks**: $\cancel{||||} \, \cancel{||||} \, ||$ (two groups of five, plus two)
- **Body counting sequence**: If each touch on the body is one count, you'd go: two hands (10 fingers), then place two more with a helper
- **Numeral**: The number is 12

All three represent the same quantity—the number twelve.

**Example 3: One-to-One Correspondence in Action**

A traveler arrives at a market with a basket of figs. The merchant needs to know how many. They don't count aloud or use marks—they simply match: one fig into one pile, another fig into another pile, and so on. When all figs are distributed into piles, they count the piles. If there are twelve piles, there are twelve figs. This pairing—each fig to a pile—is one-to-one correspondence in its purest form.

## Reasoning Task

**The Mystery of the Ishango Bone**

Scientists have debated for decades what the Ishango bone was used for. Some think it's a lunar calendar. Others think it's a record of hunts. Some believe it might be the earliest known multiplication table.

Look at these groupings on the bone:
- Row 1: 3, 6, 4, 8, 10, 5, 5, 7 (reading left to right)
- Row 2: 11, 13, 17, 19, 7
- Row 3: 11, 21, 9, 11, 13, 17, 12, 9, 5, 8, 4, 12, 9, 11, 8

Notice that the first row contains several pairs where one number is double the other: 3 and 6, 4 and 8, 5 and 10. The second row contains mostly odd numbers. The third row is harder to interpret.

**Think about this:** If you were recording something important enough to carve into bone, what would it be? Would you record hunts (which might be different each day), or something that repeats on a cycle (like moon phases or seasons)? Why might an ancient person choose to group numbers in certain ways?

**What does the pattern of double numbers suggest?** Could this bone show that ancient mathematicians understood doubling? Or is it just coincidence?

## Application

Understanding one-to-one correspondence and tally systems isn't just about history—it's practical today:

- **Scorekeeping**: Every tally you make in a game, every hash mark on a survey sheet, uses the principle that one mark = one item
- **Inventory**: Warehouses still use tally systems (often digital now) based on counting one item per unit
- **Time tracking**: Marking off days on a calendar is a form of tallying
- **Accessibility**: People with no access to written numerals can still count using tally marks, body counting, or objects—this is why tally systems are sometimes used in real-world situations with limited resources

The ancient methods weren't crude experiments that we've outgrown. They were—and still are—profoundly useful because they rest on the deepest principle of counting: one-to-one correspondence. Every method from the Ishango bone to today's digital counters follows this rule.

## Recap

The earliest humans recorded quantities using the simplest principle: one mark for one thing. This one-to-one correspondence is the foundation of all counting.

Different cultures developed different ways to record numbers:
- **Tally marks**: carved lines, often grouped in fives
- **Body counting**: touching a fixed sequence of body parts
- **Quipu**: knotted cords in various positions and colors
- **Clay tokens**: shaped objects representing different quantities

A **number** is the quantity itself (what we're counting). A **numeral** is the symbol or system we use to represent it. The same number can be written in many ways.

Grouping marks in fives (or other units) doesn't change the principle of one-to-one correspondence—it just makes larger quantities easier to read and remember.

Understanding these ancient systems reveals the logical structure underneath modern numerals: they're all built on matching one symbol to one thing counted.
