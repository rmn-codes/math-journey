## Story Hook

Imagine you are an Egyptian scribe, around 1650 BCE, working in the Rhind Papyrus project. You have a loaf of bread and must divide it among five people. How do you describe what each person gets? Our modern answer: each person gets $\frac{1}{5}$ of the loaf. But the ancient Egyptians thought differently. They almost never wrote fractions with numerators other than 1. Instead, one scribe might write: each person gets $\frac{1}{3}$ of a loaf, plus $\frac{1}{15}$ of a loaf. Why this awkward system? Because Egyptians thought of fractions as *division of loaves*. It's much easier to imagine handing someone "one-third of this loaf" than "two-fifths of this loaf."

The evolution from Egyptian **unit fractions** to our modern system took thousands of years and multiple civilizations. And the journey reveals something beautiful: fractions are one of humanity's deepest inventions, unlocking measurement, sharing, and proportion.

## Why It Matters

The moment you stop dealing with whole numbers, fractions appear everywhere. Cooking ("add $\frac{3}{4}$ cup of flour"). Medicine ("take $\frac{1}{2}$ of a tablet twice daily"). Construction ("cut the board into $\frac{5}{8}$-inch pieces"). Music theory ("a half note, a quarter note"). Probability ("you have a $\frac{1}{6}$ chance of rolling a 1"). Finance ("this stock is up $\frac{1}{3}$ today"). Without fractions, you cannot describe parts of things, and the world is full of parts.

More fundamentally, fractions introduce a new number system. The whole numbers end — there's always a largest one you're working with — but fractions fill the gaps. Between 0 and 1, there are infinitely many fractions. This idea of *density* — that no matter how close two things are, something fits between them — became crucial to calculus, physics, and advanced mathematics.

## Historical Context

The **Rhind Papyrus** (circa 1650 BCE), a 33-foot scroll now in the British Museum, shows that ancient Egyptians used fractions, but almost exclusively unit fractions ($\frac{1}{n}$). The papyrus contains tables for converting other fractions to sums of unit fractions. To an Egyptian mathematician, $\frac{2}{5}$ wasn't fundamental; instead, they decomposed it as $\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{15}$.

Meanwhile, the **Babylonians** (1800–1200 BCE) used a *base-60 system* for fractions, similar to how we measure angles (60 seconds in a minute, 60 minutes in an hour). This is why clock time persists in base-60 to this day.

**Indian mathematicians**, particularly **Aryabhata** (476 CE) and **Bhaskara II** (1114–1185 CE), developed the notation and arithmetic we recognize today: a numerator above a denominator, with rules for addition, subtraction, and multiplication. Bhaskara's work, the *Lilavati*, contains sophisticated fraction problems and proofs.

**Islamic scholars** translated and extended these Indian ideas. **Al-Khwarizmi** (circa 780–850 CE), whose name gives us the word "algorithm," systematized fraction arithmetic. His work, transmitted back to Europe via medieval scholars, became the foundation of Renaissance mathematics.

## Core Concept

A **fraction** $\frac{a}{b}$ represents a part of something. The **numerator** $a$ (top) tells you *how many* parts. The **denominator** $b$ (bottom) tells you *how many equal parts the whole is divided into*.

A fraction can mean three different things:

1. **Part of a whole:** $\frac{3}{8}$ of a pizza means the pizza is divided into 8 equal slices, and you have 3 of them.

2. **Part of a set:** If there are 12 people and 5 are teachers, then $\frac{5}{12}$ of the group are teachers.

3. **Division:** $\frac{3}{8}$ also means $3 \div 8$, which equals 0.375.

All three interpretations are equivalent and powerful.

**Equivalent fractions** represent the same quantity: $\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{50}{100}$. You create equivalent fractions by multiplying (or dividing) both numerator and denominator by the same nonzero number.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Fraction:** A number of the form $\frac{a}{b}$ where $a$ (numerator) and $b$ (denominator, $b \neq 0$) are integers.

**Unit Fraction:** A fraction of the form $\frac{1}{n}$ where $n > 0$.

**Equivalent Fractions:** Fractions that represent the same value. If $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$, then $a \cdot d = b \cdot c$ (cross-multiply).

**Simplest Form (Lowest Terms):** A fraction $\frac{a}{b}$ is in simplest form if GCF$(a, b) = 1$.

**Operations on Fractions:**

*Adding/Subtracting with Common Denominator:*
$$\frac{a}{b} + \frac{c}{b} = \frac{a+c}{b} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{a}{b} - \frac{c}{b} = \frac{a-c}{b}$$

*Adding/Subtracting with Unlike Denominators:* Find a common denominator (LCM of the denominators), convert, then add/subtract numerators.

*Multiplying:*
$$\frac{a}{b} \times \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ac}{bd}$$

*Dividing (Multiply by the Reciprocal):*
$$\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c} = \frac{ad}{bc}$$

*Comparing:* To compare $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$, cross-multiply: if $ad > bc$, then $\frac{a}{b} > \frac{c}{d}$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Simplifying a Fraction**

Simplify $\frac{24}{36}$.

Find GCF(24, 36). We know from earlier that GCF(24, 36) = 12.

$$\frac{24}{36} = \frac{24 \div 12}{36 \div 12} = \frac{2}{3}$$

**Example 2: Adding Fractions with Different Denominators**

Add $\frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{4}$.

Find a common denominator. LCM(3, 4) = 12.

$$\frac{2}{3} = \frac{8}{12} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{12}$$

$$\frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{8}{12} + \frac{3}{12} = \frac{11}{12}$$

**Example 3: Multiplying Fractions**

Multiply $\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{5}$.

$$\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{5} = \frac{2 \times 3}{3 \times 5} = \frac{6}{15} = \frac{2}{5}$$

Notice: the 3's cancelled. This is allowed because $\frac{3}{3} = 1$.

**Example 4: Dividing Fractions**

Divide $\frac{5}{6} \div \frac{2}{3}$.

Multiply by the reciprocal:

$$\frac{5}{6} \div \frac{2}{3} = \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{5 \times 3}{6 \times 2} = \frac{15}{12} = \frac{5}{4}$$

**Example 5: Comparing Fractions**

Which is larger: $\frac{5}{8}$ or $\frac{7}{12}$?

Cross-multiply: $5 \times 12 = 60$ and $7 \times 8 = 56$. Since $60 > 56$, we have $\frac{5}{8} > \frac{7}{12}$.

## Application

**Real-World Scenario: Cooking and Scaling Recipes**

You find a recipe for cookies that makes 24 cookies and calls for:
- $\frac{3}{4}$ cup of butter
- $\frac{1}{2}$ cup of sugar
- $1\frac{1}{2}$ cups of flour
- $\frac{1}{4}$ cup of cocoa powder

But you want to make only 12 cookies (half the recipe). You multiply each ingredient by $\frac{1}{2}$:

- Butter: $\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{8}$ cup
- Sugar: $\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$ cup
- Flour: $\frac{3}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{4}$ cup
- Cocoa: $\frac{1}{4} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{8}$ cup

This fraction arithmetic — scaling, multiplying, measuring — is essential in cooking. Chefs and bakers think in fractions constantly. The same principles apply when scaling up: if you want 36 cookies (1.5 times the recipe), you multiply each ingredient by $\frac{3}{2}$.

## Recap

A fraction $\frac{a}{b}$ represents a part of a whole, a part of a set, or the division $a \div b$. Equivalent fractions look different but have the same value. Simplifying means dividing numerator and denominator by their GCF. To add or subtract, find a common denominator. Multiplying fractions is straightforward: multiply numerators and multiply denominators. Dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal. These operations underlie countless real-world tasks: cooking, measuring, scheduling, sharing, and financial calculations. The evolution from unit fractions to modern fraction notation took millennia and multiple cultures, reflecting how fundamental and hard-won this mathematical tool truly is.

## Unlock Quiz

1. Simplify $\frac{15}{25}$ to lowest terms.
   - $\frac{3}{5}$
   - $\frac{5}{10}$
   - $\frac{6}{10}$
   - $\frac{15}{25}$ (already simplified)

2. Add $\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{3}$.
   - $\frac{2}{7}$
   - $\frac{1}{12}$
   - $\frac{7}{12}$
   - $\frac{2}{12}$

3. Multiply $\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{5}$.
   - $\frac{6}{20}$
   - $\frac{5}{9}$
   - $\frac{3}{10}$
   - $\frac{6}{9}$

4. Divide $\frac{2}{3} \div \frac{1}{6}$.
   - $\frac{2}{18}$
   - $\frac{4}{6}$
   - $4$
   - $\frac{1}{9}$

5. Which is greater: $\frac{7}{10}$ or $\frac{4}{5}$?
   - $\frac{7}{10}$ is greater
   - $\frac{4}{5}$ is greater
   - They are equal
   - Cannot be determined without a calculator

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **$\frac{3}{5}$** — GCF(15, 25) = 5. So $\frac{15}{25} = \frac{15 \div 5}{25 \div 5} = \frac{3}{5}$.

2. **$\frac{7}{12}$** — LCM(4, 3) = 12. $\frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{12}$ and $\frac{1}{3} = \frac{4}{12}$. So $\frac{3}{12} + \frac{4}{12} = \frac{7}{12}$.

3. **$\frac{3}{10}$** — $\frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{5} = \frac{3 \times 2}{4 \times 5} = \frac{6}{20} = \frac{3}{10}$ (simplified).

4. **$4$** — $\frac{2}{3} \div \frac{1}{6} = \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{6}{1} = \frac{12}{3} = 4$.

5. **$\frac{4}{5}$ is greater** — Cross-multiply: $7 \times 5 = 35$ and $4 \times 10 = 40$. Since $40 > 35$, we have $\frac{4}{5} > \frac{7}{10}$.
