## Story Hook

Imagine yourself a scribe in ancient Egypt, around 3000 years ago. The Nile River has flooded, and grain has been delivered from villages across the kingdom. You sit with papyrus and reed pen, recording each delivery:

- Village of Sais: 247 baskets
- Village of Memphis: 183 baskets
- Village of Thebes: 156 baskets

Your job: add them up. How much grain arrived in total? Then comes the harder part—the pharaoh's granaries need to distribute food to workers building the pyramids. You must subtract. After feeding 300 workers for a month, how much remains? Your arithmetic is not academic—it feeds nations. A scribe who miscalculates could lose a hand.

This is why addition and subtraction matter. They are the heartbeat of human organization.

## Why It Matters

Before writing and money, addition and subtraction were already essential. Whenever humans had multiple quantities and needed to know "how much altogether?" or "what's left?", they were doing addition and subtraction.

Today, addition and subtraction remain foundational. Bank accounts, recipe quantities, construction measurements, budgeting, sports scores—all rely on these two operations. More importantly, addition and subtraction are the stepping stones to multiplication, division, algebra, and calculus. Master them now, and the rest of mathematics becomes possible.

Understanding *why* these operations work—not just how to perform them—gives you the mathematical confidence to solve new problems you've never seen before.

## Historical Context

The earliest evidence of addition and subtraction comes from ancient Mesopotamia. Clay tablets from around 3000 BCE show scribes recording quantities—sheep, grain, textiles—and combining them. The Egyptians went further, developing careful methods for column arithmetic. By 1650 BCE, the Rhind Mathematical Papyrus documented Egyptian techniques for both adding and subtracting large numbers.

In India, mathematicians around 500 CE refined the place-value system and created algorithms for addition and subtraction that we still teach today. The Indian system proved so elegant that it spread along trade routes to the Islamic world and eventually to Europe. What you learn in school today is, in many ways, what Indian mathematicians perfected 1,500 years ago.

## Core Concept

### Addition as Combining

Addition answers: "If I have some and get some more, how many do I have altogether?"

$$a + b = c$$

Here, $a$ and $b$ are called *addends*, and $c$ is the *sum*.

**The Commutative Property of Addition:** The order doesn't matter.

$$a + b = b + a$$

$$3 + 5 = 5 + 3 = 8$$

This seems obvious, but it's powerful. It means you can add numbers in any order—a property that makes mental math and verification easier.

**The Associative Property of Addition:** How you group addends doesn't change the sum.

$$(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$$

$$(2 + 3) + 4 = 2 + (3 + 4) = 9$$

Again, this lets you add in smart ways. If you're adding $25 + 17 + 3$, you might group it as $25 + (17 + 3) = 25 + 20 = 45$ instead of going left to right.

### Subtraction as Taking Away

Subtraction answers: "If I have some and remove some, how many remain?" or "What's the difference between two amounts?"

$$a - b = c$$

Here, $a$ is the *minuend*, $b$ is the *subtrahend*, and $c$ is the *difference*.

**Subtraction as the Inverse of Addition:**

Subtraction undoes addition. If $5 + 3 = 8$, then $8 - 3 = 5$.

Formally: If $a + b = c$, then $c - a = b$ and $c - b = a$.

This relationship is crucial. It means subtraction always has a check: multiplication by the addends should recover the original total.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Column Addition with Regrouping

When adding multi-digit numbers, align them by place value:

```
  247
+ 183
-----
```

Start with the ones place: $7 + 3 = 10$. Write the $0$ in the ones place and carry the $1$ (the ten) to the tens place.

```
   ₁
  247
+ 183
-----
   0
```

Tens place: $1 + 4 + 8 = 13$. Write the $3$ in the tens place and carry the $1$ (the hundred) to the hundreds place.

```
  ₁₁
  247
+ 183
-----
  30
```

Hundreds place: $1 + 2 + 1 = 4$. Write the $4$.

```
  247
+ 183
-----
  430
```

The sum is $430$. We *carried* (also called *regrouping*) when the column sum exceeded 9.

### Column Subtraction with Regrouping

When subtracting multi-digit numbers:

```
  430
- 156
-----
```

Ones place: $0 - 6$. We can't do this, so we *borrow* 1 ten (regroup). The $3$ tens becomes $2$ tens, and the $0$ ones becomes $10$ ones.

$10 - 6 = 4$.

```
  4 2¹⁰
  430
- 156
-----
    4
```

Tens place: $2 - 5$. We can't do this, so we borrow 1 hundred. The $4$ hundreds becomes $3$ hundreds, and the $2$ tens becomes $12$ tens.

$12 - 5 = 7$.

```
  3¹ 2¹⁰
  430
- 156
-----
   74
```

Hundreds place: $3 - 1 = 2$.

```
  430
- 156
-----
  274
```

The difference is $274$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Multi-digit Addition with Carrying**

Add $528 + 347$.

```
    ₁₁
   528
 + 347
 -----
   875
```

- Ones: $8 + 7 = 15$. Write $5$, carry $1$.
- Tens: $1 + 2 + 4 = 7$. Write $7$.
- Hundreds: $5 + 3 = 8$. Write $8$.
- Sum: $875$.

**Example 2: Multi-digit Subtraction with Borrowing**

Subtract $625 - 348$.

```
  5¹ 1¹⁰
  625
- 348
-----
  277
```

- Ones: $5 - 8$. Can't do it. Borrow: ones become $15$, tens become $1$. $15 - 8 = 7$.
- Tens: $1 - 4$. Can't do it. Borrow: tens become $11$, hundreds become $5$. $11 - 4 = 7$.
- Hundreds: $5 - 3 = 2$.
- Difference: $277$.

**Example 3: Word Problem**

At the farmer's market, Sarah buys $12$ apples, then picks up $8$ more. How many apples does she have?

$$12 + 8 = 20$$

Sarah has $20$ apples.

**Example 4: Word Problem with Subtraction**

Sarah had $20$ apples. She used $7$ for pie. How many remain?

$$20 - 7 = 13$$

Sarah has $13$ apples left.

## Reasoning Task

**Challenge:** A bakery made 250 loaves of bread on Monday, 187 on Tuesday, and 203 on Wednesday. After selling 428 loaves, how many remain?

First, add the loaves made over the three days. Then subtract the loaves sold. Show your work and verify your answer by checking that sold loaves + remaining loaves = total loaves made.

## Application

**Scenario:** You're managing supplies for a school event. You have:
- 145 paper cups from one box
- 238 paper cups from another box

You need to set up 50 tables with 8 cups each. Do you have enough? How many will you have left over (or how many short)?

Solve step by step. First, find the total cups. Then, find how many are needed. Then, subtract to find the remainder.

## Recap

Addition and subtraction are inverse operations—they undo each other. Addition combines quantities; subtraction finds what's left or the difference between two amounts. Both operations follow commutative and associative properties (for addition), which let us compute flexibly and check our work. Multi-digit addition uses carrying (regrouping) when a column sum exceeds 9; multi-digit subtraction uses borrowing when we need to subtract a larger digit from a smaller one. These methods, developed over millennia by Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and Indian mathematicians, remain the foundation of calculation today.
