## Story Hook

Captain Elena pilots her plane due east at 500 km/h. It's a clear day over the Atlantic.

An hour later, she checks her GPS. She's traveled 500 km, but not due east. She's drifted southeast. Why?

Wind. A steady breeze from the north, 80 km/h, has pushed her off course.

To find where she *really* goes, Elena can't just add 500 + 80. Speed isn't enough — direction matters. She has to add two arrows: one pointing east (her velocity), one pointing south (the wind's effect). The tail of one touches the tip of the other. The diagonal arrow from start to finish — that's her true path.

This is the work of **vectors**: quantities that have both magnitude (500 km/h) and direction (east). Nearly everything in physics — force, velocity, acceleration, displacement, electric and magnetic fields — is a vector. Trigonometry gave us angles; vectors give us the mathematics to combine them.

---

## Why It Matters

- **Navigation**: Pilots, sailors, and GPS systems use vectors constantly to account for wind, currents, and drift.
- **Physics**: Force, momentum, and acceleration are vectors. Understanding how they combine is understanding how the world moves.
- **Engineering**: Stress and strain in materials, forces on bridges, airflow in tunnels — all vectors.
- **Computer graphics**: Every 3D video game uses vectors to position objects, calculate lighting, and detect collisions.
- **Machine learning**: High-dimensional data lives in vector spaces. Modern AI thinks in vectors.

Without vectors, we'd be limited to one-dimensional motion. The real world is three-dimensional, and vectors are how we describe it.

## Historical Context

For millennia, mathematicians thought of geometry and arithmetic as separate. Descartes changed that in the 1600s: place a coordinate system, and geometry becomes algebra (analytic geometry).

By the 1800s, William Rowan Hamilton (Ireland), Hermann Grassmann (Germany), and others realized that pairs and triples of numbers — *components* — could be manipulated algebraically. They built the language of vectors: addition, multiplication by scalars, the dot product.

James Clerk Maxwell then revealed that the language of vectors was not just convenient — it was essential. His equations of electricity and magnetism, written in vector form, were simpler and more beautiful than any scalar version could be. Vectors became the physics language, and they've stayed that way.

## Core Concept

A **vector** is a quantity with two properties: magnitude (size) and direction.

**Representation:**
- **Geometric:** An arrow. The length is magnitude; the direction is, well, the direction.
- **Component form:** A pair (or triple) of numbers: $\vec{v} = \langle a, b \rangle$ in 2D, or $\vec{v} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$ in 3D.

The component form is what makes vectors computable. If $\vec{v} = \langle 3, 4 \rangle$, it means: go 3 units right and 4 units up.

**Magnitude:**
The length of a vector is its magnitude (also called norm or modulus):
$$|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$$

For $\vec{v} = \langle 3, 4 \rangle$: $|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$.

**Unit Vector:**
A vector with magnitude 1 is called a **unit vector**. To make any vector into a unit vector, divide by its magnitude:
$$\hat{v} = \frac{\vec{v}}{|\vec{v}|}$$

This gives the direction of $\vec{v}$ without the magnitude. Often written with a hat ($\hat{v}$) or a caret.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Vector Addition (Component-wise)**
$$\vec{u} + \vec{v} = \langle u_1 + v_1, u_2 + v_2 \rangle$$

*Geometric interpretation:* Place the tail of $\vec{v}$ at the tip of $\vec{u}$. The sum points from the tail of $\vec{u}$ to the tip of $\vec{v}$. This is the **tip-to-tail** rule.

**Scalar Multiplication**
$$c \vec{v} = \langle c v_1, c v_2 \rangle$$

If $c > 0$, the direction stays the same; the magnitude scales by $c$. If $c < 0$, the direction flips.

**Dot Product (Scalar Product)**
$$\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2$$

Algebraically, multiply corresponding components and sum. Geometrically:
$$\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = |\vec{u}| |\vec{v}| \cos(\theta)$$

where $\theta$ is the angle *between* the two vectors.

**Angle Between Vectors**
From the dot product formula:
$$\cos(\theta) = \frac{\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}}{|\vec{u}| |\vec{v}|}$$
$$\theta = \arccos\left(\frac{\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}}{|\vec{u}| |\vec{v}|}\right)$$

**Perpendicular Vectors**
Two vectors are perpendicular (orthogonal) if and only if their dot product is zero:
$$\vec{u} \perp \vec{v} \iff \vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 0$$

**Parallel Vectors**
Two vectors are parallel if one is a scalar multiple of the other:
$$\vec{u} = c \vec{v} \text{ for some scalar } c$$

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Vector addition with wind**

A plane flies east at $\vec{v}_{\text{plane}} = \langle 500, 0 \rangle$ km/h. Wind from the north at $\vec{v}_{\text{wind}} = \langle 0, -80 \rangle$ km/h (negative because it pushes south).

The true velocity is:
$$\vec{v}_{\text{true}} = \vec{v}_{\text{plane}} + \vec{v}_{\text{wind}} = \langle 500, 0 \rangle + \langle 0, -80 \rangle = \langle 500, -80 \rangle \text{ km/h}$$

Magnitude:
$$|\vec{v}_{\text{true}}| = \sqrt{500^2 + 80^2} = \sqrt{250000 + 6400} = \sqrt{256400} \approx 506.4 \text{ km/h}$$

The plane's true speed is about 506.4 km/h, southeastward.

**Example 2: Angle between two vectors**

Let $\vec{u} = \langle 1, 0 \rangle$ and $\vec{v} = \langle 1, 1 \rangle$. Find the angle between them.

Dot product: $\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = 1(1) + 0(1) = 1$.

Magnitudes: $|\vec{u}| = 1$, $|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}$.

$$\cos(\theta) = \frac{1}{1 \cdot \sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$
$$\theta = \arccos\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) = 45°$$

**Example 3: Unit vector**

Find the unit vector in the direction of $\vec{w} = \langle 3, 4 \rangle$.

Magnitude: $|\vec{w}| = 5$ (from Example 1 above).

$$\hat{w} = \frac{\vec{w}}{|\vec{w}|} = \frac{\langle 3, 4 \rangle}{5} = \langle 0.6, 0.8 \rangle$$

Check: $|\hat{w}| = \sqrt{0.6^2 + 0.8^2} = \sqrt{0.36 + 0.64} = \sqrt{1} = 1$. ✓

## Application

**Bridge Engineering**

A cable hangs from a support at an angle of 60° below horizontal. The tension in the cable is 1000 N (the magnitude of the force vector). What are the horizontal and vertical components of the force?

The force vector has magnitude 1000 N and points at an angle of -60° from horizontal (below the horizontal).

$$\vec{F} = \langle 1000 \cos(-60°), 1000 \sin(-60°) \rangle$$
$$= \langle 1000(0.5), 1000(-0.866) \rangle$$
$$= \langle 500, -866 \rangle \text{ N}$$

The horizontal component is 500 N (tension pulling sideways), and the vertical component is -866 N (pulling downward). Engineers use these components to ensure the support can withstand the force.

## Recap

- **Vectors** have magnitude (size) and direction, represented as ordered pairs or triples.
- **Component form:** $\vec{v} = \langle a, b \rangle$. **Magnitude:** $|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$.
- **Vector addition** combines magnitudes and directions component-wise (or tip-to-tail geometrically).
- **Dot product:** $\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v} = |\vec{u}| |\vec{v}| \cos(\theta)$ encodes the angle between vectors.
- **Perpendicular vectors** have dot product zero. **Parallel vectors** are scalar multiples of each other.
- **Applications:** navigation, physics, engineering, computer graphics, machine learning.

Vectors are the natural language for anything that has both size and direction. They transform complicated geometric reasoning into straightforward algebra, and they're the foundation for nearly all of physics.
