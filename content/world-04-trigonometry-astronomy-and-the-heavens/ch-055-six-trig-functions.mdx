## Story Hook

In 9th century Baghdad, the mathematician al-Battani faced a challenge. The Greeks had developed sine and cosine by thinking about chords in circles. But al-Battani was surveying territory and measuring heights of mountains and buildings. For these practical problems, what mattered was not the vertical and horizontal coordinates of a point on a circle — what mattered was the **slope** of the line from the observer's eye to the peak.

That slope is what we now call the tangent function, and al-Battani's introduction of it transformed trigonometry from a tool for astronomy into a tool for the entire world. When a surveyor sights up to a peak at an angle of 30 degrees, it's the tangent of 30 degrees that tells the surveyor how high that peak is compared to its distance away.

Today, we use six trigonometric functions, not just two. And they solve nearly every problem that involves angles, heights, distances, and slopes.

## Why It Matters

The six trigonometric functions are the keys to solving triangles. Any time you have a triangle with one angle and one side known, you can find the rest. This applies to:

- Surveyors measuring heights and distances
- Engineers designing bridges and buildings
- Pilots calculating headings and altitudes
- Mapmakers projecting the curved Earth onto flat paper
- Navigation systems in aircraft and ships
- Video game programmers calculating where objects are and how they move

The six functions arise because a right triangle has three sides. Any pair of sides gives us a ratio, and those ratios — sine, cosine, tangent, and their reciprocals — are the six functions.

## Historical Context

Al-Battani (c. 858–929 CE), working in the Islamic Golden Age, is credited with introducing the tangent function. He was an astronomer and mathematician, but his work was driven by the practical needs of surveying, navigation, and determining the direction of prayer toward Mecca, which requires precise angle and distance calculations.

The Persian mathematician Abu al-Wafa (940–998 CE) further developed trigonometric tables and introduced all six functions, though not quite in the form we know today. He used the name "tangent" (meaning "touching") because the tangent line touches the unit circle.

By the 1600s, European mathematicians like Edmund Gunter had standardized notation and developed tables for all six functions. These tables were essential before calculators — sailors, surveyors, and engineers carried them everywhere.

The memory device **SOH-CAH-TOA** is modern (20th century), but the mathematical relationships it encodes go back a thousand years.

## Core Concept

In a right triangle with an acute angle $\theta$:

- **Opposite** ($\text{opp}$): the side across from $\theta$
- **Adjacent** ($\text{adj}$): the side next to $\theta$ (but not the hypotenuse)
- **Hypotenuse** ($\text{hyp}$): the longest side, across from the right angle

The six trigonometric functions are ratios of these sides:

$$\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}}, \quad \cos\theta = \frac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}}, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}$$

$$\csc\theta = \frac{\text{hyp}}{\text{opp}}, \quad \sec\theta = \frac{\text{hyp}}{\text{adj}}, \quad \cot\theta = \frac{\text{adj}}{\text{opp}}$$

Notice that the last three are reciprocals of the first three: $\csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}$, and so on.

**SOH-CAH-TOA** is the mnemonic:
- **S**ine = **O**pposite/**H**ypotenuse
- **C**osine = **A**djacent/**H**ypotenuse
- **T**angent = **O**pposite/**A**djacent

### Angle of Elevation and Depression

**Angle of elevation** is the angle above the horizontal when looking up at an object.

**Angle of depression** is the angle below the horizontal when looking down at an object.

These angles are equal by alternate interior angles if you imagine the horizontal line from the observer and the line of sight forming angles with a horizontal line at the distant object.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### The Six Trigonometric Functions (Right Triangle Form)

For an acute angle $\theta$ in a right triangle:

| Function | Ratio |
|----------|-------|
| $\sin\theta$ | $\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$ |
| $\cos\theta$ | $\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$ |
| $\tan\theta$ | $\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}$ |
| $\csc\theta$ | $\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{opposite}}$ |
| $\sec\theta$ | $\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{adjacent}}$ |
| $\cot\theta$ | $\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{opposite}}$ |

### Reciprocal Relationships

$$\csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}, \quad \sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta}, \quad \cot\theta = \frac{1}{\tan\theta}$$

### Unit Circle Form

Using the unit circle, for any angle $\theta$ (not just acute angles):

$$\sin\theta = y\text{-coordinate}, \quad \cos\theta = x\text{-coordinate}, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = \frac{y}{x}$$

(when $\cos\theta \neq 0$)

### Finding All Six Values From One

If you know one trig function value and the quadrant, you can find all six. Use the Pythagorean identity and reciprocal relationships.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: In a right triangle, the side opposite $\theta$ is 5 and the hypotenuse is 13. Find all six trig values.**

First, find the adjacent side using the Pythagorean theorem:
$$\text{adj}^2 + 5^2 = 13^2$$
$$\text{adj}^2 = 169 - 25 = 144$$
$$\text{adj} = 12$$

Now find each ratio:
$$\sin\theta = \frac{5}{13}, \quad \cos\theta = \frac{12}{13}, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{5}{12}$$
$$\csc\theta = \frac{13}{5}, \quad \sec\theta = \frac{13}{12}, \quad \cot\theta = \frac{12}{5}$$

**Example 2: A ladder leans against a wall. The ladder is 20 feet long and makes a $60°$ angle with the ground. How high up the wall does the ladder reach?**

The ladder is the hypotenuse (20 feet). The angle with the ground is $60°$. The height is the opposite side.

$$\sin 60° = \frac{\text{height}}{20}$$
$$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{\text{height}}{20}$$
$$\text{height} = 10\sqrt{3} \approx 17.3 \text{ feet}$$

**Example 3: From a point on level ground 30 meters from the base of a building, the angle of elevation to the top is $55°$. How tall is the building?**

The adjacent side (horizontal distance) is 30 meters. The angle of elevation is $55°$. We want the opposite side (height).

$$\tan 55° = \frac{\text{height}}{30}$$
$$\text{height} = 30 \tan 55° \approx 30 \times 1.428 \approx 42.8 \text{ meters}$$

**Example 4: If $\sin\theta = \frac{2}{3}$ and $\theta$ is acute, find the other five trig values.**

Use the Pythagorean identity to find $\cos\theta$:
$$\cos^2\theta = 1 - \sin^2\theta = 1 - \frac{4}{9} = \frac{5}{9}$$
$$\cos\theta = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{3}$$ (positive since $\theta$ is acute)

Now find the others:
$$\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = \frac{2/3}{\sqrt{5}/3} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{5}} = \frac{2\sqrt{5}}{5}$$
$$\csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta} = \frac{3}{2}, \quad \sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{5}} = \frac{3\sqrt{5}}{5}, \quad \cot\theta = \frac{1}{\tan\theta} = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}$$

**Example 5: Solve the right triangle: one leg is 7, the hypotenuse is 12. Find the other leg and both acute angles.**

$$\text{leg}^2 + 7^2 = 12^2$$
$$\text{leg}^2 = 144 - 49 = 95$$
$$\text{leg} = \sqrt{95} \approx 9.75$$

For one angle $\theta$:
$$\sin\theta = \frac{7}{12}$$
$$\theta = \arcsin\left(\frac{7}{12}\right) \approx 35.7°$$

The other acute angle is $90° - 35.7° = 54.3°$.

## Application

A surveyor is measuring the height of a cliff. She stands at a point 80 meters from the base of the cliff and measures the angle of elevation to the top as $42°$. What is the height of the cliff?

**Solution:**
The adjacent side (horizontal distance) is 80 meters. The angle is $42°$. The opposite side (height) is what we want.

$$\tan 42° = \frac{\text{height}}{80}$$
$$\text{height} = 80 \tan 42°$$
$$\text{height} \approx 80 \times 0.900 \approx 72 \text{ meters}$$

## Recap

- **SOH-CAH-TOA**: $\sin = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}}$, $\cos = \frac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}}$, $\tan = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}$
- **Reciprocals**: $\csc = \frac{1}{\sin}$, $\sec = \frac{1}{\cos}$, $\cot = \frac{1}{\tan}$
- **Tangent as slope**: $\tan\theta = \text{rise/run} = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}$
- **Solving right triangles**: Use trig functions and the Pythagorean theorem to find missing sides and angles.
- **Angles of elevation and depression**: Equal by alternate interior angles.
