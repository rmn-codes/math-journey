## Story Hook

Maxwell's equations. Four lines of mathematics. Written in 1865 by James Clerk Maxwell, a Scottish physicist sitting in his study.

$$\nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}$$
$$\nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0$$
$$\nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}$$
$$\nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}$$

These four equations describe all of electricity and magnetism. And from them, Maxwell derived something miraculous: a wave equation. Light, he realized, is not a stream of particles. Light is an oscillating electromagnetic field — a *wave* — traveling through space at a fixed speed.

The prediction seemed wild. But in 1887, Heinrich Hertz, a young physicist in Germany, proved Maxwell right. Hertz generated electromagnetic waves in his lab and measured their properties. Radio, television, WiFi, X-rays, visible light itself — all electromagnetic waves, all described by sine and cosine functions.

From ancient astronomers measuring angles in triangles to Maxwell describing the structure of light itself — this is the extraordinary journey of trigonometry.

---

## Why It Matters

Trigonometry began as a practical tool for measurement. It's become the language of the physical universe.

- **Electromagnetism:** Maxwell's equations, written in vector form, use sines and cosines to describe fields that oscillate.
- **Quantum mechanics:** The wave function — the core object of quantum theory — is a sinusoidal function of position and time.
- **General relativity:** Einstein's theory of gravity describes spacetime curvature. The mathematics involves periodic functions and, ultimately, sinusoids.
- **Modern technology:** Every smartphone, every WiFi signal, every MRI scan, every neural network relies on mathematics descended from trigonometry.

We've climbed from the concrete (angles in triangles) to the abstract (waves in spacetime). The tools that measured the Earth now measure the cosmos.

## Historical Context

**Antiquity to Renaissance:** Trigonometry was geometry. Hipparchus, Ptolemy, Al-Battani — astronomers who needed to measure angles in the sky, computed tables of chords and sines.

**1600s–1700s:** The unit circle and graphs of trigonometric functions emerged. Euler introduced the notation $\sin x$, $\cos x$, and proved identities. Calculus was born (Newton and Leibniz), and the derivatives of sine and cosine became central.

**1800s:** Fourier showed any periodic function is a sum of sines and cosines. Electricity and magnetism were unified by Maxwell in 1865. Hertz confirmed the existence of electromagnetic waves.

**1900s onward:** Quantum mechanics (Schrödinger, Heisenberg, Dirac) revealed that matter itself is wave-like. General relativity uses periodic functions to describe spacetime. The Fourier transform became the workhorse of signal processing, enabling digital technology.

Today, trigonometry is not a subject about triangles. It's the mathematics of oscillation, wave propagation, and the deep structure of reality.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Euler's Formula** — One of the most beautiful equations in mathematics:
$$e^{i\theta} = \cos(\theta) + i \sin(\theta)$$

where $e$ is the mathematical constant (≈ 2.718), $i$ is the imaginary unit ($i^2 = -1$), and $\theta$ is an angle in radians.

This formula connects five fundamental mathematical constants:
- $e$ (the base of natural logarithms)
- $i$ (the imaginary unit)
- $\pi$ (the ratio of circumference to diameter)
- $1$ (multiplicative identity)
- $0$ (additive identity)

The most famous consequence (setting $\theta = \pi$):
$$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$

A perfect balance of the five constants.

**Maxwell's Equations** (Conceptual Form):

The divergence equations relate charge and field:
$$\nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \rho/\epsilon_0 \quad \text{(Gauss's law)}$$
$$\nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0 \quad \text{(No magnetic monopoles)}$$

The curl equations relate changing fields:
$$\nabla \times \vec{E} = -\partial \vec{B}/\partial t \quad \text{(Faraday's law)}$$
$$\nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \partial \vec{E}/\partial t \quad \text{(Ampère-Maxwell law)}$$

From these, one can derive the wave equation:
$$\frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \nabla^2 \vec{E}$$

where $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}$ is the speed of light. The solutions are sinusoidal waves.

**Schrödinger Equation** (Time-independent, 1D):
$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2 \psi}{dx^2} + V(x) \psi = E \psi$$

The wave function $\psi(x)$ is often sinusoidal (or a sum of sinusoids). Its square $|\psi(x)|^2$ gives the probability density of finding a particle at position $x$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Deriving the speed of light from Maxwell's equations**

Maxwell's equations, in vacuum (no charges, no currents), yield two wave equations. Taking the curl of Faraday's law and substituting Ampère-Maxwell law:
$$\nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{E}) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \times \vec{B})$$

After algebraic manipulation (using vector identities), this becomes:
$$\nabla^2 \vec{E} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2}$$

This is a wave equation of the form $\nabla^2 f = v^{-2} \partial^2 f / \partial t^2$, where $v$ is the wave speed.

Comparing: $v = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}$.

From measurements of $\mu_0$ (permeability of free space) and $\epsilon_0$ (permittivity of free space), the speed comes out to approximately $3 \times 10^8$ m/s — exactly the measured speed of light!

Maxwell concluded: light is an electromagnetic wave.

**Example 2: A plane electromagnetic wave**

A sinusoidal electromagnetic wave traveling in the +x direction:
$$\vec{E}(x, t) = E_0 \cos(kx - \omega t) \hat{y}$$
$$\vec{B}(x, t) = \frac{E_0}{c} \cos(kx - \omega t) \hat{z}$$

where $k = 2\pi/\lambda$ is the wave number, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is the angular frequency, and $\lambda$ and $f$ are wavelength and frequency.

For visible light: $f \approx 5 \times 10^{14}$ Hz (500 nanometers wavelength). For radio: $f \approx 10^6$ Hz (300 meters wavelength). Same physics; different frequency.

**Example 3: Euler's formula in action**

Represent the complex number $1 + i$ in exponential form.

First, convert to polar: $|1+i| = \sqrt{2}$, and $\arg(1+i) = \arctan(1/1) = 45° = \pi/4$.

So: $1 + i = \sqrt{2} e^{i\pi/4}$.

By Euler's formula:
$$1 + i = \sqrt{2}(\cos(\pi/4) + i \sin(\pi/4)) = \sqrt{2}(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} + i \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}) = 1 + i$$ ✓

## Application

**GPS and Navigation**

Your smartphone's GPS receiver calculates your location by measuring signals from multiple satellites. Each satellite transmits an electromagnetic wave (a sinusoidal signal) at a specific frequency. The receiver compares the phase (the sinusoidal position within a cycle) of signals from four or more satellites to triangulate your position.

The mathematics:
- **Satellite signal:** $s(t) = \sin(\omega_{\text{sat}} t)$
- **Received signal (with phase delay):** $r(t) = \sin(\omega_{\text{sat}} (t - \Delta t))$
- **Phase difference:** $\Delta \phi = \omega_{\text{sat}} \Delta t$ encodes the signal travel time
- **Distance:** $d = c \Delta t$ (where $c$ is the speed of light)

From multiple satellites, the GPS receiver solves a system of equations to find your latitude, longitude, and altitude. The entire system — from Einstein's relativity (which predicts that time runs slightly differently on satellites) to Fourier's decomposition of signals to Maxwell's electromagnetic waves to Euler's complex exponentials — rests on trigonometry.

## Recap

- **The arc of trigonometry:** From angle measurement (ancient) → periodic functions (1600s) → oscillations and waves (1800s) → electromagnetic theory, quantum mechanics, and the deep structure of reality (1900s–present).
- **Euler's formula:** $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$. Connects exponentials, complex numbers, and trigonometric functions.
- **Maxwell's equations:** Four equations describing all of electricity and magnetism, with sines and cosines in the wave solutions.
- **Fourier analysis:** Any periodic signal is a sum of sinusoids. The foundation of digital signal processing.
- **Applications:** Light, radio, WiFi, X-rays, MRI, GPS, quantum mechanics, general relativity, neural networks, image compression.

Trigonometry began as a tool to measure triangles. It became the language of waves, oscillations, and the fundamental forces. Today, it underpins nearly all modern technology and the deepest physics we know.

This is not the end of the journey. Calculus — the next World — will deepen these ideas, showing why sine and cosine are their own derivatives, and introducing Fourier transforms and differential equations that govern motion and change. But the foundation is trigonometry. The angle — simple, eternal, universal — is woven into the fabric of the universe.

- The color of the light
- The distance from the satellite
- The spin of the satellite
- The temperature of the atmosphere

**Question 4:** What is the speed of light according to $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}$?
- $1/\sqrt{2}$ m/s
- Approximately $3 \times 10^8$ m/s
- Approximately $3 \times 10^6$ m/s
- Infinity

**Question 5:** The wave equation derived from Maxwell's equations has solutions of the form:
- $f(x, t) = Ae^{kx + \omega t}$
- $f(x, t) = A\sin(kx - \omega t)$ or $A\cos(kx - \omega t)$
- $f(x, t) = A\tan(kx)$
- $f(x, t) = Ae^{-kx}$

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **$\cos(\theta) + i\sin(\theta)$** — This is Euler's formula by definition.

2. **Light is an electromagnetic wave** — Maxwell's equations predict a wave solution for the electric and magnetic fields, with the wave traveling at the speed of light. This led Maxwell to conclude that light itself is an electromagnetic wave.

3. **The distance from the satellite** — The phase difference is proportional to the signal's travel time, which directly encodes distance: $d = c \Delta t$.

4. **Approximately $3 \times 10^8$ m/s** — Plugging in the measured values of $\mu_0$ and $\epsilon_0$ yields $c \approx 3 \times 10^8$ m/s, confirming the speed of light.

5. **$f(x, t) = A\sin(kx - \omega t)$ or $A\cos(kx - \omega t)$** — The wave equation is a second-order partial differential equation, whose general solutions are sinusoidal (traveling waves).
