## Story Hook

Around 150 BCE, a Greek astronomer named Hipparchus of Nicaea faced an impossible task: predict where every planet would appear on any night. The mathematics of circles was well understood, but computing the positions required tables of chord lengths — thousands of them, calculated painstakingly by hand.

Hipparchus spent years filling those tables. A chord in a circle of radius 1 is directly related to what we now call the sine of an angle. What he calculated by hand, we now compute in a microsecond. And what he discovered — that two functions, sine and cosine, can describe the position of any point on a circle — turned out to be the foundation for all wave mathematics.

Today, sine and cosine power cell phone signals, MRI machines, music software, and seismic sensors. It all begins with a circle of radius 1.

## Why It Matters

The unit circle is the gateway to understanding waves, oscillations, and periodic motion. Whenever something repeats — a heartbeat, a sound wave, the tides, the seasons — sine and cosine are there, describing it mathematically.

More than that, the unit circle is a translation device. It takes the geometry of a circle and translates it into coordinates, into functions, into algebra. Once you understand the unit circle, you can solve problems about angles, waves, and rotations that would otherwise seem completely unrelated.

## Historical Context

Hipparchus of Nicaea (190–120 BCE) is credited with creating the first trigonometric table, a table of chord lengths. His work was motivated by astronomy: he needed to calculate the positions of the Sun, Moon, and planets with enough precision to predict eclipses and movements.

The Greek mathematician Ptolemy (~100–170 CE) refined these tables, improving their accuracy. But Ptolemy and Hipparchus were thinking in terms of chords, not yet the functions we know today.

It wasn't until the 8th and 9th centuries that mathematicians in the Islamic Golden Age, particularly al-Khwarizmi and others, began translating chord relationships into what we now recognize as sine and cosine. The word "sine" comes from the Sanskrit word "jya," meaning "chord," which was translated through Arabic as "jaib" (which also means "bay" or "pocket"), and eventually Latinized as "sinus."

By the time the unit circle became standard in European mathematics (1600s–1700s), the fundamental geometry that Hipparchus had discovered was finally united with the algebraic functions that made it powerful.

## Core Concept

The **unit circle** is a circle of radius 1 centered at the origin (0, 0) in the coordinate plane.

For any angle $\theta$ in standard position, the terminal side of the angle intersects the unit circle at exactly one point. That point's coordinates are $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$.

In other words:
- $\cos\theta$ is the x-coordinate of the point.
- $\sin\theta$ is the y-coordinate of the point.

This is the foundation. Sine and cosine aren't mysterious ratios — they're simply the coordinates of a point on a circle of radius 1.

### The Pythagorean Identity

Since any point on the unit circle is at distance 1 from the origin, and the point is $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$, the Pythagorean theorem gives us:

$$\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$$

This is the most important identity in trigonometry. It says that no matter what angle you choose, the sum of the squares of sine and cosine is always 1. It will appear in nearly every trigonometric problem.

### Signs by Quadrant (ASTC)

Sine and cosine have different signs depending on which quadrant the terminal side is in:

- **Quadrant I** (0° to 90°): Both $\cos\theta > 0$ and $\sin\theta > 0$. (**All** functions are positive)
- **Quadrant II** (90° to 180°): $\cos\theta < 0$ but $\sin\theta > 0$. (**Sine** is positive)
- **Quadrant III** (180° to 270°): Both $\cos\theta < 0$ and $\sin\theta < 0$. (**Tangent** is positive)
- **Quadrant IV** (270° to 360°): $\cos\theta > 0$ but $\sin\theta < 0$. (**Cosine** is positive)

Mnemonic: **"All Students Take Calculus"** — A (Quadrant I), S (Quadrant II), T (Quadrant III), C (Quadrant IV).

### Reference Angles

A **reference angle** is the acute angle between the terminal side of an angle and the x-axis. Reference angles are always between $0°$ and $90°$.

To find the exact value of sine or cosine of any angle, find its reference angle (which will be one of the special angles: $30°, 45°, 60°$), look up that value, then apply the correct sign based on the quadrant.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Unit Circle Definition

For an angle $\theta$ in standard position, if $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ is the point where the terminal side intersects the unit circle, then:

$$\cos\theta = x\text{-coordinate}, \quad \sin\theta = y\text{-coordinate}$$

### Key Angle Values

The most important angles to memorize are $0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°$ (and their equivalents in other quadrants).

| Angle | $\cos\theta$ | $\sin\theta$ |
|-------|---------|---------|
| $0°$ (or $0$) | $1$ | $0$ |
| $30°$ (or $\pi/6$) | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ | $\frac{1}{2}$ |
| $45°$ (or $\pi/4$) | $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ | $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ |
| $60°$ (or $\pi/3$) | $\frac{1}{2}$ | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ |
| $90°$ (or $\pi/2$) | $0$ | $1$ |

For other quadrants, use reference angles and adjust signs by quadrant.

### Even and Odd Properties

- $\cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta$ — cosine is an **even** function.
- $\sin(-\theta) = -\sin\theta$ — sine is an **odd** function.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Find $\sin 150°$ and $\cos 150°$.**

The angle $150°$ is in Quadrant II. Its reference angle is $180° - 150° = 30°$.

We know $\sin 30° = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\cos 30° = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.

In Quadrant II, sine is positive and cosine is negative.

$$\sin 150° = \frac{1}{2}, \quad \cos 150° = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$$

**Example 2: Find $\sin 225°$ and $\cos 225°$.**

The angle $225°$ is in Quadrant III. Its reference angle is $225° - 180° = 45°$.

We know $\sin 45° = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ and $\cos 45° = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$.

In Quadrant III, both sine and cosine are negative.

$$\sin 225° = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \quad \cos 225° = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$$

**Example 3: Use the Pythagorean identity to find $\cos\theta$ if $\sin\theta = \frac{3}{5}$ and $\theta$ is in Quadrant II.**

$$\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$$
$$\cos^2\theta + \left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^2 = 1$$
$$\cos^2\theta + \frac{9}{25} = 1$$
$$\cos^2\theta = \frac{16}{25}$$
$$\cos\theta = \pm\frac{4}{5}$$

Since $\theta$ is in Quadrant II, $\cos\theta < 0$, so $\cos\theta = -\frac{4}{5}$.

**Example 4: Find an angle in $[0°, 360°)$ that is coterminal with $-210°$ and give its sine and cosine.**

Add $360°$: $-210° + 360° = 150°$.

From Example 1: $\sin 150° = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\cos 150° = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.

## Application

A Ferris wheel has radius 40 feet and rotates once every 4 minutes. If we place the center at the origin and the lowest point on the wheel at angle $270°$ (or $-90°$), what is the height of a rider at angle $\theta = 45°$?

**Solution:**
On the unit circle, a point at angle $45°$ has coordinates $(\cos 45°, \sin 45°) = \left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)$.

For a wheel of radius 40, multiply by 40: coordinates are $\left(20\sqrt{2}, 20\sqrt{2}\right)$.

The y-coordinate gives the height above the center. So the height is $20\sqrt{2} \approx 28.3$ feet above the center.

If the center is 40 feet above the ground (the wheel's lowest point touches ground), then the rider is at height $40 + 20\sqrt{2} \approx 68.3$ feet.

## Recap

- The **unit circle** is a circle of radius 1 centered at the origin.
- For angle $\theta$, the terminal side intersects the unit circle at $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$.
- **Pythagorean identity**: $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$ for all $\theta$.
- Key values at $0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90°$ must be memorized.
- Use **reference angles** to find exact values in other quadrants.
- **ASTC rule**: All, Sine, Tangent, Cosine — signs by quadrant.
- **Cosine is even**: $\cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta$; **Sine is odd**: $\sin(-\theta) = -\sin\theta$.

## Unlock Quiz

1. On the unit circle, what point corresponds to angle $60°$?
   - $\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)$
   - $\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right)$
   - $\left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)$
   - $\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)$

2. What is $\sin 120°$?
   - $-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
   - $\frac{1}{2}$
   - $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
   - $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$

3. If $\sin\theta = 0.8$, find $\cos^2\theta$ using the Pythagorean identity.
   - $0.36$
   - $0.64$
   - $0.80$
   - $1.64$

4. In which quadrant is angle $\theta$ if $\sin\theta > 0$ and $\cos\theta > 0$?
   - Quadrant I
   - Quadrant II
   - Quadrant III
   - Quadrant IV

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **$\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right)$** — At $60°$: $\cos 60° = \frac{1}{2}$, $\sin 60° = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$. Point is $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$.

2. **$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$** — Angle $120°$ is in Quadrant II, reference angle $60°$. Sine is positive in Quadrant II, so $\sin 120° = \sin 60° = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.

3. **$0.36$** — $\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$, so $\cos^2\theta = 1 - (0.8)^2 = 1 - 0.64 = 0.36$.

4. **Quadrant I** — Both sine and cosine are positive only in Quadrant I.
