## Story Hook

Around 250 BCE, Archimedes sits in Sicily, thinking about parabolas. He wants to find the area enclosed by a parabolic curve and a straight line. Straight lines and circles ŌĆö he can handle those. But a parabola? No formula exists.

So he invents one.

He imagines inscribing triangles under the parabola, then inscribing more triangles in the gaps, and more, and more ŌĆö each layer adding area but never quite reaching the true answer. Then he does the same from above, with triangles circumscribing the parabola. Squeezed between these two towers of triangles, the true area must lie. As he adds more and more triangles, the gap shrinks to nothing.

He calls it the method of exhaustion.

Two thousand years later, Riemann formalises Archimedes' intuition. Instead of triangles, use rectangles. Instead of stopping, let the number of rectangles become infinite. The limit of these Riemann sums is called the **definite integral** ŌĆö and it is exactly the inverse of the derivative.

## Why It Matters

Accumulation is everywhere. A reservoir fills at a rate that changes throughout the day ŌĆö integral tells you the total volume added. A car's speed changes continuously ŌĆö integral tells you the distance travelled. A disease spreads at a changing rate ŌĆö integral predicts the cumulative number infected. Money in a savings account grows with compound interest ŌĆö integral computes the total wealth.

Whenever you accumulate something that arrives at a changing rate, you need an integral.

## Historical Context

Archimedes' method was brilliant but exhausting ŌĆö literally. Computing even one area required pages of geometric argument. Mathematicians knew there had to be a better way.

In the 1600s, as calculus was being born, mathematicians noticed something miraculous: areas and slopes are linked. A function's area from $a$ to $x$ changes at exactly the rate of the function's height at $x$. This discovery ŌĆö the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus ŌĆö transformed integration from a laborious geometric procedure into something almost mechanical. Instead of constructing infinite towers of polygons like Archimedes, mathematicians could now evaluate a single antiderivative at two points.

By the 1800s, Riemann, Lebesgue, and others formalised the integral with rigour. Riemann sums provided a precise definition: the integral is the limit of sums of rectangles. Today it underlies probability, physics, engineering, and economics. When NASA engineers in the 1960s needed the volume of the Saturn V's curved fuel tanks, they used integrationŌĆöthe same concept Archimedes had intuited two millennia before.

## Core Concept

Imagine a function $f(x)$ drawn on a graph from $x = a$ to $x = b$. We want the area between the curve and the $x$-axis.

**The Riemann Sum Idea:** Divide the interval $[a, b]$ into $n$ equal subintervals, each of width $\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}$. On each subinterval, draw a rectangle. The height of the rectangle can be:
- The function's value at the **left endpoint** (left Riemann sum),
- The function's value at the **right endpoint** (right Riemann sum), or
- The function's value at the **midpoint** (midpoint Riemann sum).

Sum all the rectangle areas:
$$\text{Riemann sum} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \cdot \Delta x$$

where $x_i^*$ is the chosen point in the $i$-th subinterval.

As $n \to \infty$, $\Delta x \to 0$, and the Riemann sum approaches the **definite integral**:
$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \cdot \Delta x$$

This integral represents the signed area: area above the $x$-axis counts positive; area below counts negative.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Definite Integral:** The limit of Riemann sums as the number of subintervals approaches infinity. Notation: $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.

**Integrand:** The function $f(x)$ being integrated.

**Limits of Integration:** The values $a$ (lower) and $b$ (upper) that define the interval.

**Riemann Sum:** A sum of the form $\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \cdot \Delta x$, used to approximate the area. The three common types are left, right, and midpoint Riemann sums.

**Net Area:** The signed area, counting upward as positive and downward as negative. For a function that dips below the $x$-axis, regions below contribute negatively to the integral.

**Partition:** A division of an interval $[a, b]$ into subintervals. The width of each subinterval is $\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}$.

**Properties of Definite Integrals:**
1. Linearity: $\int_a^b [cf(x) + g(x)]\,dx = c\int_a^b f(x)\,dx + \int_a^b g(x)\,dx$.
2. Additivity: $\int_a^c f(x)\,dx + \int_c^b f(x)\,dx = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.
3. Zero interval: $\int_a^a f(x)\,dx = 0$.
4. Reversal: $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = -\int_b^a f(x)\,dx$.

**Average Value of $f$ on $[a, b]$:**
$$\text{Average} = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$

This is the height of a rectangle with width $(b-a)$ that has the same area as the region under $f$. In other words, it represents the typical or mean height of the function over the interval.

**Convergence:** As $n \to \infty$, Riemann sums converge to the definite integral for functions that are continuous or have only finitely many discontinuities.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Left and Right Riemann Sums**

Estimate $\int_0^2 (x^2 + 1)\,dx$ using 4 subintervals.

With $n = 4$, $\Delta x = \frac{2-0}{4} = 0.5$. Subintervals: $[0, 0.5], [0.5, 1], [1, 1.5], [1.5, 2]$.

Function values: $f(0) = 1$, $f(0.5) = 1.25$, $f(1) = 2$, $f(1.5) = 3.25$, $f(2) = 5$.

**Left Riemann sum** (heights at left endpoints):
$$L_4 = (1 + 1.25 + 2 + 3.25) \cdot 0.5 = 7.5 \cdot 0.5 = 3.75$$

**Right Riemann sum** (heights at right endpoints):
$$R_4 = (1.25 + 2 + 3.25 + 5) \cdot 0.5 = 11.5 \cdot 0.5 = 5.75$$

The true area lies between 3.75 and 5.75. (The exact value is $\frac{14}{3} \approx 4.67$.)

**Example 2: Net Area (Signed Area)**

Compute $\int_0^{2\pi} \sin(x)\,dx$.

The sine function is positive on $[0, \pi]$ (area above the axis) and negative on $[\pi, 2\pi]$ (area below the axis). By symmetry, the area above equals the area below, so:
$$\int_0^{2\pi} \sin(x)\,dx = 0$$

The net area is zero because positive and negative regions cancel.

**Example 3: Average Value**

Find the average value of $f(x) = x^2$ on $[0, 3]$.

$$\text{Average} = \frac{1}{3-0}\int_0^3 x^2\,dx$$

We'll need the antiderivative later, but for now think of it as the height of a rectangle over $[0, 3]$ with area equal to the region under $x^2$.

**Example 4: Riemann Sum with Midpoint Rule**

Estimate $\int_0^1 e^x\,dx$ using the midpoint Riemann sum with $n = 2$ subintervals.

With $n = 2$, $\Delta x = \frac{1-0}{2} = 0.5$. Subintervals: $[0, 0.5]$ and $[0.5, 1]$.

Midpoints: $x_1^* = 0.25$ and $x_2^* = 0.75$.

Function values: $f(0.25) = e^{0.25} \approx 1.284$ and $f(0.75) = e^{0.75} \approx 2.117$.

$$M_2 = (1.284 + 2.117) \cdot 0.5 = 3.401 \cdot 0.5 \approx 1.701$$

The true value is $e - 1 \approx 1.718$, so the midpoint rule gives a good approximation.

**Example 5: Interpreting Integral as Accumulation**

A factory produces bicycles at a rate of $r(t) = 100 + 10t$ units per day, where $t$ is days since January 1st. How many bicycles are produced in the first 10 days?

The total production is:
$$P = \int_0^{10} (100 + 10t)\,dt$$

This is the accumulated production ŌĆö we are "adding up" all the bicycles produced at the changing rate $r(t)$.

(We'll evaluate this integral once we learn the Fundamental Theorem.)

**Example 6: Comparing Riemann Sum Accuracy**

Compare all three Riemann sums for $f(x) = x$ on $[0, 2]$ using $n = 2$ subintervals.

With $n = 2$, $\Delta x = 1$. Subintervals: $[0, 1]$ and $[1, 2]$.

Function values: $f(0) = 0$, $f(1) = 1$, $f(2) = 2$.

**Left Riemann sum:** $L_2 = (0 + 1) \cdot 1 = 1$.

**Right Riemann sum:** $R_2 = (1 + 2) \cdot 1 = 3$.

**Midpoint Riemann sum:** $f(0.5) = 0.5$ and $f(1.5) = 1.5$, so $M_2 = (0.5 + 1.5) \cdot 1 = 2$.

The true integral (by geometry, a triangle) is $\frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 2$. The midpoint rule is exact! For linear functions, the midpoint rule is always exact.

## Application

Engineers designing dams must know how much water a river delivers during a flood. If they have a graph of flow rate over time, the area under that curve ŌĆö computed via integration ŌĆö tells them the total volume of water. Similarly, when epidemiologists model disease spread, the integral of the infection rate gives the cumulative number of infections. In finance, the integral of a growth rate function yields total wealth accumulated over time.

## Recap

The integral measures accumulation by summing infinitely many infinitely thin slices. Riemann sums approximate this with finitely many rectangles. As the number of rectangles increases, the Riemann sum approaches the definite integral. Archimedes used this idea geometrically 2,000 years ago; Riemann formalised it. Properties of integrals allow us to split regions, factor constants, and reverse limits. The average value of a function can be computed as an integral divided by the interval width. Integration and differentiation are inverses ŌĆö revealed by the Fundamental Theorem.
