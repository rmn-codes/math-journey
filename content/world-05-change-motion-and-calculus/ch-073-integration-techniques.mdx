## Story Hook

You're standing in your kitchen, and you realize you've left the stove on. The room is warming. You measure the temperature at different times and notice it follows a pattern — but the pattern isn't simple. To predict when the room will reach a dangerous temperature, you'd need to reverse the chain rule, undo a product, recognize a hidden trigonometric structure. That's integration techniques in a nutshell: the art of spotting disguised patterns and untangling them.

Not every integral yields to the basic power rule or direct substitution. Mathematicians in the 1700s and 1800s — Leibniz, the Bernoulli family, Euler — spent decades developing clever strategies. Like detectives, they learned to see a complicated integral and ask: *What rule was this function likely created from?* Each technique is a way of working backward, of reversing a familiar operation from calculus to expose the hidden antiderivative.

## Why It Matters

In the real world, almost no integral is simple. Engineering, physics, chemistry, economics — all involve integrals that look messy. Without integration techniques, you'd be stuck. These methods are the difference between being able to solve a real problem and being stuck on the first step.

When you see $\int x e^x \, dx$ or $\int \frac{1}{x^2 + 4x + 5} \, dx$, you need a playbook. The techniques in this chapter *are* that playbook.

## Historical Context

In the late 1600s, Newton and Leibniz invented calculus, but integration was hard. Differentiation was straightforward — take a derivative, apply the rules. But finding antiderivatives? That required *cleverness*. For the next century, mathematicians built a toolkit.

Leibniz himself developed integration by parts, recognizing that if the product rule creates a derivative, it should be reversible. Euler, the most prolific mathematician who ever lived, devised methods for integrals of rational functions (fractions of polynomials) and trigonometric expressions. The Bernoullis developed substitution strategies.

By the 1800s, a canon of techniques had solidified — the methods we use today. What's remarkable is that despite computers and symbolic algebra systems, these same techniques remain central to calculus because they teach *reasoning*: how to transform an unfamiliar problem into a familiar one.

## Core Concept

Integration techniques are **strategies for recognizing the structure hidden inside a complicated integral and transforming it into something you already know how to integrate**.

The key insight: if a function was created by a differentiation rule, you can reverse that rule to integrate it. Every technique works by asking: *What rule made this?*

### u-Substitution: Reversing the Chain Rule

The chain rule says: if $y = f(g(x))$, then $\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$.

So if you integrate both sides:
$$\int f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \, dx = f(g(x)) + C$$

This is **u-substitution**. You set $u = g(x)$, so $du = g'(x) \, dx$, and the integral becomes:
$$\int f'(u) \, du = f(u) + C = f(g(x)) + C$$

The art is recognizing which part of the integrand is $g(x)$ and which is $g'(x)$.

### Integration by Parts: Reversing the Product Rule

The product rule says: $(uv)' = u'v + uv'$.

Integrate both sides:
$$\int (u'v + uv') \, dx = uv + C$$

Rearrange:
$$\int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du$$

This is **integration by parts**. It trades one integral for another — useful when the new integral is simpler.

The **LIATE rule** tells you how to choose $u$:
- **L**ogarithmic functions
- **I**nverse trig functions
- **A**lgebraic (polynomial)
- **T**rigonometric
- **E**xponential

Whichever comes first in LIATE, make that your $u$.

### Trigonometric Integrals and Standard Forms

Some integrals appear so often they're worth memorizing:
$$\int \sin x \, dx = -\cos x + C$$
$$\int \cos x \, dx = \sin x + C$$
$$\int \sec^2 x \, dx = \tan x + C$$
$$\int \tan x \, dx = -\ln|\cos x| + C = \ln|\sec x| + C$$
$$\int \sec x \tan x \, dx = \sec x + C$$
$$\int \csc^2 x \, dx = -\cot x + C$$
$$\int \csc x \cot x \, dx = -\csc x + C$$

When you spot a trigonometric integral, you're looking for these forms — or ways to transform the integral into them using identities like $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$.

For more complex trigonometric integrals, you often use the **power reduction formulas**:
$$\sin^2 x = \frac{1 - \cos(2x)}{2}, \quad \cos^2 x = \frac{1 + \cos(2x)}{2}$$

These allow you to reduce the power of trigonometric functions and integrate. For instance:
$$\int \sin^2 x \, dx = \int \frac{1 - \cos(2x)}{2} \, dx = \frac{x}{2} - \frac{\sin(2x)}{4} + C$$

### Completing the Square

For integrals like $\int \frac{1}{x^2 + 4x + 5} \, dx$, you complete the square:
$$x^2 + 4x + 5 = (x+2)^2 + 1$$

Then use $u$-substitution with $u = x + 2$, and you get a standard arctangent form.

### Partial Fractions (Linear Factors)

For a rational function $\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ where $q(x)$ factors into distinct linear factors, you decompose it:
$$\frac{x+5}{(x-1)(x+2)} = \frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{B}{x+2}$$

Solve for $A$ and $B$, then integrate each piece separately — usually yielding logarithms.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

**Integration by substitution (u-substitution):** If $u = g(x)$, then $du = g'(x) \, dx$, and:
$$\int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \, dx = \int f(u) \, du$$

This reverses the chain rule. The key step is recognizing that the integrand contains both a composite function and the derivative of its inner function.

**Integration by parts:** If $u$ and $v$ are differentiable:
$$\int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du$$

This reverses the product rule. Use the LIATE rule to decide which factor should be $u$ (and which should be $dv$).

**Standard trigonometric integrals:** Memorize the basic forms ($\sin$, $\cos$, $\sec^2$, $\tan$, etc.) and use power reduction formulas to simplify higher powers.

**Completing the square:** Rewrite $ax^2 + bx + c$ as $a(x + p)^2 + q$ to expose a standard form (usually arctangent).

**Partial fractions:** Decompose a rational function $\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ into simpler fractions whose antiderivatives are known (usually logarithms). Factor the denominator first, then set up the decomposition and solve for unknowns.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: u-substitution with the chain rule reversed**

Find $\int 2x \cos(x^2) \, dx$.

*Solution:* Notice that if $u = x^2$, then $du = 2x \, dx$ — and that's exactly in the integrand!
$$\int 2x \cos(x^2) \, dx = \int \cos(u) \, du = \sin(u) + C = \sin(x^2) + C$$

**Example 2: integration by parts**

Find $\int x e^x \, dx$.

*Solution:* Choose $u = x$ (algebraic, lower in LIATE than exponential). Then $dv = e^x \, dx$, so $du = dx$ and $v = e^x$.
$$\int x e^x \, dx = x e^x - \int e^x \, dx = x e^x - e^x + C = e^x(x - 1) + C$$

**Example 3: trigonometric integral**

Find $\int \sec^2 x \, dx$.

*Solution:* This is a standard form.
$$\int \sec^2 x \, dx = \tan x + C$$

**Example 4: completing the square**

Find $\int \frac{1}{x^2 + 2x + 5} \, dx$.

*Solution:* Complete the square: $x^2 + 2x + 5 = (x+1)^2 + 4$. Let $u = x + 1$, $du = dx$.
$$\int \frac{1}{(x+1)^2 + 4} \, dx = \int \frac{1}{u^2 + 4} \, du$$

Now recognize the arctangent form: $\int \frac{1}{u^2 + a^2} \, du = \frac{1}{a} \arctan\left(\frac{u}{a}\right) + C$ with $a = 2$.
$$\int \frac{1}{u^2 + 4} \, du = \frac{1}{2} \arctan\left(\frac{u}{2}\right) + C = \frac{1}{2} \arctan\left(\frac{x+1}{2}\right) + C$$

**Example 5: trigonometric integral with power reduction**

Find $\int \sin^2 x \, dx$.

*Solution:* Use the power reduction formula $\sin^2 x = \frac{1 - \cos(2x)}{2}$:
$$\int \sin^2 x \, dx = \int \frac{1 - \cos(2x)}{2} \, dx = \frac{1}{2} \int (1 - \cos(2x)) \, dx$$

Integrate term by term:
$$= \frac{1}{2} \left( x - \frac{\sin(2x)}{2} \right) + C = \frac{x}{2} - \frac{\sin(2x)}{4} + C$$

## Reasoning Task

**The Detective's Dilemma:** You're given $\int x^3 \sqrt{x^2 + 1} \, dx$.

- Which integration technique(s) might work?
- Why is integration by parts awkward here?
- How does a clever $u$-substitution still fail to give a closed form?
- What does this tell you about the limits of these techniques?

(The integral doesn't have a closed form in elementary functions. But recognizing *why* is valuable.)

## Application

**Cooling Coffee:** Newton's Law of Cooling says the temperature $T(t)$ of cooling coffee satisfies:
$$\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_s)$$

where $T_s$ is room temperature and $k > 0$ is a constant. Solving this (via separation of variables, which we'll meet soon) involves the integral:
$$\int \frac{dT}{T - T_s}$$

This is a standard partial fractions form:
$$\int \frac{dT}{T - T_s} = \ln|T - T_s| + C$$

Without this single integral, you couldn't model coffee cooling — or the temperature of a building, the decay of heat in an engine, or the concentration of medication in your bloodstream.

**Motion and Arc Length:** To find the arc length of a curve $y = f(x)$ from $x = a$ to $x = b$, you integrate:
$$L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2} \, dx$$

For example, the arc length of $y = x^{3/2}$ from $0$ to $4$ requires:
$$L = \int_0^4 \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{3}{2}\sqrt{x}\right)^2} \, dx = \int_0^4 \sqrt{1 + \frac{9x}{4}} \, dx$$

Completing the square (or a clever substitution) turns this into an integrable form. Arc length calculations are essential in physics (for understanding pendulum motion), engineering (for designing roads and roller coasters), and navigation.

## Recap

- **u-substitution** reverses the chain rule: identify the "inside function," set it as $u$, and transform the integral. This works when the integrand contains a composite function along with the derivative of its inner function.
- **Integration by parts** reverses the product rule: useful when one part is easy to integrate and the other easy to differentiate. It trades one integral for (hopefully) a simpler one.
- **LIATE** tells you which part should be $u$ in integration by parts: Logarithmic, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential (in order of priority).
- **Trigonometric integrals** often reduce to standard forms via identities. Power reduction formulas turn high powers into integrableforms.
- **Completing the square** transforms awkward quadratics into recognizable arctangent or logarithm forms. This is especially useful for integrands involving $\frac{1}{x^2 + bx + c}$.
- **Partial fractions** decompose rational functions into integrable pieces, usually yielding logarithms.
- **Strategy:** Try substitution first. If that fails, try parts. Recognize standard forms. Know when to stop—some integrals don't have elementary antiderivatives.
