## Story Hook

A pharmaceutical company runs a clinical trial. They give a new vaccine to 100 volunteers. Each person either develops immunity (success) or doesn't (failure). The scientists count: 87 developed immunity.

But here's the mystery: if we run a different trial with the same vaccine on 100 *different* volunteers, will we always get exactly 87 successes? Almost certainly not. Maybe we'll get 85, maybe 91, maybe 79. The exact number bounces around — yet it bounces around in a *pattern*.

That pattern is the binomial distribution. It's the answer to a fundamental question: *In a series of independent yes-or-no experiments, how many times should we expect success?*

Before statistics formalized this, drug testing was guesswork and politics. Now it's science — because we can model, predict, and test the distribution of successes. This chapter teaches you how.

## Why It Matters

Every time you:
- Flip a coin 50 times and count heads
- Give a test with multiple-choice questions and guess randomly
- Screen 500 people for a disease and count how many test positive
- Run a manufacturing process 200 times and count defects
- Survey 1000 people about a yes-or-no question

...you're performing binomial experiments. The binomial distribution lets you:
- Predict the most likely outcome
- Calculate the probability of "exactly k successes"
- Assess whether a product is working reliably
- Decide if a sample result is statistically significant
- Design experiments with the right sample size

Without it, pharmaceutical companies couldn't prove drugs work. Quality-control engineers couldn't catch defects. Elections couldn't be called from early returns.

## Historical Context

The binomial distribution emerged in the 1700s as mathematicians studied probability. **Blaise Pascal** and **Pierre de Fermat** (1650s) solved the "problem of points" — dividing winnings in an unfinished game — which led to the binomial formula.

**Isaac Newton** (1665) discovered the binomial theorem: how to expand $(a+b)^n$. The connection to probability came soon after.

**Daniel Bernoulli** formalised the "Bernoulli trial" (a single experiment with probability $p$ of success). String many together, and you get a binomial distribution.

By the 1800s, statisticians were using the binomial distribution to study everything from death rates to election outcomes. Today it's the foundation of hypothesis testing, clinical trials, and quality assurance.

## Core Concept

Imagine a single coin flip. Probability of heads (success) = 0.5. That's one trial.

Now flip it 10 times. You might get 7 heads. Or 5. Or 9. The *number* of heads varies. But it varies predictably.

A **binomial experiment** has four properties:
1. **n** independent trials (e.g., 10 flips)
2. Each trial has two outcomes: success or failure
3. Probability of success **p** is constant (e.g., 0.5 for a fair coin)
4. We count the total number of successes

The **binomial random variable** $X$ = the total count of successes out of $n$ trials.

Key insight: $X$ can take any integer value from 0 to $n$. A binomial distribution tells us the *probability* of each value.

For example: Flip a fair coin 3 times. $X$ = number of heads.
- $P(X = 0) = P(\text{TTT}) = 0.125$
- $P(X = 1) = P(\text{HTT, THT, TTH}) = 0.375$
- $P(X = 2) = P(\text{HHT, HTH, THH}) = 0.375$
- $P(X = 3) = P(\text{HHH}) = 0.125$

Notice: these probabilities sum to 1. Also notice symmetry (0 and 3 have equal probability, 1 and 2 have equal probability). That's because $p = 0.5$.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### The Binomial Probability Formula

If $X$ follows a binomial distribution with $n$ trials and success probability $p$, then:

$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$

where $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, n$ and $\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$ (the binomial coefficient, "n choose k").

**Interpretation:**
- $\binom{n}{k}$ = number of ways to arrange $k$ successes among $n$ trials
- $p^k$ = probability of getting exactly $k$ successes
- $(1-p)^{n-k}$ = probability of getting exactly $n-k$ failures
- Multiply them together: probability of exactly $k$ successes

**Example:** Vaccine trial. $n = 100$, $p = 0.87$ (probability of immunity). What's $P(X = 87)$?

$$P(X = 87) = \binom{100}{87} (0.87)^{87} (0.13)^{13}$$

This is tedious to compute by hand — use a calculator or software. But the formula shows the logic.

### Mean and Standard Deviation

If $X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$, then:

$$\mu = np$$

$$\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}$$

These tell us the *centre* and *spread* of the distribution.

**Example:** Flip a fair coin 100 times. $n = 100$, $p = 0.5$.
- Mean: $\mu = 100 \times 0.5 = 50$ heads
- Std Dev: $\sigma = \sqrt{100 \times 0.5 \times 0.5} = \sqrt{25} = 5$ heads

So we expect about 50 heads, but with a standard deviation of 5. That means we'd typically see between 45 and 55 heads.

### Cumulative Probability

Sometimes we want $P(X \leq k)$, not $P(X = k)$.

$$P(X \leq k) = \sum_{i=0}^{k} P(X = i)$$

**Example:** What's the probability of getting *at most* 3 heads in 10 flips?

$$P(X \leq 3) = P(X=0) + P(X=1) + P(X=2) + P(X=3)$$

Again, use a calculator or binomial table for large $n$.

### Connection to Pascal's Triangle

The binomial coefficients appear in Pascal's triangle:
```
Row 0:                    1
Row 1:                  1   1
Row 2:                1   2   1
Row 3:              1   3   3   1
Row 4:            1   4   6   4   1
Row 5:          1   5  10  10   5   1
```

Row $n$, position $k$ gives $\binom{n}{k}$. This geometric pattern encodes all the probabilities.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Quality Control**

A factory makes light bulbs. 95% pass quality inspection (success probability $p = 0.95$). A random sample of 20 bulbs is tested. What's the probability that exactly 19 pass?

$$P(X = 19) = \binom{20}{19} (0.95)^{19} (0.05)^{1}$$

$$= 20 \times (0.95)^{19} \times 0.05$$

$$\approx 20 \times 0.3585 \times 0.05 \approx 0.358$$

So about a 35.8% chance. That's reasonably likely — not surprising.

**Example 2: Guessing on a Test**

You randomly guess on a 10-question multiple-choice test (each question has 4 options, so $p = 0.25$). What's the probability you get at least 5 correct?

$$P(X \geq 5) = P(X=5) + P(X=6) + \cdots + P(X=10)$$

Using a binomial calculator: $P(X \geq 5) \approx 0.078$ (about 7.8%).

So you'd expect to fail — getting 5 or more correct by pure chance is rare.

**Example 3: Mean and Spread**

A survey asks 500 people: "Do you support the new policy?" Suppose 60% say yes ($p = 0.6$). What are the mean and standard deviation of the number of "yes" responses?

$$\mu = np = 500 \times 0.6 = 300$$

$$\sigma = \sqrt{500 \times 0.6 \times 0.4} = \sqrt{120} \approx 10.95$$

We expect about 300 "yes" answers, give or take 11. So we'd typically see between 289 and 311 "yes" responses.

   - Find $P(X = 49)$ (all but one correctly identified).

   - Find $\mu$ and $\sigma$.

   - Would $X = 15$ be surprising? Explain.

   - What are $\mu$ and $\sigma$?
   - Approximately how many people should we expect to *not* develop immunity?

5. **Manufacturing Defect Rate:** A process produces defects at a 2% rate. A batch of 500 items is made. Let $X$ = number of defects.
   - Find $\mu$ and $\sigma$.
   - Would observing 20 defects be unusual? (Compare to $\mu + 3\sigma$.)

## Reasoning Task

**The Rare Event Rule:** If something has probability less than 5%, we call it "rare" or "unlikely."

A student takes a 20-question true/false test by guessing randomly ($p = 0.5$). The student gets 17 correct. Should the teacher suspect cheating?

Find:
- Mean and standard deviation of $X$ (correct answers if guessing).
- Is 17 correct within 2 standard deviations of the mean? Within 3?
- What's $P(X \geq 17)$ (probability of 17 or more correct by guessing)?
- Should the teacher suspect cheating? Explain your reasoning.

*Hint:* The rare event rule suggests we should be suspicious if an outcome has probability less than 5%.

## Application

**Real-World Scenario: Clinical Trials and FDA Approval**

The FDA evaluates new drugs using binomial distributions. Suppose a company claims their medication helps 70% of patients. The FDA tests it on 100 patients. They'll approve it only if:
1. The number of successes is "unusually high" compared to a lower baseline (say, 50% success rate for existing treatments).
2. The result is statistically significant (probability less than 5%, if the drug only matched the baseline).

**Your Task:**

Using binomial formulas, suppose the baseline success rate is 50% and the company's drug achieves success in 63 out of 100 patients.

- What would $\mu$ and $\sigma$ be if the drug only matched the baseline (50% success)?
- What's $P(X \geq 63)$ under the baseline hypothesis?
- Is 63 successes enough to recommend approval? Why or why not?

This is the foundation of hypothesis testing — comparing observed data to a null hypothesis using probability.

## Recap

The **binomial distribution** models the number of successes in $n$ independent trials, each with probability $p$ of success.

**Key Formula:** $P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$

**Key Facts:**
- Mean: $\mu = np$
- Standard Deviation: $\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}$
- Cumulative probabilities use sums

**When to Use It:**
- Counting yes/no outcomes in repeated trials
- Clinical trials, quality control, surveys, games, genetics
- Assessing whether an outcome is "rare" or "typical"

**Historical Significance:**
- Formalised probability and counting in the 1700s–1800s
- Enabled scientific drug testing and quality assurance
- Led to hypothesis testing and statistical inference

The binomial distribution is your first powerful tool for answering: *"If this experiment were repeated many times, what patterns would emerge?"*

## Unlock Quiz

**Question 1:** A fair die is rolled 8 times. Let $X$ = number of times a 6 appears. Which of the following is true?

- $X$ follows a binomial distribution with $n = 8$ and $p = 1/6$
- $X$ follows a binomial distribution with $n = 8$ and $p = 1/2$
- $X$ cannot be modeled with the binomial distribution because rolling a die has more than two outcomes
- $\mu = 8 \times 1/6 \approx 1.33$ and $\sigma \approx 1.04$

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The first and fourth statements are correct. $X$ counts successes (rolling a 6) in 8 independent trials, each with probability 1/6. The die has six outcomes, but we're counting one specific outcome (success = rolling a 6), so it fits the binomial model. Mean: $\mu = 8 \times (1/6) = 4/3 \approx 1.33$. Std Dev: $\sigma = \sqrt{8 \times (1/6) \times (5/6)} = \sqrt{40/36} \approx 1.05$.
</details>

**Question 2:** Suppose $X \sim \text{Binomial}(100, 0.3)$. What are $\mu$ and $\sigma$?

- $\mu = 30$, $\sigma = \sqrt{21} \approx 4.58$
- $\mu = 30$, $\sigma \approx 4.35$
- $\mu = 70$, $\sigma = \sqrt{21} \approx 4.58$
- $\mu = 0.3$, $\sigma = 0.45$

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$\mu = np = 100 \times 0.3 = 30$. $\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)} = \sqrt{100 \times 0.3 \times 0.7} = \sqrt{21} \approx 4.58$. The first option is correct.
</details>

**Question 3:** A company manufactures USB drives with a 3% defect rate. A batch of 50 drives is produced. Let $X$ = number of defective drives. What is $P(X = 0)$ (no defects)?

- $(0.03)^{50}$
- $(0.97)^{50}$
- $\binom{50}{0} (0.03)^0 (0.97)^{50} = (0.97)^{50}$
- $(0.03)^0 (0.97)^0 = 1$

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$P(X = 0) = \binom{50}{0} (0.03)^0 (0.97)^{50} = 1 \times 1 \times (0.97)^{50} = (0.97)^{50}$. This is the third option.
</details>

**Question 4:** Which scenario is *not* a binomial experiment?

- Rolling a standard die 20 times and counting how many times you roll exactly a 3
- Drawing cards from a deck (with replacement) 15 times and counting how many are red
- Measuring the heights of 100 people and noting how many are above 180 cm
- Flipping a weighted coin 50 times where the probability of heads is 0.6, counting the number of heads

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
The third option is not a binomial experiment because we are not performing "repeated trials" in the binomial sense — we're measuring a population characteristic (height) once per person. In the other three, we repeat the same trial multiple times and count successes.
</details>

**Question 5:** You take a quiz with 15 multiple-choice questions (4 options each) and guess on all questions. Your success probability is $p = 0.25$ per question. What's the expected number of correct answers?

- 3.75
- 5
- 7.5
- 11.25

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$\mu = np = 15 \times 0.25 = 3.75$. The first option is correct.
</details>
