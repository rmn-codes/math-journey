## Story Hook

It's 1654, Pascal is working on probability and gambling. A friend asks him: "How many different five-card poker hands can be dealt from a standard 52-card deck?"

If you tried to list them all, you would need a lifetime. There are 52 ways to choose the first card, 51 ways for the second, 50 for the third, 49 for the fourth, and 48 for the fifth. That's $52 \times 51 \times 50 \times 49 \times 48 = 311{,}875{,}200$ arrangements.

But wait. In poker, the hand {King of hearts, seven of clubs, two of spades, ace of diamonds, queen of hearts} is the *same* hand as {ace of diamonds, King of hearts, two of spades, queen of hearts, seven of clubs}. Order doesn't matter. So we've overcounted by a lot.

Pascal knew the answer. Using a formula for combinations, he calculated: there are exactly $\binom{52}{5} = 2{,}598{,}960$ distinct five-card poker hands.

No listing needed. No lifetime wasted. Just a formula—born from counting, refined in Pascal's triangle, and essential to probability.

## Why It Matters

Counting seems simple until the numbers get large. Counting becomes essential the moment you ask: "How many ways can something happen?"

- A PIN code is 4 digits. How many possible PINs exist? (Without counting every one, you can say: $10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 = 10{,}000$.)
- A state license plate has 3 letters followed by 3 numbers. How many plates can exist? ($26^3 \times 10^3$.)
- A lock has 40 positions and you choose 3 of them as your combination. How many combinations are there? (Much fewer than if order mattered!)
- A lottery draws 6 numbers from 1 to 49. How many possible tickets? (Huge—but calculable without enumeration.)

Once you can count all possibilities, you can compute probabilities. "I bought 1 lottery ticket. How many tickets were sold?" To find your winning probability, you need to know the total number of distinct tickets.

Combinatorics—the mathematics of counting—is the silent foundation beneath probability.

## Historical Context

Counting problems appear throughout history. Chinese mathematicians knew versions of Pascal's triangle a thousand years before Pascal. Indian mathematicians like Bhaskara II worked on combinatorics in the 12th century. Persian mathematicians developed early versions of binomial expansions.

But Pascal synthesized these ideas into a triangle that beautifully encodes all combinatorial values. He used it to solve the gambler's problem. From these origins, the mathematics of permutations and combinations developed.

In the 17th and 18th centuries, mathematicians like Jacob Bernoulli formalized the notation and theory. Today, combinatorics is a vast field essential to computer science, cryptography, probability, and statistics.

The ancient impulse to count systematically became the modern discipline of combinatorics.

## Core Concept

**Fundamental Counting Principle**: If one thing can happen in $m$ ways, and a second can happen independently in $n$ ways, then the two things together can happen in $m \times n$ ways.

Example: You choose an outfit. 5 shirts, 3 pants, 2 shoes. Total outfits: $5 \times 3 \times 2 = 30$.

This extends to any number of choices. If you choose a drink ($n_1$ ways), a sandwich ($n_2$ ways), and a dessert ($n_3$ ways), the total meals are $n_1 \times n_2 \times n_3$.

**Factorial**: The factorial of a positive integer $n$, written $n!$, is the product of all positive integers from 1 to $n$:

$$n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \cdots \times 2 \times 1$$

Examples:
- $3! = 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 6$
- $5! = 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 120$
- $0! = 1$ (by definition)

Factorials grow *explosively*. $10! = 3{,}628{,}800$. $20! \approx 2.4$ trillion.

**Permutations**: A permutation is an arrangement of $n$ objects where *order matters*.

If you have $n$ distinct objects and choose $r$ of them, the number of permutations is:

$$P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$

Example: You have 5 runners and want to award gold, silver, and bronze medals. Order matters (1st place is different from 2nd). How many ways can medals be awarded?

$$P(5,3) = \frac{5!}{(5-3)!} = \frac{5!}{2!} = \frac{120}{2} = 60$$

**Combinations**: A combination is a selection of $n$ objects where *order doesn't matter*.

If you have $n$ distinct objects and choose $r$ of them, the number of combinations is:

$$C(n,r) = \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$$

This is called the binomial coefficient, read as "$n$ choose $r$."

Example: A committee has 10 people. You need to choose 3 for a subcommittee. Order doesn't matter. How many subcommittees?

$$C(10,3) = \binom{10}{3} = \frac{10!}{3! \times 7!} = \frac{10 \times 9 \times 8}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{720}{6} = 120$$

**Pascal's Triangle**: Arrange binomial coefficients in a triangle:

```
                1
              1   1
            1   2   1
          1   3   3   1
        1   4   6   4   1
      1   5  10  10   5   1
    1   6  15  20  15   6   1
```

The $n$-th row contains the binomial coefficients $\binom{n}{0}, \binom{n}{1}, \ldots, \binom{n}{n}$. Each entry is the sum of the two above it. This triangle appears in expansions, probability, and combinatorics throughout mathematics.

**Complement Counting**: Sometimes it's easier to count what you *don't* want and subtract from the total. How many 4-digit numbers contain at least one 5? It's easier to count how many *don't* contain a 5, then subtract from the total.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

1. **Fundamental Counting Principle**: If task 1 can be done in $m$ ways and task 2 in $n$ ways, together they can be done in $m \times n$ ways.
2. **Factorial** ($n!$): Product of integers from 1 to $n$. $n! = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 1$. $0! = 1$.
3. **Permutation** ($P(n,r)$): Ordered arrangements of $r$ items from $n$ distinct objects. $P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$.
4. **Combination** ($C(n,r)$ or $\binom{n}{r}$): Unordered selections of $r$ items from $n$ distinct objects. $\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$.
5. **Binomial Coefficient**: Another name for $\binom{n}{r}$, the coefficient of $x^r$ in the expansion of $(x+y)^n$.
6. **Pascal's Triangle**: Triangular array of binomial coefficients where row $n$ contains $\binom{n}{0}, \binom{n}{1}, \ldots, \binom{n}{n}$.
7. **Complement Counting**: Count the complement (what you don't want) and subtract from the total.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Arranging Letters**

How many ways can you arrange the letters in the word "CAT"?

All three letters are distinct. You choose all 3 and order matters.

$$P(3,3) = 3! = 6$$

The arrangements are: CAT, CTA, ACT, ATC, TAC, TCA.

**Example 2: Choosing a Committee**

A club has 8 members. How many 3-member committees can be formed?

Order doesn't matter (committee {Alice, Bob, Carol} is the same as {Carol, Alice, Bob}).

$$C(8,3) = \binom{8}{3} = \frac{8!}{3! \times 5!} = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{336}{6} = 56$$

**Example 3: Poker Hands**

How many 5-card poker hands can be dealt from a 52-card deck?

Order doesn't matter (the hand {A♠, K♥, Q♦, J♣, 10♠} is the same regardless of order dealt).

$$C(52,5) = \binom{52}{5} = \frac{52!}{5! \times 47!} = \frac{52 \times 51 \times 50 \times 49 \times 48}{5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{311{,}875{,}200}{120} = 2{,}598{,}960$$

**Example 4: Passwords**

How many 4-character passwords are possible if each character can be a letter (26 options) or a digit (10 options), so 36 total?

Each position has 36 choices:
$$36 \times 36 \times 36 \times 36 = 36^4 = 1{,}679{,}616$$

**Example 5: Complement Counting**

How many 3-digit numbers (from 100 to 999) do *not* contain the digit 5?

- First digit: 1–9 except 5, so 8 choices.
- Second digit: 0–9 except 5, so 9 choices.
- Third digit: 0–9 except 5, so 9 choices.

Total without a 5: $8 \times 9 \times 9 = 648$.

Alternatively: Total 3-digit numbers = 900. Those containing at least one 5 is harder to count directly, so complement works well here.

## Reasoning Task

**The Birthday Paradox Revisited (Combinatorial Approach)**

In a room of 23 people, what is the probability that at least two share the same birthday?

Use combinatorial thinking. Consider:
- Total ways to assign 23 people to 365 days: $365^{23}$ (each person can be born on any day).
- Ways to assign 23 people to different days: $365 \times 364 \times 363 \times \cdots \times 343$ (first person, 365 choices; second, 364; etc.).

The probability all have different birthdays is the ratio of these. The complement gives the probability of at least one match.

Calculate (or estimate) this probability. Does the answer align with your intuition?

## Application

**Lottery Odds**

A lottery game requires you to choose 6 different numbers from 1 to 49.

How many possible tickets are there?

If you buy 1 ticket, what is your probability of winning?

A "smaller" prize is awarded for matching 5 of 6. How many ways can you choose 5 winning numbers and 1 non-winning number?

Estimate the odds and discuss: why do people buy lottery tickets despite the tiny probability?

## Recap

Counting is the foundation of probability. When all outcomes are equally likely, probability is the ratio of favorable to total outcomes. But computing the total becomes impossible without systematic counting.

The **fundamental counting principle** says that independent choices multiply. If you have $m$ options for choice 1 and $n$ for choice 2, you have $m \times n$ total combinations.

**Factorials** count arrangements where all items are used and order matters. $n!$ is the number of ways to arrange $n$ distinct objects.

**Permutations** and **combinations** extend this. A permutation counts ordered selections ($P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$). A combination counts unordered selections ($C(n,r) = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$). The difference: does order matter?

**Pascal's triangle** encodes all binomial coefficients and appears throughout mathematics, from polynomial expansion to probability to combinatorics itself.

These tools let us answer "How many ways?" without enumeration. A poker hand doesn't require listing all 2.5 million hands. A password doesn't require listing all possible codes. The formulas provide the count instantly.

Counting, properly done, reveals the vast size of possibility space. It teaches humility: most events, when broken down into all possible cases, are rarer than intuition suggests. Combinatorics quantifies this lesson.

## Unlock Quiz

**Question 1:** How many ways can 4 distinct books be arranged on a shelf?

- $4$
- $16$
- $24$
- $12$

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$24$

This is a permutation of all 4 books: $P(4,4) = 4! = 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 24$.
</details>

**Question 2:** From 10 people, how many 3-person committees can be formed?

- $P(10,3) = 720$
- $C(10,3) = 120$
- $C(10,3) = 10$
- $P(10,3) = 100$

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$C(10,3) = 120$

Order doesn't matter in a committee, so we use combinations: $\binom{10}{3} = \frac{10!}{3! \times 7!} = \frac{10 \times 9 \times 8}{6} = 120$.
</details>

**Question 3:** What is $\binom{5}{2}$?

- $5$
- $10$
- $20$
- $15$

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$10$

$\binom{5}{2} = \frac{5!}{2! \times 3!} = \frac{5 \times 4}{2 \times 1} = \frac{20}{2} = 10$.
</details>

**Question 4:** A password uses 4 digits (0–9), each digit can repeat. How many passwords are possible?

- $10^4 = 10{,}000$
- $4! = 24$
- $P(10,4) = 5{,}040$
- $C(10,4) = 210$

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$10^4 = 10{,}000$

Each of the 4 positions has 10 choices (0–9), and they're independent. By the fundamental counting principle: $10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 = 10{,}000$.
</details>
