## Story Hook

**Francis Galton** (1886) studies a curious pattern: tall parents tend to have tall children, but the children aren't *as tall* as the parents. Short parents tend to have short children, but not *as short*. There's an association between parent height and child height, but it's not perfect. The effect "regresses" toward the average height — hence the term **regression**.

Galton quantifies this relationship and invents a tool: the **correlation coefficient**, measuring how strongly two variables move together.

Soon after, his student **Karl Pearson** (1895–1896) formalises the mathematics. The **Pearson correlation** $r$ becomes standard in science. The **regression line** — a line that "fits best" through scattered data points — becomes a tool for prediction.

Today, correlation and regression are everywhere: economists predict recessions, biologists model disease spread, machine learning systems predict from data. The story of two variables moving together, and how to find the line between them, is a story of modern prediction.

## Why It Matters

Every analyst faces questions like:
- **Economics:** Does higher education spending correlate with higher student test scores? Can we predict future economic growth?
- **Medicine:** Does smoking correlate with lung cancer? How much does exercise reduce heart disease risk?
- **Meteorology:** How does ocean temperature correlate with hurricane intensity?
- **Tech:** Do more features correlate with higher user engagement? Can we predict churn?
- **Sports:** Does team payroll correlate with wins? Can we predict next season's performance?

Correlation and regression let you:
- Visualise relationships between two variables (scatter plots)
- Quantify strength of association (correlation coefficient $r$)
- Fit a predictive model (regression line $\hat{y} = a + bx$)
- Assess goodness of fit (coefficient of determination $r^2$)
- **Critically:** distinguish correlation from causation (the most common statistical mistake)

## Historical Context

**Francis Galton** (1822–1911) was a polymath — statistician, explorer, geographer. His work on heredity led him to study parent-child heights. He noticed regression to the mean and invented regression analysis.

**Karl Pearson** (1857–1936) was Galton's intellectual successor. He formalised the mathematics of correlation and regression. He also invented goodness-of-fit testing and developed the chi-squared test.

Their work was motivated by a dangerous ideology: **eugenics** (the false belief that inherited traits could be "improved" through selective breeding). While we rightfully reject eugenics today, Pearson and Galton's *statistical methods* — correlation and regression — are fundamental tools of modern science.

**Least-squares regression** (finding the best-fit line) was developed earlier by **Carl Friedrich Gauss** (1809) and **Adrien-Marie Legendre** (1805) for astronomy. Their method minimises the sum of squared deviations (residuals) — hence "least squares."

By the early 1900s, correlation and regression had become the language of quantitative analysis across all sciences.

## Core Concept

Imagine you measure two variables on a group of people: height (X) and weight (Y). You plot each person as a point: height on the horizontal axis, weight on the vertical axis. You get a **scatter plot**.

Do the points form a pattern? Often yes:
- **Positive association:** taller people tend to weigh more
- **Negative association:** students who study more tend to score higher (wait, that's positive — less studying time leaves time to play video games)
- **No association:** eye colour and height — no pattern

The **correlation coefficient** $r$ quantifies this pattern. It ranges from $-1$ to $1$:
- $r = 1$: perfect positive association (all points on a line with positive slope)
- $r = -1$: perfect negative association (all points on a line with negative slope)
- $r = 0$: no linear association (points scattered randomly)
- $0 < r < 1$: positive association, strength depends on magnitude
- $-1 < r < 0$: negative association, strength depends on magnitude

Once you've identified a strong association, the next question is: *can I predict $Y$ from $X$?* The **regression line** $\hat{y} = a + bx$ gives the best linear prediction.

### Association vs. Causation

**Critical distinction:**

**Correlation** ($r$) measures association: do two variables move together?

**Causation** means: does one *cause* the other?

Correlation *does not* imply causation. Classic examples:
- **Ice cream sales and drowning deaths:** both increase in summer (confounding variable: temperature)
- **Shoe size and reading ability in children:** both increase with age (age is the confounder)
- **Coffee consumption and heart disease:** *possibly* confounded by stress, weight, smoking, or reverse causation

Confounding variables (hidden factors) can create spurious correlations. Bad actors use this: "X and Y correlate, therefore X causes Y" — a common propaganda technique.

## Definitions and Formal Lesson

### Scatter Plot and Association

A **scatter plot** displays two quantitative variables:
- Horizontal axis: independent variable (X)
- Vertical axis: dependent variable (Y)
- Each point: one observation

**Describing association:**
- **Direction:** positive (upward trend) or negative (downward trend)
- **Form:** linear (points cluster around a line) or non-linear (curved, etc.)
- **Strength:** strong (tight cluster), moderate, or weak (scattered)
- **Outliers:** unusual points far from the main pattern

### Correlation Coefficient

The **Pearson correlation coefficient** is:

$$r = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(\frac{x_i - \bar{x}}{s_x}\right)\left(\frac{y_i - \bar{y}}{s_y}\right)$$

where $s_x$ and $s_y$ are standard deviations.

**Key properties:**
- $-1 \leq r \leq 1$
- $r > 0$: positive association
- $r < 0$: negative association
- $|r|$ close to 1: strong linear association
- $|r|$ close to 0: weak linear association
- $r = 0$: no linear association (but could have non-linear association!)

**Interpretation:**
- $r = 0.9$: very strong positive association
- $r = 0.5$: moderate positive association
- $r = 0.2$: weak positive association
- $r = 0$: no linear association
- $r = -0.7$: strong negative association

**Do not memorise the formula.** Use a calculator or software. Understand the interpretation.

### Least-Squares Regression Line

The **regression line** $\hat{y} = a + bx$ predicts $Y$ from $X$.

The **slope** is:

$$b = r \frac{s_y}{s_x}$$

The **intercept** is:

$$a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x}$$

The line passes through the point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ (the centre of mass of the scatter plot).

**Interpretation:**
- **Slope $b$:** for each unit increase in $X$, $Y$ increases (or decreases if $b < 0$) by $b$ units
- **Intercept $a$:** predicted value of $Y$ when $X = 0$ (might not be meaningful if $X$ doesn't approach 0)

**Example:** Heights and weights. Regression line: $\widehat{\text{Weight}} = 50 + 0.8 \times \text{Height}$ (in crude units).
- Slope 0.8 means: each cm of additional height predicts 0.8 kg more weight
- Intercept 50 means: a person 0 cm tall would weigh 50 kg (not meaningful — no one is 0 cm tall)

### Residuals

A **residual** is the difference between observed and predicted:

$$e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i$$

Residuals measure how far each point is from the regression line. Small residuals = good fit. Large residuals = poor fit or outliers.

**Least-squares principle:** the regression line minimises the sum of squared residuals $\sum e_i^2$. This makes it the "best" line in a specific mathematical sense.

### Coefficient of Determination ($r^2$)

$$r^2 = \text{(correlation coefficient)}^2$$

$r^2$ is the **proportion of variance in $Y$ explained by $X$.**

- $r^2 = 0.81$ means 81% of the variation in $Y$ is explained by $X$; 19% is unexplained (due to other factors, measurement error, randomness)
- $r^2 = 0.25$ means only 25% is explained; the model leaves 75% unexplained
- $r^2 = 0$ means $X$ explains none of the variation in $Y$
- $r^2 = 1$ means $X$ perfectly explains $Y$ (all points exactly on the line)

$r^2$ is more useful than $r$ because it directly quantifies explanatory power.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1: Interpreting Correlation**

Data: study hours (X) and exam scores (Y) for 20 students.
- Scatter plot shows a clear upward trend
- Correlation: $r = 0.82$
- $r^2 = 0.67$

**Interpretation:** There's a strong positive association between study hours and exam scores ($r = 0.82$). Students who study more tend to score higher. The relationship explains 67% of the variation in exam scores ($r^2 = 0.67$). The remaining 33% is due to other factors (aptitude, sleep, nutrition, test anxiety, etc.).

**Example 2: Regression Line and Prediction**

Data: advertising spending (X, in thousands of dollars) and revenue (Y, in thousands of dollars) for 12 months.

Regression line: $\hat{Y} = 50 + 8X$ with $r = 0.89$, $r^2 = 0.79$.

**Predictions:**
- If we spend $10,000 on ads: $\hat{Y} = 50 + 8(10) = 130$ thousand dollars revenue = $130,000
- If we spend $20,000: $\hat{Y} = 50 + 8(20) = 210$ thousand dollars = $210,000

**Interpretation:**
- Each additional $1,000 in advertising is predicted to increase revenue by $8,000 (slope = 8)
- Base revenue (with no advertising) is estimated at $50,000 (intercept = 50)
- The model explains 79% of revenue variation ($r^2 = 0.79$)
- We're reasonably confident in predictions *within the range of the data* (interpolation). Predicting outside (extrapolation) is risky

**Example 3: Causation vs. Correlation**

**Claim:** Ice cream sales and drowning deaths correlate ($r = 0.85$). Ice cream causes drowning!

**Reality:** Both increase in summer (confounding variable: temperature). Heat drives ice cream sales and increases swimming (and drowning risk). Temperature causes both.

**Correct conclusion:** There's a strong positive association, but temperature confounds the relationship. Ice cream doesn't cause drowning.

## Reasoning Task

**Simpson's Paradox and Confounding**

A company finds that men, on average, receive higher salaries than women. The scatter plot shows a weak positive correlation between years of experience and salary ($r = 0.3$).

But when they break down the data by *department*:
- Engineering: men earn slightly less than women (confounding by experience)
- Marketing: men earn slightly less than women
- Sales: men earn slightly less than women

How can men earn *more* overall but less in every department? The answer: **Simpson's Paradox**.

**Your Task:**

1. Propose a confounding variable that explains this paradox
2. Draw two scatter plots: one combined (showing men earning more), one separated by department (showing women earning more)
3. Explain how aggregation can reverse the direction of association

This is why correlation analysis must account for confounding variables — a foundational principle of causal inference.

## Application

**Real-World Scenario: Climate Science**

Researchers study the relationship between atmospheric CO₂ concentration (X, ppm) and global temperature anomaly (Y, °C relative to 1900s baseline).

Data (simplified):
- 1960: X = 317 ppm, Y = 0.03°C
- 1980: X = 339 ppm, Y = 0.26°C
- 2000: X = 369 ppm, Y = 0.43°C
- 2020: X = 414 ppm, Y = 1.25°C

Analysis:
- Scatter plot shows a clear upward trend
- Correlation: $r = 0.99$, $r^2 = 0.98$
- Regression line: $\hat{Y} = -3.89 + 0.011X$

**Questions:**

1. Describe the association between CO₂ and temperature
2. Interpret the slope: what does 0.011 mean in plain language?
3. Interpret $r^2 = 0.98$: what does this say about the relationship?
4. Predict the temperature anomaly if CO₂ reaches 500 ppm
5. Does correlation prove causation here? What evidence would support causation (greenhouse gas physics, isotope analysis, timing, etc.)?

This is a real example of how correlation and regression support causal inference in climate science.

## Recap

**Correlation and regression quantify relationships between two variables.**

**Correlation coefficient $r$:**
- Measures strength and direction of *linear* association
- Ranges from $-1$ to $1$
- $r = 0$ does *not* mean no association (could be non-linear!)

**Causation vs. Correlation:**
- Correlation means two variables move together
- Causation means one causes the other
- **Correlation does not imply causation** — confounding variables are common

**Regression line $\hat{y} = a + bx$:**
- Best linear prediction of $Y$ from $X$
- Slope $b$: predicted change in $Y$ per unit change in $X$
- Passes through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$
- Use for prediction *within the range of observed data*

**Coefficient of Determination $r^2$:**
- Proportion of $Y$ variation explained by $X$
- More interpretable than $r$
- Remaining $(1 - r^2)$ is unexplained

**Historical Significance:**
- Developed 1880s–1900s by Galton, Pearson, and others
- Foundation of quantitative science and modern prediction
- Misuse of correlation (forgetting the causation problem) is common in media and politics

The story of Galton and Pearson — finding patterns in inheritance data and inventing tools to quantify them — shows how correlation and regression reveal hidden structures in data. Master these tools, and you unlock the ability to predict, compare, and infer.

## Unlock Quiz

**Question 1:** Two variables have correlation $r = 0.5$. What does this indicate?

- A perfect positive association
- A moderate positive association
- A weak negative association
- No association

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$r = 0.5$ indicates a moderate positive association. The variables move together somewhat, but not strongly. The answer is "A moderate positive association".
</details>

**Question 2:** If $r = 0.7$, what is $r^2$?

- 0.49
- 0.7
- 1.4
- Cannot be determined

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$r^2 = (0.7)^2 = 0.49$. This means 49% of the variation in one variable is explained by the other. The answer is 0.49.
</details>

**Question 3:** A regression line is $\hat{y} = 10 + 2x$. What is the predicted $y$ when $x = 5$?

- 10
- 15
- 20
- 25

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
$\hat{y} = 10 + 2(5) = 10 + 10 = 20$. The answer is 20.
</details>

**Question 4:** Which scenario demonstrates causation rather than just correlation?

- Taller people play basketball more; therefore height causes basketball ability
- In a randomised controlled trial, giving a drug reduces symptoms; therefore the drug causes symptom reduction
- Students with more tutoring score higher; tutoring might correlate with prior ability
- Countries with more television ownership have higher life expectancy; TV causes longer life

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
Only the second option demonstrates causation. A randomised controlled trial (RCT) controls for confounding variables and isolates the effect of the drug. The other options show correlation that could be due to confounding. The answer is "In a randomised controlled trial, giving a drug reduces symptoms; therefore the drug causes symptom reduction".
</details>

**Question 5:** A scatter plot shows points scattered randomly with no pattern. What would you expect?

- $r \approx 1$
- $r \approx -1$
- $r \approx 0$
- $r^2 \approx 1$

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>
No pattern means no linear association, so $r \approx 0$. The answer is "$r \approx 0$".
</details>
